# Vector Analysis and Calculus

## Vector Basics

A vector has both magnitude and direction. Examples of vectors are displacement, velocity, force, and acceleration. 

A scalar has only magnitude. Examples of scalars are mass, length, time, and temperature.

In three-dimensional Euclidean space, a vector is written as:

$$
\vec{A} = A\_1\hat{i} + A\_2\hat{j} + A\_3\hat{k}
$$

Here, $\hat{i}$, $\hat{j}$, and $\hat{k}$ are unit vectors along the $x$, $y$, and $z$ axes.

### Proof: Magnitude of a Vector

We want to prove that the magnitude $A$ of vector $\vec{A}$ is:

$$
A = \sqrt{A\_1^2 + A\_2^2 + A\_3^2}
$$

Let us represent the vector in a coordinate system. Let $P(A\_1, A\_2, A\_3)$ be a point. The position vector is $\vec{OP}$.
Let $Q$ be the projection of $P$ on the $xy$-plane. Let $R$ be the projection of $Q$ on the $x$-axis.
By Pythagoras' theorem in the right-angled triangle $OPQ$:

$$
(\overline{OP})^2 = (\overline{OQ})^2 + (\overline{QP})^2
$$

Similarly, in the right-angled triangle $OQR$:

$$
(\overline{OQ})^2 = (\overline{OR})^2 + (\overline{RQ})^2
$$

Substituting this into the first equation:

$$
(\overline{OP})^2 = (\overline{OR})^2 + (\overline{RQ})^2 + (\overline{QP})^2
$$

Here:
- $\overline{OP}$ is the magnitude $A$.
- $\overline{OR}$ is $A\_1$.
- $\overline{RQ}$ is $A\_2$.
- $\overline{QP}$ is $A\_3$.

So we have:

$$
A^2 = A\_1^2 + A\_2^2 + A\_3^2 \Rightarrow A = \sqrt{A\_1^2 + A\_2^2 + A\_3^2}
$$

This completes the proof.

### Vector Products

1. **Dot Product**: 

$$
\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = AB\cos\theta
$$

If $\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = 0$ and the vectors are non-zero, they are perpendicular. 

2. **Cross Product**: 

$$
\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = AB\sin\theta\hat{n}
$$

Here, $\hat{n}$ is a unit vector perpendicular to both $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$. If $\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = 0$ and the vectors are non-zero, they are parallel.

3. **Scalar Triple Product**: 

$$
\vec{A} \cdot (\vec{B} \times \vec{C}) = \begin{vmatrix} A\_1 & A\_2 & A\_3 \\\\ B\_1 & B\_2 & B\_3 \\\\ C\_1 & C\_2 & C\_3 \end{vmatrix}
$$

### Vector Operations Example

Let us find scalars $a$, $b$, and $c$ such that:

$$
\vec{r\_4} = a\vec{r\_1} + b\vec{r\_2} + c\vec{r\_3}
$$

where:
- $\vec{r\_1} = 2\hat{i} - \hat{j} + \hat{k}$
- $\vec{r\_2} = \hat{i} + 3\hat{j} - 2\hat{k}$
- $\vec{r\_3} = -2\hat{i} + \hat{j} - 3\hat{k}$
- $\vec{r\_4} = 3\hat{i} + 2\hat{j} + 5\hat{k}$

We substitute these vectors into the equation:

$$
3\hat{i} + 2\hat{j} + 5\hat{k} = a(2\hat{i} - \hat{j} + \hat{k}) + b(\hat{i} + 3\hat{j} - 2\hat{k}) + c(-2\hat{i} + \hat{j} - 3\hat{k})
$$

Equating components:

$$
\begin{aligned}
2a + b - 2c &= 3 \\\\
-a + 3b + c &= 2 \\\\
a - 2b - 3c &= 5
\end{aligned}
$$

Solving this system gives:

$$
a = -2, \quad b = 1, \quad c = -3
$$

### Angle Between Vectors Example

Let us find the angle between:
- $\vec{A} = 2\hat{i} + 2\hat{j} - \hat{k}$
- $\vec{B} = 6\hat{i} - 3\hat{j} + 2\hat{k}$

First, we compute the magnitudes:

$$
A = \sqrt{2^2 + 2^2 + (-1)^2} = 3, \quad B = \sqrt{6^2 + (-3)^2 + 2^2} = 7
$$

Now compute the dot product:

$$
\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = 2(6) + 2(-3) + (-1)(2) = 12 - 6 - 2 = 4
$$

Using the formula:

$$
\cos\theta = \frac{\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}}{AB} = \frac{4}{21} \Rightarrow \theta = \cos^{-1}\left( \frac{4}{21} \right) \approx 79.01^\circ
$$

### Direction Angles of a Vector

Let $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $\gamma$ be the angles that vector $\vec{A} = 3\hat{i} - 6\hat{j} + 2\hat{k}$ makes with the $x$, $y$, and $z$ axes.
The magnitude is:

$$
A = \sqrt{3^2 + (-6)^2 + 2^2} = 7
$$

The angles are:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\alpha &= \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{3}{7}\right) \approx 64.6^\circ \\\\
\beta &= \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{-6}{7}\right) \approx 149.0^\circ \\\\
\gamma &= \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{2}{7}\right) \approx 73.4^\circ
\end{aligned}
$$

### Vector Projection

Let us find the projection of $\vec{A} = \hat{i} - 2\hat{j} + \hat{k}$ on $\vec{B} = 4\hat{i} - 4\hat{j} + 7\hat{k}$.
The projection is:

$$
\frac{\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}}{|\vec{B}|} = \frac{1(4) + (-2)(-4) + 1(7)}{\sqrt{4^2 + (-4)^2 + 7^2}} = \frac{4 + 8 + 7}{9} = \frac{19}{9}
$$

### Perpendicular Unit Vector

Let us find a unit vector perpendicular to the plane containing:
- $\vec{A} = 2\hat{i} - 6\hat{j} - 3\hat{k}$
- $\vec{B} = 4\hat{i} + 3\hat{j} - \hat{k}$

We calculate the cross product:

$$
\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\\\ 2 & -6 & -3 \\\\ 4 & 3 & -1 \end{vmatrix} = 15\hat{i} - 10\hat{j} + 30\hat{k}
$$

The magnitude is:

$$
|\vec{A} \times \vec{B}| = \sqrt{15^2 + (-10)^2 + 30^2} = 35
$$

So the unit normal vector is:

$$
\hat{u} = \pm \frac{15\hat{i} - 10\hat{j} + 30\hat{k}}{35} = \pm \left( \frac{3}{7}\hat{i} - \frac{2}{7}\hat{j} + \frac{6}{7}\hat{k} \right)
$$

## Vector Differentiation

Let $\vec{R}(u)$ be a vector function of a scalar $u$. The derivative is:

$$
\frac{d\vec{R}}{du} = \lim\_{\Delta u \to 0} \frac{\vec{R}(u + \Delta u) - \vec{R}(u)}{\Delta u}
$$

### Proof: Component-wise Differentiation

We want to prove:

$$
\frac{d\vec{R}}{du} = \frac{dx}{du}\hat{i} + \frac{dy}{du}\hat{j} + \frac{dz}{du}\hat{k}
$$

where $\vec{R}(u) = x(u)\hat{i} + y(u)\hat{j} + z(u)\hat{k}$.

Using the definition of derivative:

$$
\frac{d\vec{R}}{du} = \lim\_{\Delta u \to 0} \frac{[x(u+\Delta u)\hat{i} + y(u+\Delta u)\hat{j} + z(u+\Delta u)\hat{k}] - [x(u)\hat{i} + y(u)\hat{j} + z(u)\hat{k}]}{\Delta u}
$$

We group the components:

$$
\frac{d\vec{R}}{du} = \lim\_{\Delta u \to 0} \left[ \frac{x(u+\Delta u) - x(u)}{\Delta u}\hat{i} + \frac{y(u+\Delta u) - y(u)}{\Delta u}\hat{j} + \frac{z(u+\Delta u) - z(u)}{\Delta u}\hat{k} \right]
$$

Applying the limit to each term gives:

$$
\frac{d\vec{R}}{du} = \frac{dx}{du}\hat{i} + \frac{dy}{du}\hat{j} + \frac{dz}{du}\hat{k}
$$

This completes the proof.

### Velocity and Acceleration in a Direction

A particle moves along the curve:

$$
x = 2t^2, \quad y = t^2-4t, \quad z = 3t-5
$$

Let us find the components of its velocity and acceleration at $t = 1$ in the direction of:

$$
\vec{D} = \hat{i} - 3\hat{j} + 2\hat{k}
$$

The position vector is:

$$
\vec{r} = 2t^2\hat{i} + (t^2-4t)\hat{j} + (3t-5)\hat{k}
$$

The velocity vector is:

$$
\vec{v} = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = 4t\hat{i} + (2t-4)\hat{j} + 3\hat{k}
$$

The acceleration vector is:

$$
\vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = 4\hat{i} + 2\hat{j}
$$

At $t = 1$:

$$
\vec{v} = 4\hat{i} - 2\hat{j} + 3\hat{k}, \quad \vec{a} = 4\hat{i} + 2\hat{j}
$$

The unit vector in direction $\vec{D}$ is:

$$
\hat{u} = \frac{\hat{i}-3\hat{j}+2\hat{k}}{\sqrt{1^2 + (-3)^2 + 2^2}} = \frac{\hat{i}-3\hat{j}+2\hat{k}}{\sqrt{14}}
$$

The component of velocity is:

$$
\vec{v} \cdot \hat{u} = \frac{4(1) + (-2)(-3) + 3(2)}{\sqrt{14}} = \frac{16}{\sqrt{14}}
$$

The component of acceleration is:

$$
\vec{a} \cdot \hat{u} = \frac{4(1) + 2(-3)}{\sqrt{14}} = -\frac{2}{\sqrt{14}}
$$

### Vector Magnitude Differentiation Proofs

1. **Proof**: If vector $\vec{A}$ has a constant magnitude, then $\vec{A}$ and $\frac{d\vec{A}}{dt}$ are perpendicular.
Since the magnitude is constant:

$$
\vec{A} \cdot \vec{A} = \text{constant}
$$

Differentiating both sides:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{A}) = 0 \Rightarrow \vec{A} \cdot \frac{d\vec{A}}{dt} + \frac{d\vec{A}}{dt} \cdot \vec{A} = 0 \Rightarrow 2 \vec{A} \cdot \frac{d\vec{A}}{dt} = 0
$$

So we have:

$$
\vec{A} \cdot \frac{d\vec{A}}{dt} = 0
$$

Thus, the vectors are perpendicular.

2. **Proof**: Show $\vec{A} \cdot \frac{d\vec{A}}{dt} = A \frac{dA}{dt}$.
Let $\vec{A} = A\_1\hat{i} + A\_2\hat{j} + A\_3\hat{k}$ and $A = \sqrt{A\_1^2 + A\_2^2 + A\_3^2}$.
Differentiating $A^2 = \vec{A} \cdot \vec{A}$:

$$
2A\frac{dA}{dt} = 2\vec{A} \cdot \frac{d\vec{A}}{dt} \Rightarrow A\frac{dA}{dt} = \vec{A} \cdot \frac{d\vec{A}}{dt}
$$

This completes the proof.

## Frenet-Serret Formulas

The Frenet-Serret formulas describe the kinematic properties of a particle moving along a continuous space curve.

Let $\vec{r}(s)$ be a curve parameterized by arc length $s$. We define:
- Unit Tangent vector: $\vec{T} = \frac{d\vec{r}}{ds}$
- Principal Normal vector: $\vec{N}$
- Binormal vector: $\vec{B} = \vec{T} \times \vec{N}$

The Frenet-Serret formulas are:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d\vec{T}}{ds} &= k\vec{N} \\\\
\frac{d\vec{B}}{ds} &= -\tau\vec{N} \\\\
\frac{d\vec{N}}{ds} &= \tau\vec{B} - k\vec{T}
\end{aligned}
$$

Here:
- $k$ is the curvature.
- $\tau$ is the torsion.
- $\rho = 1/k$ is the radius of curvature.
- $\sigma = 1/\tau$ is the radius of torsion.

### Circular Helix Example

A particle moves along the circular helix:

$$
x = 3\cos t, \quad y = 3\sin t, \quad z = 4t
$$

Let us find its Frenet-Serret vectors, curvature, and torsion.
The position vector is:

$$
\vec{r} = 3\cos t \hat{i} + 3\sin t \hat{j} + 4t\hat{k}
$$

Differentiating:

$$
\frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = -3\sin t \hat{i} + 3\cos t \hat{j} + 4\hat{k}
$$

The magnitude is:

$$
\frac{ds}{dt} = \left|\frac{d\vec{r}}{dt}\right| = \sqrt{(-3\sin t)^2 + (3\cos t)^2 + 4^2} = 5
$$

The unit tangent vector is:

$$
\vec{T} = \frac{d\vec{r}/dt}{ds/dt} = -\frac{3}{5}\sin t \hat{i} + \frac{3}{5}\cos t \hat{j} + \frac{4}{5}\hat{k}
$$

Now differentiate $\vec{T}$ with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d\vec{T}}{dt} = -\frac{3}{5}\cos t \hat{i} - \frac{3}{5}\sin t \hat{j}
$$

Using the chain rule:

$$
\frac{d\vec{T}}{ds} = \frac{d\vec{T}/dt}{ds/dt} = -\frac{3}{25}\cos t \hat{i} - \frac{3}{25}\sin t \hat{j}
$$

Since $\frac{d\vec{T}}{ds} = k\vec{N}$, the curvature is:

$$
k = \left|\frac{d\vec{T}}{ds}\right| = \frac{3}{25} \Rightarrow \rho = \frac{25}{3}
$$

The principal normal vector is:

$$
\vec{N} = \frac{1}{k}\frac{d\vec{T}}{ds} = -\cos t \hat{i} - \sin t \hat{j}
$$

The binormal vector is:

$$
\vec{B} = \vec{T} \times \vec{N} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\\\ -\frac{3}{5}\sin t & \frac{3}{5}\cos t & \frac{4}{5} \\\\ -\cos t & -\sin t & 0 \end{vmatrix} = \frac{4}{5}\sin t \hat{i} - \frac{4}{5}\cos t \hat{j} + \frac{3}{5}\hat{k}
$$

Differentiate $\vec{B}$:

$$
\frac{d\vec{B}}{dt} = \frac{4}{5}\cos t \hat{i} + \frac{4}{5}\sin t \hat{j} \Rightarrow \frac{d\vec{B}}{ds} = \frac{4}{25}\cos t \hat{i} + \frac{4}{25}\sin t \hat{j}
$$

Since $\frac{d\vec{B}}{ds} = -\tau\vec{N}$, we have:

$$
-\tau(-\cos t \hat{i} - \sin t \hat{j}) = \frac{4}{25}\cos t \hat{i} + \frac{4}{25}\sin t \hat{j} \Rightarrow \tau = \frac{4}{25} \Rightarrow \sigma = \frac{25}{4}
$$

### Curvature and Torsion of a Space Curve

Let us find the curvature $k$ and torsion $\tau$ of the space curve:

$$
x = t, \quad y = t^2, \quad z = \frac{2}{3}t^3
$$

The position vector is:

$$
\vec{r} = t\hat{i} + t^2\hat{j} + \frac{2}{3}t^3\hat{k}
$$

Differentiating:

$$
\frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = \hat{i} + 2t\hat{j} + 2t^2\hat{k} \Rightarrow \frac{ds}{dt} = \sqrt{1^2 + (2t)^2 + (2t^2)^2} = 1 + 2t^2
$$

The unit tangent vector is:

$$
\vec{T} = \frac{\hat{i} + 2t\hat{j} + 2t^2\hat{k}}{1+2t^2}
$$

Differentiating $\vec{T}$ with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d\vec{T}}{dt} = \frac{-4t\hat{i} + (2-4t^2)\hat{j} + 4t\hat{k}}{(1+2t^2)^2}
$$

Using the chain rule:

$$
\frac{d\vec{T}}{ds} = \frac{-4t\hat{i} + (2-4t^2)\hat{j} + 4t\hat{k}}{(1+2t^2)^3}
$$

The curvature is:

$$
k = \left|\frac{d\vec{T}}{ds}\right| = \frac{2}{(1+2t^2)^2}
$$

So the principal normal vector is:

$$
\vec{N} = \frac{1}{k}\frac{d\vec{T}}{ds} = \frac{-2t\hat{i} + (1-2t^2)\hat{j} + 2t\hat{k}}{1+2t^2}
$$

The binormal vector is:

$$
\vec{B} = \vec{T} \times \vec{N} = \frac{2t^2\hat{i} - 2t\hat{j} + \hat{k}}{1+2t^2}
$$

Differentiating $\vec{B}$:

$$
\frac{d\vec{B}}{dt} = \frac{4t\hat{i} + (4t^2-2)\hat{j} - 4t\hat{k}}{(1+2t^2)^2} \Rightarrow \frac{d\vec{B}}{ds} = \frac{4t\hat{i} + (4t^2-2)\hat{j} - 4t\hat{k}}{(1+2t^2)^3}
$$

Since $\frac{d\vec{B}}{ds} = -\tau\vec{N}$, we find:

$$
\tau = \frac{2}{(1+2t^2)^2}
$$

Here, the curvature and torsion are equal.

## Vector Differential Operators

The vector differential operator is:

$$
\vec{\nabla} = \hat{i}\frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \hat{j}\frac{\partial}{\partial y} + \hat{k}\frac{\partial}{\partial z}
$$

### Gradient, Divergence, and Curl Example

Let $\vec{A} = xz^3 \hat{i} - 2x^2 yz \hat{j} + 2yz^4 \hat{k}$.
Let us compute $\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{A}$ at $(1, -1, 1)$, and verify $\vec{\nabla} \cdot (\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{A}) = 0$.

First, compute the curl:

$$
\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{A} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\\\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\\\ xz^3 & -2x^2 yz & 2yz^4 \end{vmatrix} = (2x^2y + 2z^4)\hat{i} + 3xz^2\hat{j} - 4xyz\hat{k}
$$

At $(1, -1, 1)$, we substitute the coordinates:

$$
\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{A} = 3\hat{j} + 4\hat{k}
$$

Now compute the divergence of the curl:

$$
\vec{\nabla} \cdot (\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{A}) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(2x^2y + 2z^4) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3xz^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(-4xyz) = 4xy + 0 - 4xy = 0
$$

The identity is verified.

### Vector Operator Identity Proof

We want to prove:

$$
\vec{\nabla} \cdot (\vec{A} \times \vec{r}) = 0
$$

provided that $\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{A} = 0$. Here, $\vec{r} = x\hat{i} + y\hat{j} + z\hat{k}$.

Let $\vec{A} = A\_1\hat{i} + A\_2\hat{j} + A\_3\hat{k}$.
The cross product is:

$$
\vec{A} \times \vec{r} = \hat{i}(zA\_2 - yA\_3) + \hat{j}(xA\_3 - zA\_1) + \hat{k}(yA\_1 - xA\_2)
$$

Now compute the divergence:

$$
\vec{\nabla} \cdot (\vec{A} \times \vec{r}) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(zA\_2 - yA\_3) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(xA\_3 - zA\_1) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(yA\_1 - xA\_2)
$$

Expanding using the product rule:

$$
\vec{\nabla} \cdot (\vec{A} \times \vec{r}) = z\left( \frac{\partial A\_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial A\_1}{\partial y} \right) + x\left( \frac{\partial A\_3}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial A\_2}{\partial z} \right) + y\left( \frac{\partial A\_1}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial A\_3}{\partial x} \right)
$$

We can write this as:

$$
\vec{\nabla} \cdot (\vec{A} \times \vec{r}) = (x\hat{i} + y\hat{j} + z\hat{k}) \cdot (\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{A}) = \vec{r} \cdot (\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{A})
$$

Since $\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{A} = 0$, we have:

$$
\vec{\nabla} \cdot (\vec{A} \times \vec{r}) = 0
$$

This completes the proof.

## Vector Integration

### Surface Integral Example

Let us evaluate $\iint\_S \vec{A} \cdot \hat{n} \, dS$, where:
- $\vec{A} = 18z\hat{i} - 12\hat{j} + 3y\hat{k}$
- $S$ is that part of the plane $2x + 3y + 6z = 12$ located in the first octant.

First, we find the normal vector to the plane. Let $\phi = 2x + 3y + 6z - 12$:

$$
\vec{\nabla}\phi = 2\hat{i} + 3\hat{j} + 6\hat{k}
$$

The unit normal vector is:

$$
\hat{n} = \frac{2\hat{i} + 3\hat{j} + 6\hat{k}}{\sqrt{2^2 + 3^2 + 6^2}} = \frac{2}{7}\hat{i} + \frac{3}{7}\hat{j} + \frac{6}{7}\hat{k}
$$

Now compute the dot product:

$$
\vec{A} \cdot \hat{n} = \frac{36z - 36 + 18y}{7}
$$

Since $2x + 3y + 6z = 12$, we substitute $36z = 6(12 - 2x - 3y) = 72 - 12x - 18y$:

$$
\vec{A} \cdot \hat{n} = \frac{72 - 12x - 18y - 36 + 18y}{7} = \frac{36 - 12x}{7}
$$

Next, we project $S$ onto the $xy$-plane. The region $R$ is bounded by $x=0$, $y=0$, and $2x + 3y = 12$, which gives:

$$
y = \frac{12-2x}{3}
$$

The surface element is:

$$
dS = \frac{dx \, dy}{|\hat{n} \cdot \hat{k}|}
$$

Since $\hat{n} \cdot \hat{k} = 6/7$, we have $dS = \frac{7}{6} dx \, dy$.
So the integral becomes:

$$
\iint\_S \vec{A} \cdot \hat{n} \, dS = \iint\_R \left( \frac{36 - 12x}{7} \right) \left( \frac{7}{6} dx \, dy \right) = \iint\_R (6 - 2x) dx \, dy
$$

We integrate over $R$:

$$
\int\_{x=0}^6 \int\_{y=0}^{(12-2x)/3} (6 - 2x) dy \, dx = \int\_{x=0}^6 (6 - 2x) \left( \frac{12-2x}{3} \right) dx = \frac{4}{3} \int\_{x=0}^6 (18 - 9x + x^2) dx
$$

Evaluating the integral:

$$
\frac{4}{3} \left[ 18x - \frac{9x^2}{2} + \frac{x^3}{3} \right]\_0^6 = \frac{4}{3} [108 - 162 + 72] = \frac{4}{3} (18) = 24
$$

## Integral Theorems

### Gauss Divergence Theorem

The divergence theorem states:

$$
\iint\_S \vec{A} \cdot \hat{n} \, dS = \iiint\_V \vec{\nabla} \cdot \vec{A} \, dV
$$

Here:
- $S$ is a closed surface.
- $V$ is the volume enclosed by $S$.
- $\hat{n}$ is the outward unit normal.

### Stokes' Theorem

Stokes' theorem states:

$$
\oint\_C \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{r} = \iint\_S (\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{A}) \cdot \hat{n} \, dS
$$

Here:
- $C$ is a simple closed curve.
- $S$ is a surface bounded by $C$.

### Green's Theorem in the Plane

Green's theorem states:

$$
\oint\_C (M \, dx + N \, dy) = \iint\_R \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx \, dy
$$

Here:
- $C$ is a simple closed curve.
- $R$ is the plane region enclosed by $C$.
- A simple closed curve does not intersect itself. A circle is a simple closed curve. A figure eight is self-intersecting, so it is not a simple closed curve.
