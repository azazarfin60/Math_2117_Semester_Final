## Type-03: The Divergence Theorem: Proofs and Generalizations

### Solved Problem 14

#### Question

(a) Express the divergence theorem in words and (b) write it in rectangular form.

#### Solution

(a) The surface integral of the normal component of a vector $\mathbf{A}$ taken over a closed surface is equal to the integral of the divergence of $\mathbf{A}$ taken over the volume enclosed by the surface.
(b) Let

$$
\mathbf{A} = A_1\mathbf{i} + A_2\mathbf{j} + A_3\mathbf{k}.
$$

The divergence is

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = \frac{\partial A_1}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial A_2}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial A_3}{\partial z}
$$

Let the unit outward-drawn normal to $S$ be

$$
\mathbf{n} = \cos \alpha \mathbf{i} + \cos \beta \mathbf{j} + \cos \gamma \mathbf{k},
$$

where $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ are the direction angles of $\mathbf{n}$.
Then

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = A_1 \cos \alpha + A_2 \cos \beta + A_3 \cos \gamma
$$

The divergence theorem in rectangular form is

$$
\iiint_V \left( \frac{\partial A_1}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial A_2}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial A_3}{\partial z} \right) dx dy dz = \iint_S (A_1 \cos \alpha + A_2 \cos \beta + A_3 \cos \gamma) dS
$$

### Solved Problem 16

#### Question

Prove the divergence theorem of Gauss.

#### Solution

Let $S$ be a closed surface that can be projected onto the coordinate planes such that any line parallel to the coordinate axes cuts $S$ in at most two points.
Let the upper portion of $S$ be $S_2$ with equation $z = f_2(x,y)$ and the lower portion be $S_1$ with equation $z = f_1(x,y)$. Denote their projection on the $xy$ plane by $R$.
Compute the volume integral

$$
\iiint_V \frac{\partial A_3}{\partial z} dV = \iint_R \left[ \int_{z=f_1(x,y)}^{f_2(x,y)} \frac{\partial A_3}{\partial z} dz \right] dy dx
$$

$$
= \iint_R [ A_3(x,y,f_2(x,y)) - A_3(x,y,f_1(x,y)) ] dy dx
$$

For the upper surface element $dS_2$, the outward normal $\mathbf{n}_2$ makes an acute angle $\gamma_2$ with $\mathbf{k}$, so

$$
dy dx = \cos \gamma_2 dS_2 = \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{n}_2 dS_2
$$

For the lower surface element $dS_1$, the outward normal $\mathbf{n}_1$ makes an obtuse angle $\gamma_1$ with $\mathbf{k}$, so

$$
dy dx = - \cos \gamma_1 dS_1 = - \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{n}_1 dS_1
$$

Substitute these into the projection integrals

$$
\iint_R A_3(x,y,f_2) dy dx = \iint_{S_2} A_3 \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{n}_2 dS_2
$$

$$
-\iint_R A_3(x,y,f_1) dy dx = \iint_{S_1} A_3 \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{n}_1 dS_1
$$

Adding these yields

$$
\iiint_V \frac{\partial A_3}{\partial z} dV = \iint_{S_2} A_3 \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{n}_2 dS_2 + \iint_{S_1} A_3 \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{n}_1 dS_1 = \iint_S A_3 \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS \quad (1)
$$

Similarly, projecting $S$ onto the other coordinate planes gives

$$
\iiint_V \frac{\partial A_1}{\partial x} dV = \iint_S A_1 \mathbf{i} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS \quad (2)
$$

$$
\iiint_V \frac{\partial A_2}{\partial y} dV = \iint_S A_2 \mathbf{j} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS \quad (3)
$$

Adding (1), (2), and (3) yields the divergence theorem

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} dV = \iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

For more general surfaces, subdivide the volume into subregions satisfying the coordinate projection property. The inner boundaries cancel in pairs, establishing the theorem.

### Solved Problem 19

#### Question

If $\text{div } \mathbf{A}$ denotes the divergence of a vector field $\mathbf{A}$ at a point $P$, show that

$$
\text{div } \mathbf{A} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS}{\Delta V}
$$

where $\Delta V$ is the volume enclosed by the surface $\Delta S$ containing $P$. Define sources and sinks.

#### Solution

By the divergence theorem

$$
\iiint_{\Delta V} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} dV = \iint_{\Delta S} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

Apply the mean-value theorem for integrals

$$
\overline{\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}} \iiint_{\Delta V} dV = \overline{\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}} \Delta V = \iint_{\Delta S} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

where $\overline{\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}}$ is a value intermediate between the maximum and minimum values of $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}$ inside $\Delta V$.
Rearranging

$$
\overline{\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}} = \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS}{\Delta V}
$$

Taking the limit as $\Delta V \to 0$ such that $\Delta V$ shrinks to the point $P$

$$
\text{div } \mathbf{A} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS}{\Delta V}
$$

Interpretation:
- A point $P$ where $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} > 0$ represents a net outward flux of fluid per unit volume; $P$ is called a **source**.
- A point $P$ where $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} < 0$ represents a net inward flux of fluid per unit volume; $P$ is called a **sink**.
- If

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0
$$

throughout a region, the field is **solenoidal**.

### Solved Problem 21

#### Question

Prove Green's first and second identities:
(a)

$$
\iiint_V [ \phi \nabla^2 \psi + (\nabla \phi) \cdot (\nabla \psi) ] dV = \iint_S (\phi \nabla \psi) \cdot d\mathbf{S}
$$

(b)

$$
\iiint_V ( \phi \nabla^2 \psi - \psi \nabla^2 \phi ) dV = \iint_S ( \phi \nabla \psi - \psi \nabla \phi ) \cdot d\mathbf{S}
$$

#### Solution

(a) Let

$$
\mathbf{A} = \phi \nabla \psi
$$

in the divergence theorem

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} dV = \iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{S} \implies \iiint_V \nabla \cdot (\phi \nabla \psi) dV = \iint_S (\phi \nabla \psi) \cdot d\mathbf{S}
$$

Using the vector identity

$$
\nabla \cdot (\phi \mathbf{F}) = \phi \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} + (\nabla \phi) \cdot \mathbf{F}
$$

$$
\nabla \cdot (\phi \nabla \psi) = \phi \nabla \cdot (\nabla \psi) + (\nabla \phi) \cdot (\nabla \psi) = \phi \nabla^2 \psi + (\nabla \phi) \cdot (\nabla \psi)
$$

Substitute this back

$$
\iiint_V [ \phi \nabla^2 \psi + (\nabla \phi) \cdot (\nabla \psi) ] dV = \iint_S (\phi \nabla \psi) \cdot d\mathbf{S} \quad (1)
$$

This is Green's first identity.

(b) Interchange $\phi$ and $\psi$ in Green's first identity

$$
\iiint_V [ \psi \nabla^2 \phi + (\nabla \psi) \cdot (\nabla \phi) ] dV = \iint_S (\psi \nabla \phi) \cdot d\mathbf{S} \quad (2)
$$

Subtract equation (2) from (1)

$$
\iiint_V ( \phi \nabla^2 \psi - \psi \nabla^2 \phi ) dV = \iint_S ( \phi \nabla \psi - \psi \nabla \phi ) \cdot d\mathbf{S}
$$

This is Green's second identity.

### Solved Problem 22

#### Question

Prove

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \phi dV = \iint_S \phi \mathbf{n} dS.
$$

#### Solution

Let

$$
\mathbf{A} = \phi \mathbf{C}
$$

in the divergence theorem, where $\mathbf{C}$ is a non-zero arbitrary constant vector.

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \cdot (\phi \mathbf{C}) dV = \iint_S (\phi \mathbf{C}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

Using the identity

$$
\nabla \cdot (\phi \mathbf{C}) = \phi \nabla \cdot \mathbf{C} + (\nabla \phi) \cdot \mathbf{C}
$$

:
Since $\mathbf{C}$ is constant,

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{C} = 0,
$$

so

$$
\nabla \cdot (\phi \mathbf{C}) = (\nabla \phi) \cdot \mathbf{C} = \mathbf{C} \cdot \nabla \phi.
$$

Substitute this into the integrals

$$
\iiint_V \mathbf{C} \cdot \nabla \phi dV = \iint_S \mathbf{C} \cdot (\phi \mathbf{n}) dS
$$

Since $\mathbf{C}$ is a constant vector, pull it outside the integrals

$$
\mathbf{C} \cdot \iiint_V \nabla \phi dV = \mathbf{C} \cdot \iint_S \phi \mathbf{n} dS
$$

This equation holds for any arbitrary vector $\mathbf{C}$. Thus

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \phi dV = \iint_S \phi \mathbf{n} dS
$$

### Solved Problem 23

#### Question

Prove

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{B} dV = \iint_S \mathbf{n} \times \mathbf{B} dS.
$$

#### Solution

Let

$$
\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}
$$

in the divergence theorem, where $\mathbf{C}$ is a non-zero arbitrary constant vector.

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) dV = \iint_S (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

Using the identity

$$
\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{F} \times \mathbf{G}) = \mathbf{G} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) - \mathbf{F} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{G})
$$

$$
\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{C} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) - \mathbf{B} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{C} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{B})
$$

since

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{C} = \mathbf{0}.
$$

Also

$$
(\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) \cdot \mathbf{n} = (\mathbf{C} \times \mathbf{n}) \cdot \mathbf{B} = \mathbf{C} \cdot (\mathbf{n} \times \mathbf{B})
$$

Substitute these into the integrals

$$
\iiint_V \mathbf{C} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) dV = \iint_S \mathbf{C} \cdot (\mathbf{n} \times \mathbf{B}) dS
$$

Since $\mathbf{C}$ is constant, pull it outside the integrals

$$
\mathbf{C} \cdot \iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{B} dV = \mathbf{C} \cdot \iint_S \mathbf{n} \times \mathbf{B} dS
$$

Since this holds for any arbitrary vector $\mathbf{C}$

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{B} dV = \iint_S \mathbf{n} \times \mathbf{B} dS
$$

### Solved Problem 24

#### Question

Show that at any point $P$:
(a)

$$
\nabla \phi = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \phi \mathbf{n} dS}{\Delta V}
$$

(b)

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \mathbf{n} \times \mathbf{A} dS}{\Delta V}
$$

#### Solution

(a) Take the dot product of the identity in Solved Problem 22 with the unit vector $\mathbf{i}$ over a small volume $\Delta V$

$$
\iiint_{\Delta V} \nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{i} dV = \iint_{\Delta S} \phi \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{i} dS
$$

Apply the mean-value theorem for integrals

$$
\overline{\nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{i}} \Delta V = \iint_{\Delta S} \phi \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{i} dS \implies \overline{\nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{i}} = \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \phi \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{i} dS}{\Delta V}
$$

Taking the limit as $\Delta V \to 0$

$$
\nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{i} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \phi \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{i} dS}{\Delta V} \quad (1)
$$

Similarly, for $\mathbf{j}$ and $\mathbf{k}$

$$
\nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{j} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \phi \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j} dS}{\Delta V} \quad (2)
$$

$$
\nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{k} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \phi \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k} dS}{\Delta V} \quad (3)
$$

Multiply (1), (2), and (3) by $\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}$, and $\mathbf{k}$ respectively and add, using

$$
\nabla \phi = (\nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{i})\mathbf{i} + (\nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{j})\mathbf{j} + (\nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{k})\mathbf{k}
$$

and

$$
\mathbf{n} = (\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{i})\mathbf{i} + (\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j})\mathbf{j} + (\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k})\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\nabla \phi = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \phi \mathbf{n} dS}{\Delta V}
$$

(b) Similarly, using the identity from Solved Problem 23

$$
\iiint_{\Delta V} \nabla \times \mathbf{A} dV = \iint_{\Delta S} \mathbf{n} \times \mathbf{A} dS
$$

Taking components in directions $\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}$, and $\mathbf{k}$, applying the mean-value theorem, and combining the results yields

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \mathbf{n} \times \mathbf{A} dS}{\Delta V}
$$

### Solved Problem 25

#### Question

Establish the operator equivalence

$$
\nabla \circ \ \equiv \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{1}{\Delta V} \iint_{\Delta S} d\mathbf{S} \circ
$$

where $\circ$ indicates a dot product, cross product, or ordinary product.

#### Solution

Let the operator act on a field:
- If $\circ$ is a dot product and acts on a vector field $\mathbf{A}$

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{1}{\Delta V} \iint_{\Delta S} d\mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{A} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS}{\Delta V}
$$

which matches the divergence limit definition proved in Solved Problem 19.
- If $\circ$ is a cross product and acts on a vector field $\mathbf{A}$

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{1}{\Delta V} \iint_{\Delta S} d\mathbf{S} \times \mathbf{A} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \mathbf{n} \times \mathbf{A} dS}{\Delta V}
$$

which matches the curl limit definition proved in Solved Problem 24(b).
- If $\circ$ is ordinary multiplication and acts on a scalar field $\phi$

$$
\nabla \phi = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{1}{\Delta V} \iint_{\Delta S} \phi d\mathbf{S} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\iint_{\Delta S} \phi \mathbf{n} dS}{\Delta V}
$$

which matches the gradient limit definition proved in Solved Problem 24(a).
Thus, the equivalence is established.

### Solved Problem 28

#### Question

A fluid of density $\rho(x,y,z,t)$ moves with velocity $\mathbf{v}(x,y,z,t)$. If there are no sources or sinks, prove that

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0 \quad \text{where } \mathbf{J} = \rho \mathbf{v}
$$

#### Solution

Let $V$ be an arbitrary volume inside the fluid, bounded by surface $S$. The total mass of fluid within $V$ is

$$
M = \iiint_V \rho dV
$$

The time rate of increase of this mass is

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \iiint_V \rho dV = \iiint_V \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} dV
$$

The mass of fluid leaving $V$ per unit time through surface $S$ is

$$
\iint_S \rho \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v}) dV
$$

by the divergence theorem.
Since mass is conserved and there are no sources or sinks, the rate of increase of mass inside $V$ must equal the net inflow rate

$$
\iiint_V \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} dV = - \iiint_V \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v}) dV \implies \iiint_V \left( \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v}) + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} \right) dV = 0
$$

Since the volume $V$ is arbitrary and the integrand is continuous, the integrand must be identically zero

$$
\nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v}) + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0 \implies \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0
$$

This is the continuity equation.

### Solved Problem 29

#### Question

If the temperature at any point $(x,y,z)$ of a solid at time $t$ is $U(x,y,z,t)$ and if $\kappa, \rho$ and $c$ are respectively the thermal conductivity, density, and specific heat of the solid, assumed constant, show that

$$
\frac{\partial U}{\partial t} = k \nabla^2 U \quad \text{where } k = \kappa / \rho c
$$

#### Solution

Let $V$ be an arbitrary volume inside the solid, bounded by surface $S$.
According to Fourier's law of heat conduction, the flux of heat leaving $V$ per unit time through surface element $dS$ is

$$
d\Phi = -\kappa \nabla U \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

The net heat entering $V$ per unit time is

$$
\iint_S \kappa \nabla U \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot (\kappa \nabla U) dV
$$

by the divergence theorem.
The quantity of heat stored in $V$ is

$$
Q = \iiint_V c \rho U dV
$$

The rate of increase of heat is

$$
\frac{\partial Q}{\partial t} = \iiint_V c \rho \frac{\partial U}{\partial t} dV
$$

Equating the heat entry rate to the rate of heat storage

$$
\iiint_V c \rho \frac{\partial U}{\partial t} dV = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot (\kappa \nabla U) dV \implies \iiint_V \left( c \rho \frac{\partial U}{\partial t} - \nabla \cdot (\kappa \nabla U) \right) dV = 0
$$

Since $V$ is arbitrary and the integrand is continuous, it must be zero

$$
c \rho \frac{\partial U}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot (\kappa \nabla U)
$$

Since $\kappa, c, \rho$ are constant, we find

$$
\nabla \cdot (\kappa \nabla U) = \kappa \nabla^2 U.
$$

Thus

$$
\frac{\partial U}{\partial t} = \frac{\kappa}{c \rho} \nabla^2 U = k \nabla^2 U
$$

### Supplementary Problem 55

#### Question

If $S$ is any closed surface enclosing a volume $V$ and

$$
\mathbf{A} = ax\mathbf{i} + by\mathbf{j} + cz\mathbf{k},
$$

prove that

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = (a + b + c)V.
$$

#### Solution

Find the divergence of $\mathbf{A}$

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(ax) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(by) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(cz) = a + b + c
$$

Since $a, b, c$ are constants, the divergence is constant. Apply the divergence theorem

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} dV = \iiint_V (a+b+c) dV = (a+b+c) \iiint_V dV = (a+b+c)V
$$

### Supplementary Problem 56

#### Question

If

$$
\mathbf{H} = \text{curl } \mathbf{A},
$$

prove that

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{H} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = 0
$$

for any closed surface $S$.

#### Solution

Apply the divergence theorem

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{H} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{H} dV = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) dV
$$

Since the divergence of the curl of any vector field is identically zero

$$
\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) = 0
$$

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{H} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iiint_V (0) dV = 0
$$

### Supplementary Problem 57

#### Question

If $\mathbf{n}$ is the unit outward drawn normal to any closed surface of area $S$, show that

$$
\iiint_V \text{div } \mathbf{n} dV = S.
$$

#### Solution

Apply the divergence theorem to the vector field

$$
\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{n}
$$

$$
\iiint_V \text{div } \mathbf{n} dV = \iint_S \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

Since $\mathbf{n}$ is a unit vector,

$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{n} = |\mathbf{n}|^2 = 1.
$$

Thus

$$
\iiint_V \text{div } \mathbf{n} dV = \iint_S (1) dS = S
$$

where $S$ is the total surface area.

### Supplementary Problem 58

#### Question

Prove

$$
\iiint_V \frac{dV}{r^2} = \iint_S \frac{\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n}}{r^2} dS.
$$

#### Solution

Let

$$
\mathbf{A} = \frac{\mathbf{r}}{r^2}.
$$

Compute the divergence of $\mathbf{A}$

$$
\nabla \cdot \left(\frac{\mathbf{r}}{r^2}\right) = \frac{1}{r^2}\nabla \cdot \mathbf{r} + \mathbf{r} \cdot \nabla(r^{-2})
$$

Since

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{r} = 3
$$

and

$$
\nabla(r^{-2}) = -2r^{-3}\nabla r = -2r^{-3}\frac{\mathbf{r}}{r} = -2\frac{\mathbf{r}}{r^4}
$$

$$
\nabla \cdot \left(\frac{\mathbf{r}}{r^2}\right) = \frac{3}{r^2} + \mathbf{r} \cdot \left( -2\frac{\mathbf{r}}{r^4} \right) = \frac{3}{r^2} - 2\frac{r^2}{r^4} = \frac{3}{r^2} - \frac{2}{r^2} = \frac{1}{r^2}
$$

Apply the divergence theorem

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} dV \implies \iint_S \frac{\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n}}{r^2} dS = \iiint_V \frac{dV}{r^2}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 59

#### Question

Prove

$$
\iint_S r^5 \mathbf{n} dS = \iiint_V 5r^3 \mathbf{r} dV.
$$

#### Solution

Let $\mathbf{C}$ be an arbitrary constant vector. Apply the divergence theorem to the vector field

$$
\mathbf{A} = r^5 \mathbf{C}
$$

$$
\iint_S (r^5 \mathbf{C}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot (r^5 \mathbf{C}) dV
$$

Using the vector identity

$$
\nabla \cdot (u \mathbf{F}) = u \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} + \mathbf{F} \cdot \nabla u
$$

$$
\nabla \cdot (r^5 \mathbf{C}) = r^5 \nabla \cdot \mathbf{C} + \mathbf{C} \cdot \nabla (r^5)
$$

Since $\mathbf{C}$ is constant,

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{C} = 0.
$$

The gradient of $r^5$ is

$$
\nabla(r^5) = 5r^4 \nabla r = 5r^4 \frac{\mathbf{r}}{r} = 5r^3 \mathbf{r}.
$$

Substitute these

$$
\nabla \cdot (r^5 \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{C} \cdot (5r^3 \mathbf{r})
$$

The divergence theorem equation becomes

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{C} \cdot (r^5 \mathbf{n}) dS = \iiint_V \mathbf{C} \cdot (5r^3 \mathbf{r}) dV
$$

Pull $\mathbf{C}$ out of the integrals

$$
\mathbf{C} \cdot \iint_S r^5 \mathbf{n} dS = \mathbf{C} \cdot \iiint_V 5r^3 \mathbf{r} dV
$$

Since this holds for any arbitrary constant vector $\mathbf{C}$

$$
\iint_S r^5 \mathbf{n} dS = \iiint_V 5r^3 \mathbf{r} dV
$$

### Supplementary Problem 60

#### Question

Prove

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{n} dS = \mathbf{0}
$$

for any closed surface $S$.

#### Solution

Let $\mathbf{C}$ be an arbitrary constant vector. Apply the divergence theorem to the vector field

$$
\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{C}
$$

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{C} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{C} dV
$$

Since $\mathbf{C}$ is constant,

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{C} = 0.
$$

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{C} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = 0 \implies \mathbf{C} \cdot \iint_S \mathbf{n} dS = 0
$$

Since this holds for any arbitrary constant vector $\mathbf{C}$

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{n} dS = \mathbf{0}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 61

#### Question

Show that Green's second identity can be written

$$
\iiint_V ( \phi \nabla^2 \psi - \psi \nabla^2 \phi ) dV = \iint_S \left( \phi \frac{d\psi}{dn} - \psi \frac{d\phi}{dn} \right) dS
$$

#### Solution

Green's second identity is

$$
\iiint_V ( \phi \nabla^2 \psi - \psi \nabla^2 \phi ) dV = \iint_S ( \phi \nabla \psi - \psi \nabla \phi ) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

The directional derivative of a scalar function in the direction of the unit outward normal vector $\mathbf{n}$ is

$$
\frac{d\psi}{dn} = \nabla \psi \cdot \mathbf{n}, \quad \frac{d\phi}{dn} = \nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{n}
$$

Substitute these definitions into the surface integral

$$
\iint_S ( \phi \nabla \psi \cdot \mathbf{n} - \psi \nabla \phi \cdot \mathbf{n} ) dS = \iint_S \left( \phi \frac{d\psi}{dn} - \psi \frac{d\phi}{dn} \right) dS
$$

Thus

$$
\iiint_V ( \phi \nabla^2 \psi - \psi \nabla^2 \phi ) dV = \iint_S \left( \phi \frac{d\psi}{dn} - \psi \frac{d\phi}{dn} \right) dS
$$

### Supplementary Problem 62

#### Question

Prove

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{r} \times d\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{0}
$$

for any closed surface $S$.

#### Solution

Note that

$$
d\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{n} dS.
$$

We want to show

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{n} dS = \mathbf{0}.
$$

Let $\mathbf{C}$ be an arbitrary constant vector. Compute

$$
\mathbf{C} \cdot \iint_S \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_S \mathbf{C} \cdot (\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{n}) dS
$$

Using the scalar triple product permutation

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) = (\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot \mathbf{C}
$$

$$
= \iint_S (\mathbf{C} \times \mathbf{r}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

Apply the divergence theorem to the vector field

$$
\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{C} \times \mathbf{r}
$$

$$
= \iiint_V \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{C} \times \mathbf{r}) dV
$$

Using the identity

$$
\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{F} \times \mathbf{G}) = \mathbf{G} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) - \mathbf{F} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{G})
$$

$$
\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{C} \times \mathbf{r}) = \mathbf{r} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{C}) - \mathbf{C} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{r})
$$

Since $\mathbf{C}$ is constant,

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{C} = \mathbf{0}.
$$

The curl of the position vector

$$
\mathbf{r} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}
$$

is

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{r} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
x & y & z
\end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{0}
$$

Thus,

$$
\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{C} \times \mathbf{r}) = 0.
$$

The volume integral is

$$
\iiint_V (0) dV = 0
$$

So

$$
\mathbf{C} \cdot \iint_S \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{n} dS = 0
$$

Since this holds for any arbitrary constant vector $\mathbf{C}$

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{r} \times d\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{0}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 71

#### Question

Prove

$$
\iiint_V \nabla\phi \cdot \mathbf{A} dV = \iint_S \phi \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS - \iiint_V \phi \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} dV.
$$

#### Solution

Using the product rule for divergence

$$
\nabla \cdot (\phi \mathbf{A}) = \phi \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} + \nabla\phi \cdot \mathbf{A}
$$

Rearrange

$$
\nabla\phi \cdot \mathbf{A} = \nabla \cdot (\phi \mathbf{A}) - \phi \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}
$$

Integrate both sides over the volume $V$

$$
\iiint_V \nabla\phi \cdot \mathbf{A} dV = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot (\phi \mathbf{A}) dV - \iiint_V \phi \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} dV
$$

Apply the divergence theorem to the first volume integral on the right-hand side

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \cdot (\phi \mathbf{A}) dV = \iint_S \phi \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

Substitute this back

$$
\iiint_V \nabla\phi \cdot \mathbf{A} dV = \iint_S \phi \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS - \iiint_V \phi \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} dV
$$
