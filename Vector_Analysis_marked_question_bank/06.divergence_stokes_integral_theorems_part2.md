## Type-02: Green's Theorem in the Plane: Verification and Path Independence

### Solved Problem 2

#### Question

Verify Green's theorem in the plane for

$$
\oint_C (xy + y^2) dx + x^2 dy
$$

where $C$ is the closed curve of the region bounded by $y = x$ and $y = x^2$.

#### Solution

The curve $C$ is traversed counterclockwise: along the parabola $y = x^2$ from $(0,0)$ to $(1,1)$, and then returning along the line $y = x$ from $(1,1)$ to $(0,0)$.
1. Evaluate the line integral:
- Along $y = x^2 \implies dy = 2x dx$

$$
\int_0^1 [ (x)(x^2) + x^4 ] dx + x^2 (2x dx) = \int_0^1 (3x^3 + x^4) dx = \left[ \frac{3}{4}x^4 + \frac{1}{5}x^5 \right]\Big|_0^1 = \frac{19}{20}
$$

- Along $y = x \implies dy = dx$ from $(1,1)$ to $(0,0)$

$$
\int_1^0 [ (x)(x) + x^2 ] dx + x^2 dx = \int_1^0 3x^2 dx = \left[ x^3 \right]\Big|_1^0 = -1
$$

The total line integral is

$$
\oint_C = \frac{19}{20} - 1 = -\frac{1}{20}
$$

2. Evaluate the double integral:
Here $M = xy + y^2$ and $N = x^2$.

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = x + 2y, \quad \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = 2x
$$

$$
\iint_R \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy = \iint_R (2x - (x+2y)) dx dy = \int_0^1 \int_{x^2}^x (x - 2y) dy dx
$$

$$
= \int_0^1 \left[ xy - y^2 \right]\Big|_{x^2}^x dx = \int_0^1 [ (x^2 - x^2) - (x^3 - x^4) ] dx = \int_0^1 (x^4 - x^3) dx
$$

$$
= \left[ \frac{x^5}{5} - \frac{x^4}{4} \right]\Big|_0^1 = \frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{4} = -\frac{1}{20}
$$

The line integral and double integral match, verifying Green's theorem.

### Solved Problem 5

#### Question

Interpret physically the vector notation of Green's theorem in the plane

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_R (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) \cdot \mathbf{k} dR.
$$

#### Solution

Let $\mathbf{A}$ represent a force field acting on a particle. The line integral

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$

represents the work done in moving the particle around the closed path $C$.
According to Green's theorem, this work is equal to the integral of the normal component of the curl of the force field over the enclosed region $R$.
If

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{0}
$$

everywhere, then the line integral around any closed path $C$ is zero

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0
$$

This indicates that the work done is independent of the path taken between any two points, meaning the force field is conservative. In the plane,

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{0}
$$

is equivalent to $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$ where

$$
\mathbf{A} = M\mathbf{i} + N\mathbf{j}.
$$

### Solved Problem 6

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\int_{(0,0)}^{(2,1)} (10x^4 - 2xy^3) dx - 3x^2y^2 dy
$$

along the path $x^4 - 6xy^3 = 4y^2$.

#### Solution

Let $M = 10x^4 - 2xy^3$ and $N = -3x^2y^2$. Check for path independence

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = -6xy^2, \quad \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = -6xy^2
$$

Since $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$, the line integral is independent of the path.
We evaluate the integral along a simpler path: straight from $(0,0)$ to $(2,0)$ along the $x$-axis, then vertically to $(2,1)$.
1. Along the $x$-axis from $x=0$ to $2$ (where $y = 0, dy = 0$)

$$
\int_0^2 10x^4 dx = \left[ 2x^5 \right]\Big|_0^2 = 64
$$

2. Along the vertical segment from $y=0$ to $1$ (where $x = 2, dx = 0$)

$$
\int_0^1 -3(2)^2 y^2 dy = \int_0^1 -12y^2 dy = \left[ -4y^3 \right]\Big|_0^1 = -4
$$

The total line integral is

$$
64 - 4 = 60
$$

Alternatively, since it is an exact differential

$$
(10x^4 - 2xy^3) dx - 3x^2y^2 dy = d(2x^5 - x^2y^3)
$$

$$
\int_{(0,0)}^{(2,1)} d(2x^5 - x^2y^3) = \left( 2(2)^5 - (2)^2(1)^3 \right) - 0 = 64 - 4 = 60
$$

### Solved Problem 9

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\oint_C (y - \sin x) dx + \cos x dy
$$

around the triangle with vertices $O(0,0)$, $A(\pi/2,0)$, and $B(\pi/2,1)$ in the counterclockwise direction:
(a) directly,
(b) by using Green's theorem in the plane.

#### Solution

(a) Evaluate directly along the three boundary segments:
1. Along $OA$: $y = 0, dy = 0$ as $x$ goes from $0$ to $\pi/2$

$$
\int_0^{\pi/2} (-\sin x) dx = \left[ \cos x \right]\Big|_0^{\pi/2} = 0 - 1 = -1
$$

2. Along $AB$: $x = \pi/2, dx = 0$ as $y$ goes from $0$ to $1$

$$
\int_0^1 \cos(\pi/2) dy = 0
$$

3. Along $BO$: $y = \frac{2x}{\pi} \implies dy = \frac{2}{\pi} dx$ as $x$ goes from $\pi/2$ to $0$

$$
\int_{\pi/2}^0 \left[ \left(\frac{2x}{\pi} - \sin x\right) + \cos x \left(\frac{2}{\pi}\right) \right] dx
$$

$$
= \left[ \frac{x^2}{\pi} + \cos x + \frac{2}{\pi}\sin x \right]\Big|_{\pi/2}^0 = (1) - \left( \frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{2}{\pi} \right) = 1 - \frac{\pi}{4} - \frac{2}{\pi}
$$

Summing the three integrals

$$
-1 + 0 + 1 - \frac{\pi}{4} - \frac{2}{\pi} = -\frac{\pi}{4} - \frac{2}{\pi}
$$

(b) Using Green's theorem:
Here $M = y - \sin x$ and $N = \cos x$.

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = 1, \quad \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = - \sin x
$$

$$
\iint_R \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy = \int_0^{\pi/2} \int_0^{2x/\pi} (-\sin x - 1) dy dx
$$

$$
= \int_0^{\pi/2} (-\sin x - 1) \left(\frac{2x}{\pi}\right) dx = -\frac{2}{\pi} \int_0^{\pi/2} (x \sin x + x) dx
$$

Using integration by parts for the first term

$$
\int_0^{\pi/2} x \sin x dx = \left[ -x \cos x + \sin x \right]\Big|_0^{\pi/2} = 1
$$

$$
\int_0^{\pi/2} x dx = \frac{\pi^2}{8}
$$

Substitute these back

$$
= -\frac{2}{\pi} \left( 1 + \frac{\pi^2}{8} \right) = -\frac{2}{\pi} - \frac{\pi}{4}
$$

Both methods yield the same result.

### Solved Problem 10

#### Question

Show that Green's theorem in the plane is also valid for a multiply-connected region $R$ with a single inner boundary (one hole).

#### Solution

Let the outer boundary be $C_1$ (traversed counterclockwise) and the inner boundary be $C_2$ (traversed clockwise). Construct a cross-cut line segment $AD$ connecting $C_1$ and $C_2$.
The path $ADEFGDALKJHA$ forms a simple closed boundary for a simply-connected region. Applying Green's theorem

$$
\oint_{ADEFGDALKJHA} M dx + N dy = \iint_R \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy
$$

Decompose the left-hand side

$$
\oint = \int_{AD} + \oint_{DEFGD} + \int_{DA} + \oint_{ALKJHA}
$$

Since

$$
\int_{AD} = -\int_{DA},
$$

the integrals along the cross-cut cancel out

$$
= \oint_{C_1} + \oint_{C_2} = \oint_C M dx + N dy
$$

where $C$ is the total boundary consisting of $C_1$ and $C_2$. Therefore

$$
\oint_C M dx + N dy = \iint_R \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy
$$

### Solved Problem 11

#### Question

Show that Green's theorem in the plane holds for a region $R$ bounded by simple closed curves $C_1$ (outer boundary) and $C_2, C_3, C_4$ (inner boundaries).

#### Solution

Construct cross-cuts $AH$ (connecting $C_1$ to $C_2$), $LQ$ (connecting $C_2$ to $C_3$), and $TV$ (connecting $C_3$ to $C_4$).
This system of cross-cuts transforms $R$ into a simply-connected region with a single continuous boundary.
The line integral along this combined boundary is

$$
\int_{AH} + \int_{C_2} + \int_{LQ} + \int_{C_3} + \int_{TV} + \int_{C_4} + \int_{VT} + \int_{C_3'} + \int_{QL} + \int_{C_2'} + \int_{HA} + \int_{C_1}
$$

Because the cross-cut segments $AH, LQ$, and $TV$ are traversed twice in opposite directions, their contributions cancel

$$
\int_{AH} + \int_{HA} = 0, \quad \int_{LQ} + \int_{QL} = 0, \quad \int_{TV} + \int_{VT} = 0
$$

The remaining line integrals represent traversals of all boundaries in the positive sense

$$
\oint_{C_1} + \oint_{C_2} + \oint_{C_3} + \oint_{C_4} = \oint_C M dx + N dy
$$

where $C$ represents the collection of all boundary curves. Thus

$$
\oint_C M dx + N dy = \iint_R \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy
$$

### Solved Problem 12

#### Question

Prove that

$$
\oint_C M dx + N dy = 0
$$

around every closed curve $C$ in a simply-connected region if and only if $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$ everywhere in the region.

#### Solution

*Sufficiency:* Assume $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$ in the region $R$ bounded by $C$. By Green's theorem

$$
\oint_C M dx + N dy = \iint_R \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy = \iint_R (0) dx dy = 0
$$

*Necessity:* Suppose

$$
\oint_C M dx + N dy = 0
$$

for all closed curves $C$.
If $\frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \ne 0$ at some point $P$, say it is positive, then by continuity of the partial derivatives, it must remain positive in a small surrounding region $A$ centered at $P$.
Let $\Gamma$ be the closed boundary of $A$. Then

$$
\oint_{\Gamma} M dx + N dy = \iint_A \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy > 0
$$

This contradicts the hypothesis that the line integral is zero around every closed path. A similar contradiction arises if we assume it is negative.
Thus, $\frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = 0$ everywhere.

### Solved Problem 13

#### Question

Let

$$
\mathbf{F} = \frac{-y\mathbf{i} + x\mathbf{j}}{x^2 + y^2}.
$$

(a) Calculate $\nabla \times \mathbf{F}$.
(b) Evaluate

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$

around any closed path and explain the results.

#### Solution

(a) Compute the curl of

$$
\mathbf{F} = M\mathbf{i} + N\mathbf{j}
$$

where $M = \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}$ and $N = \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}$

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
M & N & 0
\end{vmatrix} = \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) \mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left( \frac{x}{x^2+y^2} \right) = \frac{(x^2+y^2)(1) - x(2x)}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = \frac{y^2-x^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}
$$

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left( \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2} \right) = \frac{(x^2+y^2)(-1) - (-y)(2y)}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = \frac{y^2-x^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}
$$

Thus, $\frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = 0$, meaning

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}
$$

at all points except at the origin $(0,0)$, where the field is undefined.

(b) Write the line integral in polar coordinates $x = \rho \cos \phi, y = \rho \sin \phi$

$$
dx = \cos \phi d\rho - \rho \sin \phi d\phi, \quad dy = \sin \phi d\rho + \rho \cos \phi d\phi
$$

$$
\mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \frac{-y dx + x dy}{x^2+y^2} = \frac{-rho \sin \phi (\cos \phi d\rho - \rho \sin \phi d\phi) + \rho \cos \phi (\sin \phi d\rho + \rho \cos \phi d\phi)}{\rho^2} = d\phi
$$

- For a closed path $C$ enclosing the origin, the angle $\phi$ changes by $2\pi$

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi} d\phi = 2\pi
$$

- For a closed path $C$ not enclosing the origin, the angle $\phi$ starts and ends at the same value $\phi_0$ without completing a loop

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \oint_{\phi_0}^{\phi_0} d\phi = 0
$$

This does not contradict Solved Problem 12 because the derivatives are not continuous at the origin $(0,0)$.

### Supplementary Problem 37

#### Question

Verify Green's theorem in the plane for

$$
\oint_C (3x^2 - 8y^2) dx + (4y - 6xy) dy
$$

where $C$ is the boundary of the region defined by:
(a) $y = \sqrt{x}$, $y = x^2$
(b) $x = 0$, $y = 0$, $x + y = 1$

#### Solution

Let $M = 3x^2 - 8y^2$ and $N = 4y - 6xy$.

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = -16y, \quad \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = -6y
$$

$$
\frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = -6y - (-16y) = 10y
$$

(a) Region bounded by $y = \sqrt{x}$ and $y = x^2$ (intersecting at $(0,0)$ and $(1,1)$).
- Double Integral

$$
\iint_R 10y dy dx = \int_0^1 \int_{x^2}^{\sqrt{x}} 10y dy dx = \int_0^1 5 \left[ y^2 \right]\Big|_{x^2}^{\sqrt{x}} dx = 5 \int_0^1 (x - x^4) dx
$$

$$
= 5 \left[ \frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{x^5}{5} \right]\Big|_0^1 = 5 \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{5} \right) = \frac{3}{2}
$$

- Line Integral:
Along $y = x^2 \implies dy = 2x dx$

$$
\int_0^1 [ (3x^2 - 8x^4) + (4x^2 - 6x^3)(2x) ] dx = \int_0^1 (3x^2 + 8x^3 - 20x^4) dx
$$

$$
= \left[ x^3 + 2x^4 - 4x^5 \right]\Big|_0^1 = -1
$$

Along $y = \sqrt{x} \implies x = y^2 \implies dx = 2y dy$ from $(1,1)$ to $(0,0)$

$$
\int_1^0 [ (3y^4 - 8y^2)(2y) + (4y - 6y^3) ] dy = \int_1^0 (6y^5 - 22y^3 + 4y) dy
$$

$$
= \left[ y^6 - \frac{11}{2}y^4 + 2y^2 \right]\Big|_1^0 = - \left( 1 - \frac{11}{2} + 2 \right) = \frac{5}{2}
$$

Total line integral $= -1 + \frac{5}{2} = \frac{3}{2}$. Verified.

(b) Region bounded by $x = 0$, $y = 0$, $x+y=1$.
- Double Integral

$$
\iint_R 10y dy dx = \int_0^1 \int_0^{1-x} 10y dy dx = \int_0^1 5(1-x)^2 dx = \left[ -\frac{5}{3}(1-x)^3 \right]\Big|_0^1 = \frac{5}{3}
$$

- Line Integral:
1. Along $y = 0, dy = 0$ from $x=0$ to $1$

$$
\int_0^1 3x^2 dx = \left[ x^3 \right]\Big|_0^1 = 1
$$

2. Along $x+y = 1 \implies dy = -dx$ from $x=1$ to $0$

$$
\int_1^0 [ (3x^2 - 8(1-x)^2) - (4(1-x) - 6x(1-x)) ] dx
$$

$$
= \int_1^0 (-11x^2 + 26x - 12) dx = \left[ -\frac{11}{3}x^3 + 13x^2 - 12x \right]\Big|_1^0 = \frac{8}{3}
$$

3. Along $x = 0, dx = 0$ from $y=1$ to $0$

$$
\int_1^0 4y dy = \left[ 2y^2 \right]\Big|_1^0 = -2
$$

Total line integral $= 1 + \frac{8}{3} - 2 = \frac{5}{3}$. Verified.

### Supplementary Problem 38

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\oint_C (3x + 4y) dx + (2x - 3y) dy
$$

where $C$, a circle of radius two with center at the origin, is traversed counterclockwise.

#### Solution

Let $M = 3x+4y$ and $N = 2x-3y$.

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = 4, \quad \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = 2 \implies \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = 2 - 4 = -2
$$

Apply Green's theorem

$$
\oint_C M dx + N dy = \iint_R -2 dx dy = -2 \times (\text{Area of circle}) = -2 (4\pi) = -8\pi
$$

### Supplementary Problem 40

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\oint_C (x^2 - 2xy) dx + (x^2y + 3) dy
$$

around the boundary of the region defined by $y^2 = 8x$ and $x = 2$:
(a) directly,
(b) by using Green's theorem.

#### Solution

Let $M = x^2 - 2xy$ and $N = x^2y + 3$.

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = -2x, \quad \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = 2xy \implies \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = 2xy + 2x
$$

(a) Evaluate directly along two boundary segments:
The region is bounded by $x=2$ on the right, and the parabola on the left.
Moving counterclockwise: along $x=2$ from $y=-4$ to $y=4$ (going upwards), and then along the parabola from $(2,4)$ to $(2,-4)$ (going left and downwards).
1. Along the vertical line $x = 2 \implies dx = 0$ as $y$ goes from $-4$ to $4$

$$
\int_{-4}^{4} (4y + 3) dy = \left[ 2y^2 + 3y \right]\Big|_{-4}^{4} = (32 + 12) - (32 - 12) = 24
$$

2. Along the parabola $y^2 = 8x \implies x = \frac{y^2}{8} \implies dx = \frac{y}{4} dy$ as $y$ goes from $4$ to $-4$

$$
\int_{4}^{-4} \left[ \left(\frac{y^4}{64} - \frac{y^3}{4}\right) \left(\frac{y}{4}\right) + \left(\frac{y^5}{64} + 3\right) \right] dy
$$

$$
= \int_{4}^{-4} \left( \frac{5y^5}{256} - \frac{y^4}{16} + 3 \right) dy
$$

Since $\frac{5y^5}{256}$ is odd, its integral is 0.

$$
= 2 \int_4^{0} \left( -\frac{y^4}{16} + 3 \right) dy = -2 \left[ -\frac{y^5}{80} + 3y \right]\Big|_0^4 = -2 \left( -\frac{1024}{80} + 12 \right) = \frac{8}{5}
$$

Total line integral $= 24 + \frac{8}{5} = \frac{128}{5}$.

(b) Using Green's theorem

$$
\iint_R 2x(y+1) dy dx = \int_0^2 2x \left[ \frac{y^2}{2} + y \right]\Big|_{-\sqrt{8x}}^{\sqrt{8x}} dx
$$

$$
= \int_0^2 2x \left( 2\sqrt{8x} \right) dx = 8\sqrt{2} \int_0^2 x^{3/2} dx = 8\sqrt{2} \left[ \frac{2}{5}x^{5/2} \right]\Big|_0^2 = \frac{128}{5}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 41

Evaluate

$$
\int_{(0,0)}^{(\pi, 2)} (6xy - y^2) dx + (3x^2 - 2xy) dy
$$

along the cycloid $x = \theta - \sin \theta$, $y = 1 - \cos \theta$.

#### Solution

$$

$$
\int_{(0,0)}^{(\pi, 2)} d(3x^2y - xy^2) = \left[ 3x^2y - xy^2 \right]\Big|_{(0,0)}^{(\pi, 2)} = 3\pi^2(2) - \pi(2)^2 = 6\pi^2 - 4\pi
$$

### Supplementary Problem 42

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\oint_C (3x^2 + 2y) dx - (x + 3\cos y) dy
$$

around the parallelogram having vertices at $(0,0)$, $(2,0)$, $(3,1)$ and $(1,1)$ in the counterclockwise direction.

#### Solution

Let $M = 3x^2+2y$ and $N = -x - 3\cos y$.

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = 2, \quad \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = -1 \implies \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = -1 - 2 = -3
$$

Apply Green's theorem

$$
\oint_C M dx + N dy = \iint_R -3 dx dy = -3 \times (\text{Area of parallelogram})
$$

The base of the parallelogram is on the $x$-axis from $x=0$ to $2$, so base $b = 2$. The height is $h = 1$ (the vertical distance to $y=1$).

$$
\text{Area} = b \times h = 2 \times 1 = 2
$$

Thus

$$
\oint_C = -3 \times 2 = -6
$$

### Supplementary Problem 49

#### Question

Verify Green's theorem in the plane for

$$
\oint_C (2x - y^3) dx - xy dy
$$

where $C$ is the boundary of the region enclosed by the concentric circles $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ and $x^2 + y^2 = 9$.

#### Solution

Let $M = 2x-y^3$ and $N = -xy$.

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = -3y^2, \quad \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = -y \implies \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = 3y^2 - y
$$

1. Evaluate the double integral over the annular region $R$:
Convert to polar coordinates

$$
\iint_R (3y^2 - y) dx dy = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_1^3 (3\rho^2 \sin^2 \theta - \rho \sin \theta) \rho d\rho d\theta
$$

$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} \left[ \frac{3}{4}\rho^4 \sin^2 \theta - \frac{1}{3}\rho^3 \sin \theta \right]\Big|_1^3 d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi} \left( 60 \sin^2 \theta - \frac{26}{3}\sin \theta \right) d\theta
$$

$$
= 60 \int_0^{2\pi} \sin^2 \theta d\theta - 0 = 60\pi
$$

2. Evaluate the line integral:
The boundary $C$ consists of the outer circle $C_1$ of radius 3 (counterclockwise) and the inner circle $C_2$ of radius 1 (clockwise).
For a circle of radius $a$

$$
x = a \cos \theta, \quad y = a \sin \theta \implies dx = -a \sin \theta d\theta, \quad dy = a \cos \theta d\theta
$$

$$
\oint (2x - y^3) dx - xy dy = \int_0^{2\pi} [ (2a\cos \theta - a^3\sin^3 \theta)(-a\sin \theta) - a^2\sin \theta\cos \theta(a\cos \theta) ] d\theta
$$

$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} ( -2a^2 \sin \theta\cos \theta + a^4 \sin^4 \theta - a^3 \sin \theta\cos^2 \theta ) d\theta = a^4 \left(\frac{3\pi}{4}\right)
$$

- For the outer circle $C_1$ ($a=3$)

$$
\oint_{C_1} = 81 \left(\frac{3\pi}{4}\right) = \frac{243\pi}{4}
$$

- For the inner circle $C_2$ ($a=1$, traversed clockwise)

$$
\oint_{C_2} = - 1 \left(\frac{3\pi}{4}\right) = -\frac{3\pi}{4}
$$

The total line integral is

$$
\oint_C = \frac{243\pi}{4} - \frac{3\pi}{4} = 60\pi
$$

Both integrals match, verifying Green's theorem.

### Supplementary Problem 50

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\int_{(1,0)}^{(-1,0)} \frac{-y dx + x dy}{x^2 + y^2}
$$

along the following paths:
(a) straight line segments from $(1,0)$ to $(1,1)$, then to $(-1,1)$, then to $(-1,0)$.
(b) straight line segments from $(1,0)$ to $(1,-1)$, then to $(-1,-1)$, then to $(-1,0)$.
Explain why the results differ despite $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$.

#### Solution

In polar coordinates

$$
\frac{-y dx + x dy}{x^2 + y^2} = d\phi
$$

(a) Along the upper path, the angle $\phi$ starts at $0$ (at $(1,0)$) and increases continuously to $\pi$ (at $(-1,0)$)

$$
\int_0^\pi d\phi = \pi
$$

(b) Along the lower path, the angle $\phi$ starts at $0$ and decreases continuously to $-\pi$ (at $(-1,0)$)

$$
\int_0^{-\pi} d\phi = -\pi
$$

Explanation: Although

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}
$$

at all points where the field is defined, the field is undefined at the origin $(0,0)$. The region containing the two paths cannot be enclosed in a simply-connected region that excludes the origin. Green's theorem does not apply to a loop surrounding the origin.
