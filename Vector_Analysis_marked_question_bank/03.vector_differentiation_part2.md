## Type-03: Frenet-Serret Formulas, Curvature, and Torsion of Space Curves

### Solved Problem 6

#### Question

(a) Find the unit tangent vector to any point on the curve $x = t^2 + 1$, $y = 4t - 3$, $z = 2t^2 - 6t$.
(b) Determine the unit tangent at the point where $t = 2$.

#### Solution

(a) The position vector of any point on the space curve is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = (t^2 + 1)\mathbf{i} + (4t - 3)\mathbf{j} + (2t^2 - 6t)\mathbf{k}
$$

Differentiate the position vector with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = 2t\mathbf{i} + 4\mathbf{j} + (4t - 6)\mathbf{k}
$$

Calculate the magnitude of this derivative:

$$
\left| \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \right| = \sqrt{(2t)^2 + 4^2 + (4t - 6)^2} = \sqrt{4t^2 + 16 + 16t^2 - 48t + 36}
$$

$$
\left| \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \right| = \sqrt{20t^2 - 48t + 52} = 2\sqrt{5t^2 - 12t + 13}
$$

The unit tangent vector $\mathbf{T}$ is:

$$
\mathbf{T} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}/dt}{|d\mathbf{r}/dt|} = \frac{2t\mathbf{i} + 4\mathbf{j} + (4t - 6)\mathbf{k}}{2\sqrt{5t^2 - 12t + 13}} = \frac{t\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} + (2t - 3)\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{5t^2 - 12t + 13}}
$$

(b) Evaluate the unit tangent vector at $t = 2$:

$$
\mathbf{T} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} + (2(2) - 3)\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{5(2)^2 - 12(2) + 13}} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{20 - 24 + 13}}
$$

$$
\mathbf{T} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{9}} = \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{k}
$$

### Solved Problem 22

#### Question

Find the curvature $\kappa$ and torsion $\tau$ for the space curve $x = t$, $y = t^2$, $z = \frac{2}{3}t^3$.

#### Solution

The position vector of any point on the curve is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = t\mathbf{i} + t^2\mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}t^3\mathbf{k}
$$

Differentiate with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \mathbf{i} + 2t\mathbf{j} + 2t^2\mathbf{k}
$$

Find the derivative of arc length with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{ds}{dt} = \left| \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \right| = \sqrt{1^2 + (2t)^2 + (2t^2)^2} = \sqrt{1 + 4t^2 + 4t^4} = \sqrt{(1 + 2t^2)^2} = 1 + 2t^2
$$

The unit tangent vector is:

$$
\mathbf{T} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}/dt}{ds/dt} = \frac{\mathbf{i} + 2t\mathbf{j} + 2t^2\mathbf{k}}{1 + 2t^2}
$$

Differentiate $\mathbf{T}$ with respect to $t$ using the quotient rule:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{dt} = \frac{(1 + 2t^2)(2\mathbf{j} + 4t\mathbf{k}) - (\mathbf{i} + 2t\mathbf{j} + 2t^2\mathbf{k})(4t)}{(1 + 2t^2)^2}
$$

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{dt} = \frac{-4t\mathbf{i} + (2 - 4t^2)\mathbf{j} + 4t\mathbf{k}}{(1 + 2t^2)^2}
$$

Using the relation $\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds} = \frac{d\mathbf{T}/dt}{ds/dt}$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds} = \frac{-4t\mathbf{i} + (2 - 4t^2)\mathbf{j} + 4t\mathbf{k}}{(1 + 2t^2)^3}
$$

The curvature $\kappa$ is the magnitude of $\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds}$:

$$
\kappa = \left| \frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds} \right| = \frac{\sqrt{(-4t)^2 + (2 - 4t^2)^2 + (4t)^2}}{(1 + 2t^2)^3} = \frac{\sqrt{16t^2 + 4 - 16t^2 + 16t^4 + 16t^2}}{(1 + 2t^2)^3}
$$

$$
\kappa = \frac{\sqrt{16t^4 + 16t^2 + 4}}{(1 + 2t^2)^3} = \frac{\sqrt{4(4t^4 + 4t^2 + 1)}}{(1 + 2t^2)^3} = \frac{2(1 + 2t^2)}{(1 + 2t^2)^3} = \frac{2}{(1 + 2t^2)^2}
$$

From the Frenet-Serret formula $\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds} = \kappa\mathbf{N}$, we solve for the principal normal vector $\mathbf{N}$:

$$
\mathbf{N} = \frac{1}{\kappa}\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds} = \frac{(1 + 2t^2)^2}{2} \cdot \frac{-4t\mathbf{i} + (2 - 4t^2)\mathbf{j} + 4t\mathbf{k}}{(1 + 2t^2)^3} = \frac{-2t\mathbf{i} + (1 - 2t^2)\mathbf{j} + 2t\mathbf{k}}{1 + 2t^2}
$$

The binormal vector $\mathbf{B}$ is:

$$
\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{T} \times \mathbf{N} = \frac{1}{(1 + 2t^2)^2} \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
1 & 2t & 2t^2 \\
-2t & 1 - 2t^2 & 2t
\end{vmatrix}
$$

$$
\mathbf{B} = \frac{1}{(1 + 2t^2)^2} \left\{ \mathbf{i}[4t^2 - 2t^2(1 - 2t^2)] - \mathbf{j}[2t - 2t^2(-2t)] + \mathbf{k}[1 - 2t^2 - 2t(-2t)] \right\}
$$

$$
\mathbf{B} = \frac{1}{(1 + 2t^2)^2} \left\{ 2t^2(1 + 2t^2)\mathbf{i} - 2t(1 + 2t^2)\mathbf{j} + (1 + 2t^2)\mathbf{k} \right\} = \frac{2t^2\mathbf{i} - 2t\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}}{1 + 2t^2}
$$

To find the torsion $\tau$, differentiate $\mathbf{B}$ with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{B}}{dt} = \frac{(1 + 2t^2)(4t\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j}) - (2t^2\mathbf{i} - 2t\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k})(4t)}{(1 + 2t^2)^2}
$$

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{B}}{dt} = \frac{4t\mathbf{i} + (4t^2 - 2)\mathbf{j} - 4t\mathbf{k}}{(1 + 2t^2)^2}
$$

Divide by $ds/dt = 1 + 2t^2$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{B}}{ds} = \frac{4t\mathbf{i} + (4t^2 - 2)\mathbf{j} - 4t\mathbf{k}}{(1 + 2t^2)^3}
$$

By Frenet-Serret, $\frac{d\mathbf{B}}{ds} = -\tau\mathbf{N}$. Substitute the expression for $\mathbf{N}$:

$$
-\tau\mathbf{N} = -\tau \left( \frac{-2t\mathbf{i} + (1 - 2t^2)\mathbf{j} + 2t\mathbf{k}}{1 + 2t^2} \right) = \frac{2\tau t\mathbf{i} - \tau(1 - 2t^2)\mathbf{j} - 2\tau t\mathbf{k}}{1 + 2t^2}
$$

Equating components, we solve for $\tau$:

$$
\frac{2\tau t}{1 + 2t^2} = \frac{4t}{(1 + 2t^2)^3} \implies \tau = \frac{2}{(1 + 2t^2)^2}
$$

Thus, the curvature and torsion are equal:

$$
\kappa = \frac{2}{(1 + 2t^2)^2}, \quad \tau = \frac{2}{(1 + 2t^2)^2}
$$

### Solved Problem 19

#### Question

Sketch the space curve $x = 3\cos t$, $y = 3\sin t$, $z = 4t$ and find:
(a) the unit tangent $\mathbf{T}$,
(b) the principal normal $\mathbf{N}$, curvature $\kappa$, and radius of curvature $\rho$,
(c) the binormal $\mathbf{B}$, torsion $\tau$, and radius of torsion $\sigma$.

#### Solution

The curve lies on the cylinder $x^2 + y^2 = 9$. It is a circular helix of radius 3.

(a) The position vector for any point on the curve is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = 3\cos t \mathbf{i} + 3\sin t \mathbf{j} + 4t\mathbf{k}
$$

Differentiate with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = -3\sin t \mathbf{i} + 3\cos t \mathbf{j} + 4\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\frac{ds}{dt} = \left| \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \right| = \sqrt{(-3\sin t)^2 + (3\cos t)^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t) + 16} = 5
$$

Thus, the unit tangent vector is:

$$
\mathbf{T} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}/dt}{ds/dt} = -\frac{3}{5}\sin t \mathbf{i} + \frac{3}{5}\cos t \mathbf{j} + \frac{4}{5}\mathbf{k}
$$

(b) Differentiate $\mathbf{T}$ with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{dt} = -\frac{3}{5}\cos t \mathbf{i} - \frac{3}{5}\sin t \mathbf{j}
$$

Divide by $ds/dt = 5$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds} = \frac{d\mathbf{T}/dt}{ds/dt} = -\frac{3}{25}\cos t \mathbf{i} - \frac{3}{25}\sin t \mathbf{j}
$$

The curvature $\kappa$ is the magnitude of $\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds}$:

$$
\kappa = \left| \frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds} \right| = \sqrt{\left(-\frac{3}{25}\cos t\right)^2 + \left(-\frac{3}{25}\sin t\right)^2} = \frac{3}{25}
$$

The radius of curvature is:

$$
\rho = \frac{1}{\kappa} = \frac{25}{3}
$$

The principal normal vector is:

$$
\mathbf{N} = \frac{1}{\kappa}\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds} = \frac{25}{3}\left(-\frac{3}{25}\cos t \mathbf{i} - \frac{3}{25}\sin t \mathbf{j}\right) = -\cos t \mathbf{i} - \sin t \mathbf{j}
$$

(c) The binormal vector is:

$$
\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{T} \times \mathbf{N} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
-\frac{3}{5}\sin t & \frac{3}{5}\cos t & \frac{4}{5} \\
-\cos t & -\sin t & 0
\end{vmatrix} = \frac{4}{5}\sin t \mathbf{i} - \frac{4}{5}\cos t \mathbf{j} + \frac{3}{5}\mathbf{k}
$$

Differentiate $\mathbf{B}$ with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{B}}{dt} = \frac{4}{5}\cos t \mathbf{i} + \frac{4}{5}\sin t \mathbf{j}
$$

Divide by $ds/dt = 5$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{B}}{ds} = \frac{d\mathbf{B}/dt}{ds/dt} = \frac{4}{25}\cos t \mathbf{i} + \frac{4}{25}\sin t \mathbf{j}
$$

By Frenet-Serret, $\frac{d\mathbf{B}}{ds} = -\tau\mathbf{N}$:

$$
\frac{4}{25}\cos t \mathbf{i} + \frac{4}{25}\sin t \mathbf{j} = -\tau(-\cos t \mathbf{i} - \sin t \mathbf{j}) \implies \tau = \frac{4}{25}
$$

The radius of torsion is:

$$
\sigma = \frac{1}{\tau} = \frac{25}{4}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 49

#### Question

Find the curvature $\kappa$ and torsion $\tau$ for the space curve $x = t$, $y = t^2$, $z = t^3$ (the twisted cubic).

#### Solution

The position vector of the curve is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = t\mathbf{i} + t^2\mathbf{j} + t^3\mathbf{k}
$$

Differentiate with respect to $t$ to find the first three derivatives:

$$
\dot{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{i} + 2t\mathbf{j} + 3t^2\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = 2\mathbf{j} + 6t\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\dddot{\mathbf{r}} = 6\mathbf{k}
$$

Calculate the cross product of the first and second derivatives:

$$
\dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
1 & 2t & 3t^2 \\
0 & 2 & 6t
\end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{i}(12t^2 - 6t^2) - \mathbf{j}(6t - 0) + \mathbf{k}(2 - 0)
$$

$$
\dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = 6t^2\mathbf{i} - 6t\mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}
$$

Find the magnitude of the velocity vector:

$$
|\dot{\mathbf{r}}| = \sqrt{1^2 + (2t)^2 + (3t^2)^2} = \sqrt{9t^4 + 4t^2 + 1}
$$

Find the magnitude of the cross product:

$$
|\dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}}| = \sqrt{(6t^2)^2 + (-6t)^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{36t^4 + 36t^2 + 4} = 2\sqrt{9t^4 + 9t^2 + 1}
$$

Using the curvature formula:

$$
\kappa = \frac{|\dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}}|}{|\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^3} = \frac{2\sqrt{9t^4 + 9t^2 + 1}}{(9t^4 + 4t^2 + 1)^{3/2}}
$$

To find the torsion $\tau$, compute the scalar triple product:

$$
\dot{\mathbf{r}} \cdot \ddot{\mathbf{r}} \times \dddot{\mathbf{r}} = (\dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}}) \cdot \dddot{\mathbf{r}} = (6t^2\mathbf{i} - 6t\mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}) \cdot (6\mathbf{k}) = 12
$$

Using the torsion formula:

$$
\tau = \frac{\dot{\mathbf{r}} \cdot \ddot{\mathbf{r}} \times \dddot{\mathbf{r}}}{|\dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}}|^2} = \frac{12}{36t^4 + 36t^2 + 4} = \frac{3}{9t^4 + 9t^2 + 1}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 60

#### Question

Find equations for the tangent, principal normal, and binormal to the curve $x = 3\cos t$, $y = 3\sin t$, $z = 4t$ at the point where $t = \pi$.

#### Solution

At $t = \pi$, the position vector of the point on the curve is:

$$
\mathbf{r}_0 = 3\cos \pi \mathbf{i} + 3\sin \pi \mathbf{j} + 4\pi\mathbf{k} = -3\mathbf{i} + 4\pi\mathbf{k}
$$

Using the vector derivations from Solved Problem 19 at $t = \pi$:

$$
\mathbf{T}_0 = -\frac{3}{5}\sin \pi \mathbf{i} + \frac{3}{5}\cos \pi \mathbf{j} + \frac{4}{5}\mathbf{k} = -\frac{3}{5}\mathbf{j} + \frac{4}{5}\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\mathbf{N}_0 = -\cos \pi \mathbf{i} - \sin \pi \mathbf{j} = \mathbf{i}
$$

$$
\mathbf{B}_0 = \frac{4}{5}\sin \pi \mathbf{i} - \frac{4}{5}\cos \pi \mathbf{j} + \frac{3}{5}\mathbf{k} = \frac{4}{5}\mathbf{j} + \frac{3}{5}\mathbf{k}
$$

(a) The tangent line passes through $\mathbf{r}_0$ and is parallel to $\mathbf{T}_0$. Its vector equation is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + t'\mathbf{T}_0 \implies \mathbf{r} = -3\mathbf{i} + 4\pi\mathbf{k} + t'\left(-\frac{3}{5}\mathbf{j} + \frac{4}{5}\mathbf{k}\right)
$$

In rectangular form (letting $t = t'/5$):

$$
x = -3, \quad y = -3t, \quad z = 4\pi + 4t
$$

(b) The principal normal line passes through $\mathbf{r}_0$ and is parallel to $\mathbf{N}_0$. Its vector equation is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + t'\mathbf{N}_0 \implies \mathbf{r} = -3\mathbf{i} + 4\pi\mathbf{k} + t'\mathbf{i}
$$

In rectangular form (letting $t = t'-3$):

$$
x = t, \quad y = 4\pi, \quad z = 0
$$

(c) The binormal line passes through $\mathbf{r}_0$ and is parallel to $\mathbf{B}_0$. Its vector equation is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + t'\mathbf{B}_0 \implies \mathbf{r} = -3\mathbf{i} + 4\pi\mathbf{k} + t'\left(\frac{4}{5}\mathbf{j} + \frac{3}{5}\mathbf{k}\right)
$$

In rectangular form (letting $t = t'/5$):

$$
x = -3, \quad y = 4\pi + 4t, \quad z = 3t
$$

## Type-04: Tangent, Normal, and Osculating Planes to Surfaces and Curves

### Solved Problem 23

#### Question

Find equations in vector and rectangular form for the (a) tangent, (b) principal normal, and (c) binormal to the curve $x = t, y = t^2, z = \frac{2}{3}t^3$ at the point where $t = 1$.

#### Solution

At $t = 1$, the position vector of the point on the curve is:

$$
\mathbf{r}_0 = \mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k}
$$

From the derivations in Solved Problem 22 at $t = 1$:

$$
\mathbf{T}_0 = \frac{\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{3} = \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{i} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\mathbf{N}_0 = \frac{-2\mathbf{i} + (1 - 2)\mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{3} = -\frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} - \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\mathbf{B}_0 = \frac{2\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}}{3} = \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} - \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{k}
$$

(a) The tangent line is parallel to $\mathbf{T}_0$. Its vector equation is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + u\mathbf{T}_0 = \left(\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k}\right) + u\left(\frac{1}{3}\mathbf{i} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k}\right)
$$

In rectangular form:

$$
\frac{x - 1}{1/3} = \frac{y - 1}{2/3} = \frac{z - 2/3}{2/3} \implies x - 1 = \frac{y - 1}{2} = \frac{z - 2/3}{2}
$$

(b) The principal normal line is parallel to $\mathbf{N}_0$. Its vector equation is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + u\mathbf{N}_0 = \left(\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k}\right) + u\left(-\frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} - \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k}\right)
$$

In rectangular form:

$$
\frac{x - 1}{-2/3} = \frac{y - 1}{-1/3} = \frac{z - 2/3}{2/3} \implies \frac{x - 1}{-2} = \frac{y - 1}{-1} = \frac{z - 2/3}{2}
$$

(c) The binormal line is parallel to $\mathbf{B}_0$. Its vector equation is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + u\mathbf{B}_0 = \left(\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k}\right) + u\left(\frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} - \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{k}\right)
$$

In rectangular form:

$$
\frac{x - 1}{2/3} = \frac{y - 1}{-2/3} = \frac{z - 2/3}{1/3} \implies \frac{x - 1}{2} = \frac{y - 1}{-2} = z - \frac{2}{3}
$$

### Solved Problem 24

#### Question

Find equations in vector and rectangular form for the (a) osculating plane, (b) normal plane, and (c) rectifying plane to the curve of Problems 22 and 23 at the point where $t = 1$.

#### Solution

At $t = 1$, the position vector of the point is $\mathbf{r}_0 = \mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k}$.

(a) The **osculating plane** contains the tangent and principal normal. It is perpendicular to the binormal vector $\mathbf{B}_0 = \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} - \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{k}$.
Its vector equation is:

$$
(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0) \cdot \mathbf{B}_0 = 0
$$

$$
\left[ (x - 1)\mathbf{i} + (y - 1)\mathbf{j} + \left(z - \frac{2}{3}\right)\mathbf{k} \right] \cdot \left( \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} - \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{k} \right) = 0
$$

$$
\frac{2}{3}(x - 1) - \frac{2}{3}(y - 1) + \frac{1}{3}\left(z - \frac{2}{3}\right) = 0
$$

Multiply by 3 to simplify:

$$
2(x - 1) - 2(y - 1) + \left(z - \frac{2}{3}\right) = 0 \implies 2x - 2y + z = \frac{2}{3}
$$

(b) The **normal plane** is perpendicular to the unit tangent vector $\mathbf{T}_0 = \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{i} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k}$.
Its vector equation is:

$$
(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0) \cdot \mathbf{T}_0 = 0
$$

$$
\left[ (x - 1)\mathbf{i} + (y - 1)\mathbf{j} + \left(z - \frac{2}{3}\right)\mathbf{k} \right] \cdot \left( \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{i} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k} \right) = 0
$$

$$
\frac{1}{3}(x - 1) + \frac{2}{3}(y - 1) + \frac{2}{3}\left(z - \frac{2}{3}\right) = 0
$$

Multiply by 3 to simplify:

$$
(x - 1) + 2(y - 1) + 2\left(z - \frac{2}{3}\right) = 0 \implies x + 2y + 2z = \frac{13}{3}
$$

(c) The **rectifying plane** is perpendicular to the principal normal vector $\mathbf{N}_0 = -\frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} - \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k}$.
Its vector equation is:

$$
(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0) \cdot \mathbf{N}_0 = 0
$$

$$
\left[ (x - 1)\mathbf{i} + (y - 1)\mathbf{j} + \left(z - \frac{2}{3}\right)\mathbf{k} \right] \cdot \left( -\frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} - \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{k} \right) = 0
$$

$$
-\frac{2}{3}(x - 1) - \frac{1}{3}(y - 1) + \frac{2}{3}\left(z - \frac{2}{3}\right) = 0
$$

Multiply by 3 to simplify:

$$
-2(x - 1) - (y - 1) + 2\left(z - \frac{2}{3}\right) = 0 \implies 2x + y - 2z = \frac{5}{3}
$$

### Solved Problem 26

#### Question

Find equations for the tangent plane and normal line to the surface $z = x^2 + y^2$ at the point $(1, 2, 5)$.

#### Solution

We represent the surface equation as the level set of a function $F(x, y, z) = x^2 + y^2 - z = 0$.
The gradient of $F$ is a vector normal to the surface at any point $(x, y, z)$:

$$
\nabla F = 2x\mathbf{i} + 2y\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}
$$

Evaluate the gradient at the point $(1, 2, 5)$ to find the normal vector $\mathbf{n}$:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \nabla F|_{(1,2,5)} = 2(1)\mathbf{i} + 2(2)\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k} = 2\mathbf{i} + 4\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}
$$

The tangent plane passes through $(1, 2, 5)$ and is perpendicular to $\mathbf{n}$. Its equation is:

$$
2(x - 1) + 4(y - 2) - 1(z - 5) = 0
$$

$$
2x - 2 + 4y - 8 - z + 5 = 0 \implies 2x + 4y - z = 5
$$

The normal line passes through $(1, 2, 5)$ and is parallel to the normal vector $\mathbf{n} = 2\mathbf{i} + 4\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}$.
Its rectangular equations are:

$$
\frac{x - 1}{2} = \frac{y - 2}{4} = \frac{z - 5}{-1}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 61

#### Question

Find equations for the (a) osculating plane, (b) normal plane, and (c) rectifying plane to the curve $x = 3t - t^3$, $y = 3t^2$, $z = 3t + t^3$ at the point where $t = 1$.

#### Solution

The position vector of the space curve is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = (3t - t^3)\mathbf{i} + 3t^2\mathbf{j} + (3t + t^3)\mathbf{k}
$$

At $t = 1$, the position vector of the point on the curve is:

$$
\mathbf{r}_0 = (3(1) - 1^3)\mathbf{i} + 3(1)^2\mathbf{j} + (3(1) + 1^3)\mathbf{k} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} + 4\mathbf{k}
$$

Now calculate the first and second derivatives of $\mathbf{r}$ with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = (3 - 3t^2)\mathbf{i} + 6t\mathbf{j} + (3 + 3t^2)\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = -6t\mathbf{i} + 6\mathbf{j} + 6t\mathbf{k}
$$

Evaluate both derivatives at $t = 1$:

$$
\left.\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\right|_{t=1} = 6\mathbf{j} + 6\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\left.\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2}\right|_{t=1} = -6\mathbf{i} + 6\mathbf{j} + 6\mathbf{k}
$$

The speed $ds/dt$ at $t = 1$ is:

$$
\frac{ds}{dt} = \left| \left.\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\right|_{t=1} \right| = \sqrt{0^2 + 6^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{72} = 6\sqrt{2}
$$

The unit tangent vector $\mathbf{T}_0$ at $t = 1$ is:

$$
\mathbf{T}_0 = \frac{\left.\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\right|_{t=1}}{ds/dt} = \frac{6\mathbf{j} + 6\mathbf{k}}{6\sqrt{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\mathbf{j} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\mathbf{k}
$$

The normal vector to the normal plane is parallel to $\mathbf{T}_0$, or simply $\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}$.
We compute the binormal direction using the cross product of $\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ and $\ddot{\mathbf{r}}$ at $t=1$:

$$
\dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
0 & 6 & 6 \\
-6 & 6 & 6
\end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{i}(36 - 36) - \mathbf{j}(0 - (-36)) + \mathbf{k}(0 - (-36))
$$

$$
\dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -36\mathbf{j} + 36\mathbf{k}
$$

The binormal vector $\mathbf{B}_0$ is parallel to this cross product, so the normal vector to the osculating plane is parallel to $-\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}$.
The principal normal vector $\mathbf{N}_0$ is parallel to $\mathbf{B}_0 \times \mathbf{T}_0$:

$$
\mathbf{N}_0 \propto (-\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}) \times (\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}) = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
0 & -1 & 1 \\
0 & 1 & 1
\end{vmatrix} = -2\mathbf{i}
$$

Thus, the principal normal vector is parallel to $\mathbf{i}$.

(a) The **osculating plane** is perpendicular to $-\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}$ and passes through $(2, 3, 4)$:

$$
0(x - 2) - 1(y - 3) + 1(z - 4) = 0 \implies -y + 3 + z - 4 = 0 \implies y - z + 1 = 0
$$

(b) The **normal plane** is perpendicular to $\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}$ and passes through $(2, 3, 4)$:

$$
0(x - 2) + 1(y - 3) + 1(z - 4) = 0 \implies y + z - 7 = 0
$$

(c) The **rectifying plane** is perpendicular to $\mathbf{i}$ and passes through $(2, 3, 4)$:

$$
1(x - 2) + 0(y - 3) + 0(z - 4) = 0 \implies x = 2
$$

### Supplementary Problem 64

#### Question

Find equations for the tangent plane and normal line to the surface $4z = x^2 - y^2$ at the point $(3, 1, 2)$.

#### Solution

We define the surface equation as the level set of a function $G(x, y, z) = x^2 - y^2 - 4z = 0$.
The gradient vector is:

$$
\nabla G = 2x\mathbf{i} - 2y\mathbf{j} - 4\mathbf{k}
$$

Evaluate the gradient at the point $(3, 1, 2)$ to find the normal vector to the surface:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \nabla G|_{(3,1,2)} = 2(3)\mathbf{i} - 2(1)\mathbf{j} - 4\mathbf{k} = 6\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j} - 4\mathbf{k}
$$

Dividing by 2, we use a simpler parallel normal vector:

$$
\mathbf{n}' = 3\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j} - 2\mathbf{k}
$$

The tangent plane passes through $(3, 1, 2)$ and is perpendicular to $\mathbf{n}'$. Its equation is:

$$
3(x - 3) - 1(y - 1) - 2(z - 2) = 0
$$

$$
3x - 9 - y + 1 - 2z + 4 = 0 \implies 3x - y - 2z = 4
$$

The normal line passes through $(3, 1, 2)$ and is parallel to $\mathbf{n}' = 3\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j} - 2\mathbf{k}$.
Its equations are:

$$
\frac{x - 3}{3} = \frac{y - 1}{-1} = \frac{z - 2}{-2}
$$

Or in parametric form with parameter $t$:

$$
x = 3t + 3, \quad y = 1 - t, \quad z = 2 - 2t
$$

## Type-05: Vector Differential Equations

### Supplementary Problem 39

#### Question

Show that $\mathbf{r} = e^{-t}(\mathbf{C}_1 \cos 2t + \mathbf{C}_2 \sin 2t)$, where $\mathbf{C}_1$ and $\mathbf{C}_2$ are constant vectors, is a solution of the differential equation $\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} + 2\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} + 5\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{0}$.

#### Solution

We are given:

$$
\mathbf{r} = e^{-t}(\mathbf{C}_1 \cos 2t + \mathbf{C}_2 \sin 2t)
$$

Differentiate once with respect to $t$ using the product rule:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = -e^{-t}(\mathbf{C}_1 \cos 2t + \mathbf{C}_2 \sin 2t) + e^{-t}(-2\mathbf{C}_1 \sin 2t + 2\mathbf{C}_2 \cos 2t)
$$

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = e^{-t} \left[ (-\mathbf{C}_1 + 2\mathbf{C}_2)\cos 2t + (-2\mathbf{C}_1 - \mathbf{C}_2)\sin 2t \right]
$$

Differentiate again to find the second derivative:

$$
\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = -e^{-t} \left[ (-\mathbf{C}_1 + 2\mathbf{C}_2)\cos 2t + (-2\mathbf{C}_1 - \mathbf{C}_2)\sin 2t \right] + e^{-t} \left[ -2(-\mathbf{C}_1 + 2\mathbf{C}_2)\sin 2t + 2(-2\mathbf{C}_1 - \mathbf{C}_2)\cos 2t \right]
$$

$$
\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = e^{-t} \left[ (\mathbf{C}_1 - 2\mathbf{C}_2 - 4\mathbf{C}_1 - 2\mathbf{C}_2)\cos 2t + (2\mathbf{C}_1 + \mathbf{C}_2 + 2\mathbf{C}_1 - 4\mathbf{C}_2)\sin 2t \right]
$$

$$
\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = e^{-t} \left[ (-3\mathbf{C}_1 - 4\mathbf{C}_2)\cos 2t + (4\mathbf{C}_1 - 3\mathbf{C}_2)\sin 2t \right]
$$

Now substitute the expressions into the left-hand side of the differential equation:

$$
\text{LHS} = \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} + 2\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} + 5\mathbf{r}
$$

$$
\text{LHS} = e^{-t} \left[ (-3\mathbf{C}_1 - 4\mathbf{C}_2)\cos 2t + (4\mathbf{C}_1 - 3\mathbf{C}_2)\sin 2t \right] + 2e^{-t} \left[ (-\mathbf{C}_1 + 2\mathbf{C}_2)\cos 2t + (-2\mathbf{C}_1 - \mathbf{C}_2)\sin 2t \right] + 5e^{-t} \left[ \mathbf{C}_1 \cos 2t + \mathbf{C}_2 \sin 2t \right]
$$

Combine coefficient terms for $\cos 2t$:

$$
(-3\mathbf{C}_1 - 4\mathbf{C}_2) + 2(-\mathbf{C}_1 + 2\mathbf{C}_2) + 5\mathbf{C}_1 = (-3 - 2 + 5)\mathbf{C}_1 + (-4 + 4)\mathbf{C}_2 = \mathbf{0}
$$

Combine coefficient terms for $\sin 2t$:

$$
(4\mathbf{C}_1 - 3\mathbf{C}_2) + 2(-2\mathbf{C}_1 - \mathbf{C}_2) + 5\mathbf{C}_2 = (4 - 4)\mathbf{C}_1 + (-3 - 2 + 5)\mathbf{C}_2 = \mathbf{0}
$$

So, the equation simplifies to:

$$
\text{LHS} = e^{-t} (\mathbf{0}\cos 2t + \mathbf{0}\sin 2t) = \mathbf{0} = \text{RHS}
$$

This confirms that the given vector function $\mathbf{r}$ is a solution.

### Supplementary Problem 38

#### Question

If $\frac{d^2\mathbf{A}}{dt^2} = 6t\mathbf{i} - 24t^2\mathbf{j} + 4\sin t \mathbf{k}$, find $\mathbf{A}$ given that $\mathbf{A} = 2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}$ and $\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = -\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{k}$ at $t = 0$.

#### Solution

Integrate the second derivative vector once with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = \int (6t\mathbf{i} - 24t^2\mathbf{j} + 4\sin t \mathbf{k}) dt = 3t^2\mathbf{i} - 8t^3\mathbf{j} - 4\cos t \mathbf{k} + \mathbf{C}_1
$$

where $\mathbf{C}_1$ is a constant vector.

Use the initial condition $\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = -\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{k}$ at $t = 0$:

$$
-\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{k} = 3(0)^2\mathbf{i} - 8(0)^3\mathbf{j} - 4\cos 0 \mathbf{k} + \mathbf{C}_1
$$

$$
-\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{k} = -4\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{C}_1 \implies \mathbf{C}_1 = -\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{k}
$$

Substitute $\mathbf{C}_1$ back into the first derivative equation:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = 3t^2\mathbf{i} - 8t^3\mathbf{j} - 4\cos t \mathbf{k} - \mathbf{i} + \mathbf{k} = (3t^2 - 1)\mathbf{i} - 8t^3\mathbf{j} + (1 - 4\cos t)\mathbf{k}
$$

Integrate once more with respect to $t$:

$$
\mathbf{A} = \int \left[ (3t^2 - 1)\mathbf{i} - 8t^3\mathbf{j} + (1 - 4\cos t)\mathbf{k} \right] dt = (t^3 - t)\mathbf{i} - 2t^4\mathbf{j} + (t - 4\sin t)\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{C}_2
$$

where $\mathbf{C}_2$ is a constant vector.

Use the initial condition $\mathbf{A} = 2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}$ at $t = 0$:

$$
2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} = (0^3 - 0)\mathbf{i} - 2(0)^4\mathbf{j} + (0 - 4\sin 0)\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{C}_2 \implies \mathbf{C}_2 = 2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}
$$

Substitute $\mathbf{C}_2$ back into the expression for $\mathbf{A}$:

$$
\mathbf{A} = (t^3 - t)\mathbf{i} - 2t^4\mathbf{j} + (t - 4\sin t)\mathbf{k} + 2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}
$$

$$
\mathbf{A} = (t^3 - t + 2)\mathbf{i} + (1 - 2t^4)\mathbf{j} + (t - 4\sin t)\mathbf{k}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 40

#### Question

Show that the general solution of the differential equation $\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} + 2\alpha\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} + \omega^2\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{0}$, where $\alpha$ and $\omega$ are constants, is:  
(a) $\mathbf{r} = e^{-\alpha t}\left(\mathbf{C}_1 e^{\sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega^2}t} + \mathbf{C}_2 e^{-\sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega^2}t}\right)$ if $\alpha^2 - \omega^2 > 0$  
(b) $\mathbf{r} = e^{-\alpha t}\left(\mathbf{C}_1 \sin \sqrt{\omega^2 - \alpha^2}t + \mathbf{C}_2 \cos \sqrt{\omega^2 - \alpha^2}t\right)$ if $\alpha^2 - \omega^2 < 0$  
(c) $\mathbf{r} = e^{-\alpha t}(\mathbf{C}_1 + \mathbf{C}_2 t)$ if $\alpha^2 - \omega^2 = 0$,  
where $\mathbf{C}_1$ and $\mathbf{C}_2$ are arbitrary constant vectors.

#### Solution

Let the vector function be written component-wise as $\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\mathbf{i} + y(t)\mathbf{j} + z(t)\mathbf{k}$.
Substituting this definition into the vector differential equation yields three independent linear scalar differential equations of the same form:

$$
\frac{d^2u}{dt^2} + 2\alpha\frac{du}{dt} + \omega^2u = 0
$$

where $u$ represents $x$, $y$, or $z$.

The auxiliary algebraic equation is:

$$
m^2 + 2\alpha m + \omega^2 = 0
$$

Its roots are:

$$
m = \frac{-2\alpha \pm \sqrt{4\alpha^2 - 4\omega^2}}{2} = -\alpha \pm \sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega^2}
$$

We analyze the three cases based on the sign of the discriminant:

**Case (a): $\alpha^2 - \omega^2 > 0$**

The roots are real and distinct:

$$
m_1 = -\alpha + \sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega^2}, \quad m_2 = -\alpha - \sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega^2}
$$

The general solutions for $x$, $y$, and $z$ are:

$$
x(t) = c_{1x}e^{m_1 t} + c_{2x}e^{m_2 t}
$$

$$
y(t) = c_{1y}e^{m_1 t} + c_{2y}e^{m_2 t}
$$

$$
z(t) = c_{1z}e^{m_1 t} + c_{2z}e^{m_2 t}
$$

Reconstructing the vector solution $\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\mathbf{i} + y(t)\mathbf{j} + z(t)\mathbf{k}$:

$$
\mathbf{r}(t) = (c_{1x}\mathbf{i} + c_{1y}\mathbf{j} + c_{1z}\mathbf{k})e^{m_1 t} + (c_{2x}\mathbf{i} + c_{2y}\mathbf{j} + c_{2z}\mathbf{k})e^{m_2 t}
$$

Let $\mathbf{C}_1 = c_{1x}\mathbf{i} + c_{1y}\mathbf{j} + c_{1z}\mathbf{k}$ and $\mathbf{C}_2 = c_{2x}\mathbf{i} + c_{2y}\mathbf{j} + c_{2z}\mathbf{k}$ be constant vectors. We obtain:

$$
\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{C}_1 e^{(-\alpha + \sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega^2})t} + \mathbf{C}_2 e^{(-\alpha - \sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega^2})t}
$$

$$
\mathbf{r}(t) = e^{-\alpha t} \left( \mathbf{C}_1 e^{\sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega^2}t} + \mathbf{C}_2 e^{-\sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega^2}t} \right)
$$

**Case (b): $\alpha^2 - \omega^2 < 0$**

The roots are complex conjugates:

$$
m = -\alpha \pm i\sqrt{\omega^2 - \alpha^2}
$$

The general solutions for $x$, $y$, and $z$ are:

$$
x(t) = e^{-\alpha t} \left( c_{1x} \sin \sqrt{\omega^2 - \alpha^2}t + c_{2x} \cos \sqrt{\omega^2 - \alpha^2}t \right)
$$

$$
y(t) = e^{-\alpha t} \left( c_{1y} \sin \sqrt{\omega^2 - \alpha^2}t + c_{2y} \cos \sqrt{\omega^2 - \alpha^2}t \right)
$$

$$
z(t) = e^{-\alpha t} \left( c_{1z} \sin \sqrt{\omega^2 - \alpha^2}t + c_{2z} \cos \sqrt{\omega^2 - \alpha^2}t \right)
$$

Reconstructing the vector solution using constant vectors $\mathbf{C}_1$ and $\mathbf{C}_2$:

$$
\mathbf{r}(t) = e^{-\alpha t} \left( \mathbf{C}_1 \sin \sqrt{\omega^2 - \alpha^2}t + \mathbf{C}_2 \cos \sqrt{\omega^2 - \alpha^2}t \right)
$$

**Case (c): $\alpha^2 - \omega^2 = 0$**

The roots are real and equal:

$$
m_1 = m_2 = -\alpha
$$

The general solutions for $x$, $y$, and $z$ are:

$$
x(t) = e^{-\alpha t} (c_{1x} + c_{2x} t)
$$

$$
y(t) = e^{-\alpha t} (c_{1y} + c_{2y} t)
$$

$$
z(t) = e^{-\alpha t} (c_{1z} + c_{2z} t)
$$

Reconstructing the vector solution using constant vectors $\mathbf{C}_1$ and $\mathbf{C}_2$:

$$
\mathbf{r}(t) = e^{-\alpha t} (\mathbf{C}_1 + \mathbf{C}_2 t)
$$

This completes the derivation.
