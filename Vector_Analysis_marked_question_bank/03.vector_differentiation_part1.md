## Type-01: Velocity, Acceleration, and Particle Motion (Mechanics)

### Solved Problem 3

#### Question

A particle moves along a curve whose parametric equations are $x = e^{-t}$, $y = 2\cos 3t$, $z = 2\sin 3t$, where $t$ is the time. 
(a) Find the velocity and acceleration at any time.
(b) Find the magnitudes of the velocity and acceleration at $t = 0$.

#### Solution

(a) The position vector of the particle at any time $t$ is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = e^{-t}\mathbf{i} + 2\cos 3t\mathbf{j} + 2\sin 3t\mathbf{k}
$$

The velocity vector $\mathbf{v}$ is the first derivative of the position vector with respect to $t$:

$$
\mathbf{v} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = -e^{-t}\mathbf{i} - 6\sin 3t\mathbf{j} + 6\cos 3t\mathbf{k}
$$

The acceleration vector $\mathbf{a}$ is the second derivative of the position vector with respect to $t$:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = e^{-t}\mathbf{i} - 18\cos 3t\mathbf{j} - 18\sin 3t\mathbf{k}
$$

(b) Substitute $t = 0$ into the velocity and acceleration vectors:

$$
\mathbf{v} = -e^0\mathbf{i} - 6\sin 0\mathbf{j} + 6\cos 0\mathbf{k} = -\mathbf{i} + 6\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\mathbf{a} = e^0\mathbf{i} - 18\cos 0\mathbf{j} - 18\sin 0\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{i} - 18\mathbf{j}
$$

Now calculate the magnitude of the velocity vector at $t = 0$:

$$
|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{1 + 36} = \sqrt{37}
$$

Calculate the magnitude of the acceleration vector at $t = 0$:

$$
|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{1^2 + (-18)^2} = \sqrt{1 + 324} = \sqrt{325} = 5\sqrt{13}
$$

### Solved Problem 4

#### Question

A particle moves along the curve $x = 2t^2$, $y = t^2 - 4t$, $z = 3t - 5$, where $t$ is the time. Find the components of its velocity and acceleration at time $t = 1$ in the direction:

$$
\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}
$$

#### Solution

The position vector of the particle is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = 2t^2\mathbf{i} + (t^2 - 4t)\mathbf{j} + (3t - 5)\mathbf{k}
$$

Differentiate with respect to $t$ to find the velocity vector $\mathbf{v}$:

$$
\mathbf{v} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = 4t\mathbf{i} + (2t - 4)\mathbf{j} + 3\mathbf{k}
$$

Differentiate again to find the acceleration vector $\mathbf{a}$:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = 4\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j}
$$

Evaluate both vectors at $t = 1$:

$$
\mathbf{v} = 4\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j} + 3\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\mathbf{a} = 4\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j}
$$

We find the unit vector $\mathbf{u}$ in the direction of vector $\mathbf{d}$ by dividing it by its magnitude:

$$
\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{d}}{|\mathbf{d}|} = \frac{\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{1^2 + (-3)^2 + 2^2}} = \frac{\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{14}}
$$

The component of velocity in the direction of $\mathbf{d}$ is the dot product of $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{u}$:

$$
\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{u} = (4\mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j} + 3\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left( \frac{\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{14}} \right) = \frac{(4)(1) + (-2)(-3) + (3)(2)}{\sqrt{14}} = \frac{16}{\sqrt{14}}
$$

The component of acceleration in the direction of $\mathbf{d}$ is the dot product of $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{u}$:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{u} = (4\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j}) \cdot \left( \frac{\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{14}} \right) = \frac{(4)(1) + (2)(-3) + (0)(2)}{\sqrt{14}} = -\frac{2}{\sqrt{14}}
$$

### Solved Problem 12

#### Question

A particle moves so that its position vector is given by:

$$
\mathbf{r} = \cos \omega t \mathbf{i} + \sin \omega t \mathbf{j}
$$

where $\omega$ is constant. Show that:
(a) the velocity $\mathbf{v}$ of the particle is perpendicular to $\mathbf{r}$,
(b) the acceleration $\mathbf{a}$ is directed toward the origin and has magnitude proportional to the distance,
(c) $\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{v} = \text{constant vector}$.

#### Solution

(a) Differentiate the position vector $\mathbf{r}$ with respect to $t$ to find the velocity vector $\mathbf{v}$:

$$
\mathbf{v} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = -\omega \sin \omega t \mathbf{i} + \omega \cos \omega t \mathbf{j}
$$

We calculate the dot product of $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{v}$:

$$
\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{v} = (\cos \omega t \mathbf{i} + \sin \omega t \mathbf{j}) \cdot (-\omega \sin \omega t \mathbf{i} + \omega \cos \omega t \mathbf{j})
$$

$$
\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{v} = -\omega \sin \omega t \cos \omega t + \omega \sin \omega t \cos \omega t = 0
$$

Since the dot product is zero, the velocity vector $\mathbf{v}$ is perpendicular to $\mathbf{r}$.

(b) Differentiate the velocity vector $\mathbf{v}$ with respect to $t$ to find the acceleration vector $\mathbf{a}$:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = -\omega^2 \cos \omega t \mathbf{i} - \omega^2 \sin \omega t \mathbf{j}
$$

Factor out the scalar term $-\omega^2$:

$$
\mathbf{a} = -\omega^2 (\cos \omega t \mathbf{i} + \sin \omega t \mathbf{j}) = -\omega^2 \mathbf{r}
$$

Since $\mathbf{a}$ is a negative scalar multiple of the position vector $\mathbf{r}$, it is directed toward the origin.
Its magnitude is:

$$
|\mathbf{a}| = |-\omega^2 \mathbf{r}| = \omega^2 |\mathbf{r}|
$$

which is directly proportional to the distance $|\mathbf{r}|$ from the origin.

(c) We calculate the cross product of the position and velocity vectors:

$$
\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{v} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\cos \omega t & \sin \omega t & 0 \\
-\omega \sin \omega t & \omega \cos \omega t & 0
\end{vmatrix}
$$

$$
\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{k} \left[ (\cos \omega t)(\omega \cos \omega t) - (\sin \omega t)(-\omega \sin \omega t) \right]
$$

$$
\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{v} = \omega (\cos^2 \omega t + \sin^2 \omega t)\mathbf{k} = \omega\mathbf{k}
$$

Since $\omega$ is constant and the unit vector $\mathbf{k}$ is constant, the cross product is a constant vector.

### Supplementary Problem 32

#### Question

Find the velocity and acceleration of a particle which moves along the curve $x = 2 \sin 3t$, $y = 2 \cos 3t$, $z = 8t$ at any time $t > 0$. Find the magnitude of the velocity and acceleration.

#### Solution

The position vector of the particle is:

$$
\mathbf{r} = 2 \sin 3t \mathbf{i} + 2 \cos 3t \mathbf{j} + 8t\mathbf{k}
$$

Differentiate the position vector once with respect to $t$ to find the velocity vector $\mathbf{v}$:

$$
\mathbf{v} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = 6 \cos 3t \mathbf{i} - 6 \sin 3t \mathbf{j} + 8\mathbf{k}
$$

Differentiate the velocity vector with respect to $t$ to find the acceleration vector $\mathbf{a}$:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = -18 \sin 3t \mathbf{i} - 18 \cos 3t \mathbf{j}
$$

Now calculate the magnitude of the velocity vector:

$$
|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{(6 \cos 3t)^2 + (-6 \sin 3t)^2 + 8^2}
$$

$$
|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{36\cos^2 3t + 36\sin^2 3t + 64} = \sqrt{36(\cos^2 3t + \sin^2 3t) + 64}
$$

$$
|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{36(1) + 64} = \sqrt{100} = 10
$$

Calculate the magnitude of the acceleration vector:

$$
|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{(-18 \sin 3t)^2 + (-18 \cos 3t)^2}
$$

$$
|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{324\sin^2 3t + 324\cos^2 3t} = \sqrt{324(1)} = 18
$$

### Supplementary Problem 66

#### Question

A particle moves along the curve $\mathbf{r} = (t^3 - 4t)\mathbf{i} + (t^2 + 4t)\mathbf{j} + (8t^2 - 3t^3)\mathbf{k}$, where $t$ is the time. Find the magnitudes of the tangential and normal components of its acceleration when $t = 2$.

#### Solution

Differentiate the position vector $\mathbf{r}$ to find the velocity vector $\mathbf{v}$:

$$
\mathbf{v} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = (3t^2 - 4)\mathbf{i} + (2t + 4)\mathbf{j} + (16t - 9t^2)\mathbf{k}
$$

Evaluate the velocity vector at $t = 2$:

$$
\mathbf{v} = (3(2)^2 - 4)\mathbf{i} + (2(2) + 4)\mathbf{j} + (16(2) - 9(2)^2)\mathbf{k} = 8\mathbf{i} + 8\mathbf{j} - 4\mathbf{k}
$$

Calculate the speed $v$ (the magnitude of the velocity vector) at $t = 2$:

$$
v = |\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 8^2 + (-4)^2} = \sqrt{64 + 64 + 16} = \sqrt{144} = 12
$$

Differentiate the velocity vector to find the acceleration vector $\mathbf{a}$:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = 6t\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} + (16 - 18t)\mathbf{k}
$$

Evaluate the acceleration vector at $t = 2$:

$$
\mathbf{a} = 6(2)\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} + (16 - 18(2))\mathbf{k} = 12\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} - 20\mathbf{k}
$$

The unit tangent vector $\mathbf{T}$ at $t = 2$ is:

$$
\mathbf{T} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|} = \frac{8\mathbf{i} + 8\mathbf{j} - 4\mathbf{k}}{12} = \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} - \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{k}
$$

The tangential component of the acceleration $a_T$ is the projection of $\mathbf{a}$ along $\mathbf{T}$:

$$
a_T = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{T} = (12\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} - 20\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left( \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} - \frac{1}{3}\mathbf{k} \right)
$$

$$
a_T = (12)\left(\frac{2}{3}\right) + (2)\left(\frac{2}{3}\right) + (-20)\left(-\frac{1}{3}\right) = 8 + \frac{4}{3} + \frac{20}{3} = 8 + 8 = 16
$$

The total acceleration magnitude squared satisfies $|\mathbf{a}|^2 = a_T^2 + a_N^2$.
First, calculate the square of the magnitude of the acceleration vector:

$$
|\mathbf{a}|^2 = 12^2 + 2^2 + (-20)^2 = 144 + 4 + 400 = 548
$$

Now calculate the normal component of the acceleration $a_N$:

$$
a_N^2 = |\mathbf{a}|^2 - a_T^2 = 548 - 16^2 = 548 - 256 = 292
$$

$$
a_N = \sqrt{292} = 2\sqrt{73}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 68

#### Question

An object is attracted to a fixed point $O$ with a force $\mathbf{F} = f(r)\mathbf{r}$, called a *central force*, where $\mathbf{r}$ is the position vector of the object relative to $O$. Show that $\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{h}$ where $\mathbf{h}$ is a constant vector. Prove that the angular momentum is constant.

#### Solution

By Newton's second law, the net force acting on a particle of constant mass $m$ is:

$$
\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a} = m\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt}
$$

Since the force is a central force $\mathbf{F} = f(r)\mathbf{r}$, we have:

$$
m\mathbf{a} = f(r)\mathbf{r} \implies \mathbf{a} = \frac{f(r)}{m}\mathbf{r}
$$

This shows that the acceleration vector $\mathbf{a}$ is parallel to the position vector $\mathbf{r}$.
Now, consider the time derivative of the cross product of the position and velocity vectors:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}(\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{v}) = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \times \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt}
$$

Since the derivative of the position vector is the velocity vector ($\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \mathbf{v}$), the first term is:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}
$$

Since the derivative of the velocity vector is the acceleration vector ($\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = \mathbf{a}$), the second term is:

$$
\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{r} \times \left( \frac{f(r)}{m}\mathbf{r} \right) = \frac{f(r)}{m}(\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{r}) = \mathbf{0}
$$

Since both terms are zero:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}(\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{0}
$$

Integrating both sides with respect to $t$:

$$
\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{h}
$$

where $\mathbf{h}$ is a constant vector.

The angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ is defined as:

$$
\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times (m\mathbf{v}) = m(\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{v})
$$

Since $m$ is constant and $\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{h}$ is a constant vector:

$$
\mathbf{L} = m\mathbf{h} = \text{constant vector}
$$

This completes the proof.

## Type-02: Vector Magnitude Differentiation Proofs

### Solved Problem 9

#### Question

If $\mathbf{A}$ has a constant magnitude show that $\mathbf{A}$ and $\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}$ are perpendicular provided $\left|\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}\right| \neq 0$.

#### Solution

Let the constant magnitude of vector $\mathbf{A}$ be $|\mathbf{A}| = c$ (where $c$ is a constant).
The dot product of vector $\mathbf{A}$ with itself gives the square of its magnitude:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{A} = |\mathbf{A}|^2 = c^2
$$

Differentiate both sides of this equation with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d}{dt} (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{A}) = \frac{d}{dt} (c^2)
$$

Using the product rule for vector dot products:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} + \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0
$$

Since the dot product is commutative ($\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{a}$):

$$
2 \left( \mathbf{A} \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} \right) = 0 \implies \mathbf{A} \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = 0
$$

The dot product of $\mathbf{A}$ and $\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}$ is zero. Since we are given that $\left|\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}\right| \neq 0$ and the magnitude of $\mathbf{A}$ is a non-zero constant, the two vectors must be perpendicular.

### Solved Problem 14

#### Question

Show that $\mathbf{A} \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = A\frac{dA}{dt}$ where $A$ is the magnitude of vector $\mathbf{A}$.

#### Solution

Let the magnitude of vector $\mathbf{A}$ be $A$. We can write:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{A} = A^2
$$

Differentiate both sides of this equation with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d}{dt} (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{A}) = \frac{d}{dt} (A^2)
$$

The derivative of the left side using the product rule is:

$$
\frac{d}{dt} (\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{A}) = \mathbf{A} \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} + \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} \cdot \mathbf{A} = 2\left(\mathbf{A} \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}\right)
$$

The derivative of the right side using the chain rule is:

$$
\frac{d}{dt} (A^2) = 2A\frac{dA}{dt}
$$

Equating these two derivatives gives:

$$
2\left(\mathbf{A} \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}\right) = 2A\frac{dA}{dt}
$$

Dividing both sides by 2 yields the desired relation:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = A\frac{dA}{dt}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 67

#### Question

If a particle has velocity $\mathbf{v}$ and acceleration $\mathbf{a}$ along a space curve, prove that the radius of curvature of its path is given numerically by:

$$
\rho = \frac{v^3}{|\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{a}|}
$$

#### Solution

We express the velocity vector $\mathbf{v}$ in terms of its speed $v = ds/dt$ and the unit tangent vector $\mathbf{T}$:

$$
\mathbf{v} = v\mathbf{T}
$$

Differentiate with respect to time $t$ to find the acceleration vector $\mathbf{a}$:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = \frac{dv}{dt}\mathbf{T} + v\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{dt}
$$

Using the chain rule, we express the derivative of the unit tangent vector with respect to $t$:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{dt} = \frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds}\frac{ds}{dt} = v\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds}
$$

By the Frenet-Serret formulas, $\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds} = \kappa\mathbf{N} = \frac{1}{\rho}\mathbf{N}$ where $\mathbf{N}$ is the unit normal vector. Thus:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{dt} = \frac{v}{\rho}\mathbf{N}
$$

Substitute this back into the expression for acceleration:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \frac{dv}{dt}\mathbf{T} + \frac{v^2}{\rho}\mathbf{N}
$$

Now calculate the cross product of the velocity and acceleration vectors:

$$
\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{a} = (v\mathbf{T}) \times \left( \frac{dv}{dt}\mathbf{T} + \frac{v^2}{\rho}\mathbf{N} \right)
$$

$$
\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{a} = v\frac{dv}{dt}(\mathbf{T} \times \mathbf{T}) + \frac{v^3}{\rho}(\mathbf{T} \times \mathbf{N})
$$

Since the cross product of any vector with itself is zero ($\mathbf{T} \times \mathbf{T} = \mathbf{0}$) and the cross product of the tangent and normal vectors is the unit binormal vector ($\mathbf{T} \times \mathbf{N} = \mathbf{B}$):

$$
\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{a} = \frac{v^3}{\rho}\mathbf{B}
$$

Take the magnitude of both sides of the equation. Since $\mathbf{B}$ is a unit vector ($|\mathbf{B}| = 1$):

$$
|\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{a}| = \frac{v^3}{\rho}|\mathbf{B}| = \frac{v^3}{\rho}
$$

Solving for the radius of curvature $\rho$:

$$
\rho = \frac{v^3}{|\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{a}|}
$$

This completes the proof.
