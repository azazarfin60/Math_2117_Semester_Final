### Solved Problem 1

#### Question

If $\mathbf{R}(u) = (u-u^2)\mathbf{i} + 2u^3\mathbf{j} - 3\mathbf{k}$, find:  
(a) $\int \mathbf{R}(u) du$  
(b) $\int_1^2 \mathbf{R}(u) du$

#### Solution

(a) Compute the indefinite integral of each component of $\mathbf{R}(u)$ with respect to $u$:
$$
\int \mathbf{R}(u) du = \mathbf{i} \int (u-u^2) du + \mathbf{j} \int 2u^3 du + \mathbf{k} \int -3 du
$$
$$
= \left( \frac{u^2}{2} - \frac{u^3}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + \frac{u^4}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3u\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}
$$
where $\mathbf{c} = c_1\mathbf{i} + c_2\mathbf{j} + c_3\mathbf{k}$ is a constant vector.

(b) Compute the definite integral by evaluating the antiderivative from $u = 1$ to $u = 2$:
$$
\int_1^2 \mathbf{R}(u) du = \left[ \left( \frac{u^2}{2} - \frac{u^3}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + \frac{u^4}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3u\mathbf{k} \right]\Big|_1^2
$$
$$
= \left[ \left( \frac{2^2}{2} - \frac{2^3}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + \frac{2^4}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3(2)\mathbf{k} \right] - \left[ \left( \frac{1^2}{2} - \frac{1^3}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + \frac{1^4}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3(1)\mathbf{k} \right]
$$
$$
= \left( 2 - \frac{8}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + 8\mathbf{j} - 6\mathbf{k} - \left[ \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3\mathbf{k} \right]
$$
$$
= -\frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} + 8\mathbf{j} - 6\mathbf{k} - \left( \frac{1}{6}\mathbf{i} + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3\mathbf{k} \right)
$$
$$
= \left(-\frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{6}\right)\mathbf{i} + \left(8 - \frac{1}{2}\right)\mathbf{j} + (-6 - (-3))\mathbf{k}
$$
$$
= -\frac{5}{6}\mathbf{i} + \frac{15}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3\mathbf{k}
$$

### Solved Problem 2

#### Question

The acceleration of a particle at any time $t \ge 0$ is given by:
$$
\mathbf{a} = \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = 12 \cos 2t \mathbf{i} - 8 \sin 2t \mathbf{j} + 16t \mathbf{k}
$$
If the velocity $\mathbf{v}$ and displacement $\mathbf{r}$ are zero at $t=0$, find $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{r}$ at any time.

#### Solution

Integrate the acceleration vector $\mathbf{a}$ with respect to $t$ to find the velocity $\mathbf{v}$:
$$
\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{i} \int 12 \cos 2t dt + \mathbf{j} \int -8 \sin 2t dt + \mathbf{k} \int 16t dt
$$
$$
= 6 \sin 2t \mathbf{i} + 4 \cos 2t \mathbf{j} + 8t^2 \mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}_1
$$

Apply the initial condition $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ at $t = 0$:
$$
\mathbf{0} = 6 \sin(0)\mathbf{i} + 4 \cos(0)\mathbf{j} + 8(0)^2\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}_1
$$
$$
\mathbf{0} = 4\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{c}_1 \implies \mathbf{c}_1 = -4\mathbf{j}
$$

Substitute $\mathbf{c}_1$ back into the velocity expression:
$$
\mathbf{v} = 6 \sin 2t \mathbf{i} + (4 \cos 2t - 4)\mathbf{j} + 8t^2 \mathbf{k}
$$

Integrate the velocity vector $\mathbf{v} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}$ with respect to $t$ to find the position vector $\mathbf{r}$:
$$
\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{i} \int 6 \sin 2t dt + \mathbf{j} \int (4 \cos 2t - 4) dt + \mathbf{k} \int 8t^2 dt
$$
$$
= -3 \cos 2t \mathbf{i} + (2 \sin 2t - 4t)\mathbf{j} + \frac{8}{3}t^3 \mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}_2
$$

Apply the initial condition $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{0}$ at $t = 0$:
$$
\mathbf{0} = -3 \cos(0)\mathbf{i} + (2\sin(0) - 4(0))\mathbf{j} + \frac{8}{3}(0)^3\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}_2
$$
$$
\mathbf{0} = -3\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{c}_2 \implies \mathbf{c}_2 = 3\mathbf{i}
$$

Substitute $\mathbf{c}_2$ back into the position vector expression:
$$
\mathbf{r} = (3 - 3 \cos 2t)\mathbf{i} + (2 \sin 2t - 4t)\mathbf{j} + \frac{8}{3}t^3 \mathbf{k}
$$

### Solved Problem 3

#### Question

Evaluate:
$$
\int \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{A}}{dt^2} dt
$$

#### Solution

Consider the derivative of the cross product of $\mathbf{A}$ and its first derivative:
$$
\frac{d}{dt} \left( \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} \right) = \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{A}}{dt^2} + \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} \times \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}
$$

Since the cross product of any vector with itself is zero, $\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} \times \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = \mathbf{0}$. Therefore:
$$
\frac{d}{dt} \left( \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} \right) = \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{A}}{dt^2}
$$

Integrating both sides with respect to $t$:
$$
\int \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{A}}{dt^2} dt = \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} + \mathbf{c}
$$
where $\mathbf{c}$ is a constant vector.

### Solved Problem 4

#### Question

The equation of motion of a particle $P$ of mass $m$ is given by:
$$
m \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = f(r)\mathbf{r}_1
$$
where $\mathbf{r}$ is the position vector of $P$ measured from an origin $O$, $\mathbf{r}_1$ is a unit vector in the direction of $\mathbf{r}$, and $f(r)$ is a scalar function of the distance $r$ of $P$ from $O$.  
(a) Show that $\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \mathbf{c}$ where $\mathbf{c}$ is a constant vector.  
(b) Interpret physically the cases $f(r) < 0$ and $f(r) > 0$.  
(c) Interpret the result in (a) geometrically.  
(d) Describe how the results obtained relate to the motion of the planets in our solar system.

#### Solution

(a) Take the cross product of $\mathbf{r}$ with both sides of the equation of motion:
$$
m \mathbf{r} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = f(r) \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{r}_1
$$

Since the unit vector $\mathbf{r}_1$ is collinear with the position vector $\mathbf{r}$, their cross product is zero:
$$
\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{r}_1 = \mathbf{0}
$$

Thus, dividing by the non-zero mass $m$:
$$
\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = \mathbf{0}
$$

Now consider the time derivative of $\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}$:
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\right) = \mathbf{r} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} + \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \mathbf{0} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}
$$

Integrating both sides with respect to $t$:
$$
\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \mathbf{c}
$$
where $\mathbf{c}$ is a constant vector.

(b) If $f(r) < 0$, the force points in the direction of $-\mathbf{r}_1$ (toward the origin $O$), representing an attractive force.  
If $f(r) > 0$, the force points in the direction of $\mathbf{r}_1$ (away from the origin $O$), representing a repulsive force.  
Such a central force depends only on the distance $r$ from $O$.

(c) The quantity $\mathbf{H} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\right)$ represents the areal velocity of the particle. The result in (a) shows that the areal velocity is constant, meaning the position vector sweeps out equal areas in equal times. Since $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{c} = 0$, the position vector always lies in a plane perpendicular to the constant vector $\mathbf{c}$, meaning the motion is planar.

(d) A planet of mass $m$ revolving around the Sun of mass $M$ experiences an attractive gravitational force:
$$
m \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = -\frac{GMm}{r^2}\mathbf{r}_1
$$
By the result in (c), the planet's orbit is planar, and its position vector sweeps out equal areas in equal times (Kepler's second law of planetary motion).

### Supplementary Problem 28

#### Question

If $\mathbf{R}(t) = (3t^2-t)\mathbf{i} + (2-6t)\mathbf{j} - 4t\mathbf{k}$, find:  
(a) $\int \mathbf{R}(t) dt$  
(b) $\int_2^4 \mathbf{R}(t) dt$

#### Solution

(a) Compute the indefinite integral of each component:
$$
\int \mathbf{R}(t) dt = \mathbf{i} \int (3t^2-t) dt + \mathbf{j} \int (2-6t) dt + \mathbf{k} \int -4t dt
$$
$$
= (t^3 - t^2/2)\mathbf{i} + (2t - 3t^2)\mathbf{j} - 2t^2\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}
$$
where $\mathbf{c}$ is a constant vector.

(b) Evaluate the antiderivative at the upper and lower limits:
$$
\int_2^4 \mathbf{R}(t) dt = \left[ (t^3 - t^2/2)\mathbf{i} + (2t - 3t^2)\mathbf{j} - 2t^2\mathbf{k} \right]\Big|_2^4
$$
$$
= \left[ (4^3 - 4^2/2)\mathbf{i} + (2(4) - 3(4^2))\mathbf{j} - 2(4^2)\mathbf{k} \right] - \left[ (2^3 - 2^2/2)\mathbf{i} + (2(2) - 3(2^2))\mathbf{j} - 2(2^2)\mathbf{k} \right]
$$
$$
= (56\mathbf{i} - 40\mathbf{j} - 32\mathbf{k}) - (6\mathbf{i} - 8\mathbf{j} - 8\mathbf{k})
$$
$$
= 50\mathbf{i} - 32\mathbf{j} - 24\mathbf{k}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 30

#### Question

If $\mathbf{A}(t) = t\mathbf{i} - t^2\mathbf{j} + (t-1)\mathbf{k}$ and $\mathbf{B}(t) = 2t^2\mathbf{i} + 6t\mathbf{k}$, evaluate:  
(a) $\int_0^2 \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} dt$  
(b) $\int_0^2 \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} dt$

#### Solution

(a) Compute the dot product $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B}$:
$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = (t)(2t^2) + (-t^2)(0) + (t-1)(6t) = 2t^3 + 6t^2 - 6t
$$
Integrate this scalar expression from $t = 0$ to $t = 2$:
$$
\int_0^2 \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} dt = \int_0^2 (2t^3 + 6t^2 - 6t) dt = \left[ \frac{1}{2}t^4 + 2t^3 - 3t^2 \right]\Big|_0^2
$$
$$
= \left( \frac{1}{2}(16) + 2(8) - 3(4) \right) - 0 = 8 + 16 - 12 = 12
$$

(b) Compute the cross product $\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}$:
$$
\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
t & -t^2 & t-1 \\
2t^2 & 0 & 6t
\end{vmatrix}
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i} \left[ (-t^2)(6t) - 0 \right] - \mathbf{j} \left[ (t)(6t) - (t-1)(2t^2) \right] + \mathbf{k} \left[ 0 - (-t^2)(2t^2) \right]
$$
$$
= -6t^3\mathbf{i} - (6t^2 - 2t^3 + 2t^2)\mathbf{j} + 2t^4\mathbf{k}
$$
$$
= -6t^3\mathbf{i} - (8t^2 - 2t^3)\mathbf{j} + 2t^4\mathbf{k}
$$
Integrate this vector expression component-wise from $t = 0$ to $t = 2$:
$$
\int_0^2 \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} dt = \mathbf{i} \int_0^2 -6t^3 dt - \mathbf{j} \int_0^2 (8t^2 - 2t^3) dt + \mathbf{k} \int_0^2 2t^4 dt
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i} \left[ -\frac{3}{2}t^4 \right]\Big|_0^2 - \mathbf{j} \left[ \frac{8}{3}t^3 - \frac{1}{2}t^4 \right]\Big|_0^2 + \mathbf{k} \left[ \frac{2}{5}t^5 \right]\Big|_0^2
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i} \left(-\frac{3}{2}(16)\right) - \mathbf{j} \left( \frac{8}{3}(8) - \frac{1}{2}(16) \right) + \mathbf{k} \left( \frac{2}{5}(32) \right)
$$
$$
= -24\mathbf{i} - \frac{40}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{64}{5}\mathbf{k}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 39

#### Question

Solve the differential equation:
$$
\frac{d^2\mathbf{A}}{dt^2} - 4\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} + 3\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{0}
$$
subject to the conditions that at $t=0$, $\mathbf{A} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}$ and $\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = \mathbf{0}$.

#### Solution

This is a second-order linear homogeneous ordinary differential equation. The auxiliary equation for the components is:
$$
m^2 - 4m + 3 = 0 \implies (m-1)(m-3) = 0 \implies m = 1, \quad m = 3
$$
Thus, the general solution is:
$$
\mathbf{A}(t) = \mathbf{C}_1 e^t + \mathbf{C}_2 e^{3t}
$$
where $\mathbf{C}_1$ and $\mathbf{C}_2$ are constant vectors.
The derivative with respect to $t$ is:
$$
\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = \mathbf{C}_1 e^t + 3\mathbf{C}_2 e^{3t}
$$
Apply the initial conditions at $t=0$:
1. $\mathbf{A}(0) = \mathbf{C}_1 + \mathbf{C}_2 = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}$
2. $\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}(0) = \mathbf{C}_1 + 3\mathbf{C}_2 = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{C}_1 = -3\mathbf{C}_2$

Substitute $\mathbf{C}_1 = -3\mathbf{C}_2$ into the first equation:
$$
-3\mathbf{C}_2 + \mathbf{C}_2 = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}
$$
$$
-2\mathbf{C}_2 = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k} \implies \mathbf{C}_2 = -\frac{1}{2}(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k})
$$
Then find $\mathbf{C}_1$:
$$
\mathbf{C}_1 = -3\left[-\frac{1}{2}(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k})\right] = \frac{3}{2}(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k})
$$
Substitute $\mathbf{C}_1$ and $\mathbf{C}_2$ back into the general solution:
$$
\mathbf{A}(t) = \frac{3}{2}(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}) e^t - \frac{1}{2}(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}) e^{3t}
$$
$$
= \frac{1}{2}(3e^t - e^{3t})(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k})
$$

## Type-02: Line Integrals and Work Done (Path-Dependent Fields)

### Solved Problem 6

#### Question

If $\mathbf{A} = (3x^2+6y)\mathbf{i} - 14yz\mathbf{j} + 20xz^2\mathbf{k}$, evaluate $\int_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ from $(0,0,0)$ to $(1,1,1)$ along the following paths $C$:  
(a) $x=t$, $y=t^2$, $z=t^3$.  
(b) the straight lines from $(0,0,0)$ to $(1,0,0)$, then to $(1,1,0)$, and then to $(1,1,1)$.  
(c) the straight line joining $(0,0,0)$ and $(1,1,1)$.

#### Solution

The differential line integral is:
$$
\int_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_C (3x^2+6y) dx - 14yz dy + 20xz^2 dz
$$

(a) For the path $x=t, y=t^2, z=t^3$, the limits of integration are $t=0$ to $t=1$. The differentials are $dx = dt, dy = 2t dt, dz = 3t^2 dt$. Substitute these variables:
$$
\int_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^1 \left[ 3t^2+6t^2 \right] dt - 14(t^2)(t^3) (2t dt) + 20(t)(t^3)^2 (3t^2 dt)
$$
$$
= \int_{0}^1 (9t^2 - 28t^6 + 60t^9) dt
$$
$$
= \left[ 3t^3 - 4t^7 + 6t^{10} \right]\Big|_0^1 = 3 - 4 + 6 = 5
$$

(b) Break the path into three straight segments:
1. Segment $C_1$: $(0,0,0)$ to $(1,0,0)$. Here $y=0, z=0$, so $dy=0, dz=0$, while $x$ goes from $0$ to $1$:
$$
\int_{C_1} \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{x=0}^1 3x^2 dx = \left[ x^3 \right]\Big|_0^1 = 1
$$
2. Segment $C_2$: $(1,0,0)$ to $(1,1,0)$. Here $x=1, z=0$, so $dx=0, dz=0$, while $y$ goes from $0$ to $1$:
$$
\int_{C_2} \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{y=0}^1 -14y(0) dy = 0
$$
3. Segment $C_3$: $(1,1,0)$ to $(1,1,1)$. Here $x=1, y=1$, so $dx=0, dy=0$, while $z$ goes from $0$ to $1$:
$$
\int_{C_3} \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{z=0}^1 20(1)z^2 dz = \left[ \frac{20}{3}z^3 \right]\Big|_0^1 = \frac{20}{3}
$$
Sum the contributions of each segment:
$$
\int_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 1 + 0 + \frac{20}{3} = \frac{23}{3}
$$

(c) The straight line joining $(0,0,0)$ and $(1,1,1)$ is parameterized by $x=t, y=t, z=t$ from $t=0$ to $t=1$. The differentials are $dx = dy = dz = dt$. Substitute these variables:
$$
\int_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^1 (3t^2+6t) dt - 14(t)(t) dt + 20(t)(t)^2 dt
$$
$$
= \int_{0}^1 (20t^3 - 11t^2 + 6t) dt
$$
$$
= \left[ 5t^4 - \frac{11}{3}t^3 + 3t^2 \right]\Big|_0^1 = 5 - \frac{11}{3} + 3 = \frac{13}{3}
$$

### Solved Problem 7

#### Question

Find the total work done in moving a particle in a force field given by $\mathbf{F} = 3xy\mathbf{i} - 5z\mathbf{j} + 10x\mathbf{k}$ along the curve $x = t^2+1$, $y = 2t^2$, $z = t^3$ from $t=1$ to $t=2$.

#### Solution

The work done is:
$$
W = \int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_C 3xy dx - 5z dy + 10x dz
$$
Given the parameterizations:
$$
x = t^2+1 \implies dx = 2t dt
$$
$$
y = 2t^2 \implies dy = 4t dt
$$
$$
z = t^3 \implies dz = 3t^2 dt
$$
Substitute these expressions into the integrand:
$$
W = \int_{1}^2 3(t^2+1)(2t^2)(2t dt) - 5(t^3)(4t dt) + 10(t^2+1)(3t^2 dt)
$$
$$
= \int_1^2 \left[ 12t^3(t^2+1) - 20t^4 + 30t^2(t^2+1) \right] dt
$$
$$
= \int_1^2 \left( 12t^5 + 12t^3 - 20t^4 + 30t^4 + 30t^2 \right) dt
$$
$$
= \int_1^2 \left( 12t^5 + 10t^4 + 12t^3 + 30t^2 \right) dt
$$
$$
= \left[ 2t^6 + 2t^5 + 3t^4 + 10t^3 \right]\Big|_1^2
$$
$$
= \left[ 2(64) + 2(32) + 3(16) + 10(8) \right] - \left[ 2(1) + 2(1) + 3(1) + 10(1) \right]
$$
$$
= (128 + 64 + 48 + 80) - (2 + 2 + 3 + 10) = 320 - 17 = 303
$$

### Solved Problem 8

#### Question

If $\mathbf{F} = 3xy\mathbf{i} - y^2\mathbf{j}$, evaluate $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $C$ is the curve in the $xy$ plane, $y = 2x^2$, from $(0,0)$ to $(1,2)$.

#### Solution

Since the motion is in the $xy$ plane, $z = 0$ and $dz = 0$. The line integral is:
$$
\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_C 3xy dx - y^2 dy
$$
Substitute $y = 2x^2$ and $dy = 4x dx$ directly, with $x$ varying from $0$ to $1$:
$$
\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^1 3x(2x^2) dx - (2x^2)^2 (4x dx)
$$
$$
= \int_0^1 (6x^3 - 16x^5) dx
$$
$$
= \left[ \frac{3}{2}x^4 - \frac{8}{3}x^6 \right]\Big|_0^1
$$
$$
= \frac{3}{2} - \frac{8}{3} = \frac{9 - 16}{6} = -\frac{7}{6}
$$

### Solved Problem 9

#### Question

Find the work done in moving a particle once around a circle $C$ in the $xy$ plane, if the circle has center at the origin and radius 3 and if the force field is given by:
$$
\mathbf{F} = (2x - y + z)\mathbf{i} + (x + y - z^2)\mathbf{j} + (3x - 2y + 4z)\mathbf{k}
$$

#### Solution

Since the curve $C$ lies in the $xy$ plane, $z = 0$ and $dz = 0$. The force field simplifies to:
$$
\mathbf{F} = (2x - y)\mathbf{i} + (x + y)\mathbf{j} + (3x - 2y)\mathbf{k}
$$
The work done is:
$$
W = \oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \oint_C (2x-y) dx + (x+y) dy
$$
Parameterize the circle of radius 3 as $x = 3 \cos t, y = 3 \sin t$ where $t$ varies from $0$ to $2\pi$. The differentials are $dx = -3\sin t dt, dy = 3\cos t dt$:
$$
W = \int_{0}^{2\pi} [2(3\cos t) - 3\sin t](-3\sin t dt) + [3\cos t + 3\sin t](3\cos t dt)
$$
$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} \left( -18\cos t \sin t + 9\sin^2 t + 9\cos^2 t + 9\sin t \cos t \right) dt
$$
$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} \left( 9(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t) - 9\sin t \cos t \right) dt
$$
$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} \left( 9 - 9\sin t \cos t \right) dt
$$
$$
= \left[ 9t - \frac{9}{2}\sin^2 t \right]\Big|_0^{2\pi} = 18\pi
$$

### Solved Problem 16

#### Question

If $\phi = 2xyz^2$, $\mathbf{F} = xy\mathbf{i} - z\mathbf{j} + x^2\mathbf{k}$ and $C$ is the curve $x=t^2$, $y=2t$, $z=t^3$ from $t=0$ to $t=1$, evaluate the line integrals:  
(a) $\int_C \phi d\mathbf{r}$  
(b) $\int_C \mathbf{F} \times d\mathbf{r}$

#### Solution

(a) Express $\phi$ and $\mathbf{r}$ along the path $C$ in terms of the parameter $t$:
$$
\phi = 2(t^2)(2t)(t^3)^2 = 4t^9
$$
$$
\mathbf{r} = t^2\mathbf{i} + 2t\mathbf{j} + t^3\mathbf{k} \implies d\mathbf{r} = (2t\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} + 3t^2\mathbf{k}) dt
$$
Now set up and evaluate the integral:
$$
\int_C \phi d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^1 4t^9 (2t\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} + 3t^2\mathbf{k}) dt
$$
$$

## Type-03: Conservative Fields, Scalar Potentials, and Line Integrals (Path-Independent Fields)

### Solved Problem 10

#### Question

(a) If $\mathbf{F} = \nabla\phi$, where $\phi$ is single-valued and has continuous partial derivatives, show that the work done in moving a particle from one point $P_1 \equiv (x_1, y_1, z_1)$ to another point $P_2 \equiv (x_2, y_2, z_2)$ is independent of the path joining the two points.  
(b) Conversely, if $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ is independent of the path $C$ joining any two points, show that there exists a scalar potential $\phi$ such that $\mathbf{F} = \nabla\phi$.

#### Solution

(a) The work done in moving a particle from $P_1$ to $P_2$ along path $C$ is:
$$
\int_{P_1}^{P_2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{P_1}^{P_2} \nabla\phi \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$
$$
= \int_{P_1}^{P_2} \left( \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z}\mathbf{k} \right) \cdot (dx\mathbf{i} + dy\mathbf{j} + dz\mathbf{k})
$$
$$
= \int_{P_1}^{P_2} \left( \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y} dy + \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} dz \right)
$$
Since $d\phi = \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y} dy + \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} dz$:
$$
\int_{P_1}^{P_2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{P_1}^{P_2} d\phi = \phi(P_2) - \phi(P_1) = \phi(x_2, y_2, z_2) - \phi(x_1, y_1, z_1)
$$
The value of the line integral depends only on the coordinates of the endpoints $P_1$ and $P_2$, showing the work is path-independent.

(b) Let $\mathbf{F} = F_1\mathbf{i} + F_2\mathbf{j} + F_3\mathbf{k}$. Define $\phi(x,y,z)$ as:
$$
\phi(x,y,z) = \int_{(x_1,y_1,z_1)}^{(x,y,z)} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{(x_1,y_1,z_1)}^{(x,y,z)} F_1 dx + F_2 dy + F_3 dz
$$
Since this integral is path-independent, we have:
$$
\phi(x+\Delta x, y, z) - \phi(x,y,z) = \int_{(x,y,z)}^{(x+\Delta x,y,z)} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$
Choosing a straight-line path from $(x,y,z)$ to $(x+\Delta x,y,z)$, we have $dy = dz = 0$. Thus:
$$
\phi(x+\Delta x, y, z) - \phi(x,y,z) = \int_{x}^{x+\Delta x} F_1(x,y,z) dx
$$
By the mean value theorem for integrals, for some $\xi \in [x, x+\Delta x]$:
$$
\phi(x+\Delta x, y, z) - \phi(x,y,z) = F_1(\xi, y, z) \Delta x
$$
Dividing by $\Delta x$ and taking the limit as $\Delta x \to 0$:
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\phi(x+\Delta x, y, z) - \phi(x,y,z)}{\Delta x} = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} F_1(\xi, y, z) = F_1(x,y,z)
$$
Similarly, by choosing paths parallel to the $y$ and $z$ axes:
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y} = F_2(x,y,z), \quad \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} = F_3(x,y,z)
$$
Hence:
$$
\mathbf{F} = F_1\mathbf{i} + F_2\mathbf{j} + F_3\mathbf{k} = \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z}\mathbf{k} = \nabla\phi
$$

### Solved Problem 11

#### Question

(a) If $\mathbf{F}$ is a conservative field, prove that $\text{curl } \mathbf{F} = \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ (i.e., $\mathbf{F}$ is irrotational).  
(b) Conversely, if $\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$, prove that $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative.

#### Solution

(a) If $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative, then $\mathbf{F} = \nabla\phi$ for a scalar function $\phi$ with continuous second partial derivatives.
Computing the curl of $\mathbf{F}$:
$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \nabla \times (\nabla\phi) = \mathbf{0}
$$
This identity holds because the mixed partial derivatives are equal.

(b) If $\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$, we have:
$$
\frac{\partial F_3}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial z}, \quad \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial z} = \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial x}, \quad \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y}
$$
Define a scalar function $\phi(x,y,z)$ as the work done along the path consisting of three straight segments: $(x_1,y_1,z_1) \to (x,y_1,z_1) \to (x,y,z_1) \to (x,y,z)$:
$$
\phi(x,y,z) = \int_{x_1}^x F_1(t,y_1,z_1) dt + \int_{y_1}^y F_2(x,t,z_1) dt + \int_{z_1}^z F_3(x,y,t) dt
$$
Compute the partial derivatives of $\phi$:
1. With respect to $z$:
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} = 0 + 0 + F_3(x,y,z) = F_3(x,y,z)
$$
2. With respect to $y$:
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y} = 0 + F_2(x,y,z_1) + \int_{z_1}^z \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial y}(x,y,t) dt
$$
Substitute $\frac{\partial F_3}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial z}$:
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y} = F_2(x,y,z_1) + \int_{z_1}^z \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial z}(x,y,t) dt = F_2(x,y,z_1) + \left[ F_2(x,y,t) \right]\Big|_{z_1}^z
$$
$$
= F_2(x,y,z_1) + F_2(x,y,z) - F_2(x,y,z_1) = F_2(x,y,z)
$$
3. With respect to $x$:
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} = F_1(x,y_1,z_1) + \int_{y_1}^y \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x}(x,t,z_1) dt + \int_{z_1}^z \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial x}(x,y,t) dt
$$
Substitute $\frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y}$ and $\frac{\partial F_3}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial z}$:
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} = F_1(x,y_1,z_1) + \int_{y_1}^y \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y}(x,t,z_1) dt + \int_{z_1}^z \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial z}(x,y,t) dt
$$
$$
= F_1(x,y_1,z_1) + \left[ F_1(x,t,z_1) \right]\Big|_{y_1}^y + \left[ F_1(x,y,t) \right]\Big|_{z_1}^z
$$
$$
= F_1(x,y_1,z_1) + F_1(x,y,z_1) - F_1(x,y_1,z_1) + F_1(x,y,z) - F_1(x,y,z_1) = F_1(x,y,z)
$$
Since $\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} = F_1, \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y} = F_2, \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} = F_3$, we have $\mathbf{F} = \nabla\phi$. Thus, $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative.

### Solved Problem 12

#### Question

(a) Show that $\mathbf{F} = (2xy+z^3)\mathbf{i} + x^2\mathbf{j} + 3xz^2\mathbf{k}$ is a conservative force field.  
(b) Find the scalar potential $\phi$.  
(c) Find the work done in moving an object in this field from $(1,-2,1)$ to $(3,1,4)$.

#### Solution

(a) Verify that the curl of $\mathbf{F}$ is zero:
$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
2xy + z^3 & x^2 & 3xz^2
\end{vmatrix}
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i} \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3xz^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(x^2) \right] - \mathbf{j} \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(3xz^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(2xy+z^3) \right] + \mathbf{k} \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(2xy+z^3) \right]
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i}(0 - 0) - \mathbf{j}(3z^2 - 3z^2) + \mathbf{k}(2x - 2x) = \mathbf{0}
$$
Since $\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$, $\mathbf{F}$ is a conservative field.

(b) Find $\phi$ such that $\mathbf{F} = \nabla\phi$. This requires:
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} = 2xy + z^3 \quad \text{(1)}
$$
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y} = x^2 \quad \text{(2)}
$$
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} = 3xz^2 \quad \text{(3)}
$$
Integrate equation (1) with respect to $x$:
$$
\phi(x,y,z) = x^2y + xz^3 + f(y,z)
$$
Differentiate this with respect to $y$ and compare with equation (2):
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y} = x^2 + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x^2 \implies \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 0
$$
Thus, $f$ is a function of $z$ only: $f(y,z) = g(z)$. Update the potential function:
$$
\phi(x,y,z) = x^2y + xz^3 + g(z)
$$
Differentiate with respect to $z$ and compare with equation (3):
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} = 3xz^2 + g'(z) = 3xz^2 \implies g'(z) = 0 \implies g(z) = C
$$
Thus, the scalar potential is:
$$
\phi(x,y,z) = x^2y + xz^3 + C
$$

(c) The work done by a conservative field is the potential difference between endpoints:
$$
W = \phi(3,1,4) - \phi(1,-2,1)
$$
$$
= \left[ (3^2)(1) + (3)(4^3) \right] - \left[ (1^2)(-2) + (1)(1^3) \right]
$$
$$
= (9 + 192) - (-2 + 1) = 201 - (-1) = 202
$$

### Solved Problem 13

#### Question

Prove that if $\int_{P_1}^{P_2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ is independent of the path joining any two points $P_1$ and $P_2$ in a given region, then $\oint \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ for all closed paths in the region, and conversely.

#### Solution

Let $C$ be a simple closed curve starting at $P_1$, passing through $P_2$, and returning to $P_1$. Split $C$ into two distinct paths: path $C_1$ from $P_1$ to $P_2$, and path $C_2$ from $P_2$ back to $P_1$. The line integral around the closed loop is:
$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{C_1} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} + \int_{C_2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{C_1} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} - \int_{-C_2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$
where $-C_2$ is the path from $P_1$ to $P_2$.
If the integral is path-independent, then:
$$
\int_{C_1} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{-C_2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$
Substituting this into the closed loop equation:
$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{C_1} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} - \int_{C_1} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0
$$

Conversely, suppose $\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ for all closed paths. Choose two arbitrary paths $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ from $P_1$ to $P_2$. The path consisting of $Y_1$ followed by $-Y_2$ is a closed curve $C$. Therefore:
$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{Y_1} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} + \int_{-Y_2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{Y_1} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} - \int_{Y_2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0
$$
$$
\implies \int_{Y_1} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{Y_2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$
This proves that the line integral is independent of the path.

### Solved Problem 14

#### Question

(a) Show that a necessary and sufficient condition that $F_1 dx + F_2 dy + F_3 dz$ be an exact differential is that $\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ where $\mathbf{F} = F_1\mathbf{i} + F_2\mathbf{j} + F_3\mathbf{k}$.  
(b) Show that $(y^2z^3 \cos x - 4x^3z) dx + 2z^3y \sin x dy + (3y^2z^2 \sin x - x^4) dz$ is an exact differential of a function $\phi$, and find $\phi$.

#### Solution

(a) Let $F_1 dx + F_2 dy + F_3 dz = d\phi$. The total differential of $\phi(x,y,z)$ is:
$$
d\phi = \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y} dy + \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} dz
$$
Since $x, y, z$ are independent variables, equating the coefficients:
$$
F_1 = \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x}, \quad F_2 = \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y}, \quad F_3 = \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z}
$$
This means $\mathbf{F} = \nabla\phi$. Thus:
$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \nabla \times (\nabla\phi) = \mathbf{0}
$$
Conversely, if $\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$, then $\mathbf{F} = \nabla\phi$ by Solved Problem 11, which gives:
$$
F_1 dx + F_2 dy + F_3 dz = \nabla\phi \cdot d\mathbf{r} = d\phi
$$
which is an exact differential.

(b) Let the vector field components be:
$$
F_1 = y^2z^3 \cos x - 4x^3z, \quad F_2 = 2z^3y \sin x, \quad F_3 = 3y^2z^2 \sin x - x^4
$$
Verify that the curl is zero:
$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
y^2z^3 \cos x - 4x^3z & 2z^3y \sin x & 3y^2z^2 \sin x - x^4
\end{vmatrix}
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i} \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3y^2z^2 \sin x - x^4) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(2z^3y \sin x) \right]
$$
$$
- \mathbf{j} \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(3y^2z^2 \sin x - x^4) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(y^2z^3 \cos x - 4x^3z) \right]
$$
$$
+ \mathbf{k} \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(2z^3y \sin x) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y^2z^3 \cos x - 4x^3z) \right]
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i}\left( 6yz^2 \sin x - 6yz^2 \sin x \right) - \mathbf{j}\left( 3y^2z^2 \cos x - 4x^3 - (3y^2z^2 \cos x - 4x^3) \right)
$$
$$
+ \mathbf{k}\left( 2z^3y \cos x - 2yz^3 \cos x \right) = \mathbf{0}
$$
Since the curl is zero, it is an exact differential of a potential function $\phi$:
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} = y^2z^3 \cos x - 4x^3z \quad \text{(4)}
$$
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y} = 2z^3y \sin x \quad \text{(5)}
$$
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} = 3y^2z^2 \sin x - x^4 \quad \text{(6)}
$$
Integrate equation (5) with respect to $y$:
$$
\phi(x,y,z) = y^2z^3 \sin x + g(x,z)
$$
Differentiate with respect to $x$ and compare with equation (4):
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} = y^2z^3 \cos x + \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} = y^2z^3 \cos x - 4x^3z \implies \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} = -4x^3z
$$
Integrate with respect to $x$:
$$
g(x,z) = -x^4z + h(z) \implies \phi(x,y,z) = y^2z^3 \sin x - x^4z + h(z)
$$
Differentiate with respect to $z$ and compare with equation (6):
$$
\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} = 3y^2z^2 \sin x - x^4 + h'(z) = 3y^2z^2 \sin x - x^4 \implies h'(z) = 0 \implies h(z) = C
$$
Thus, the potential is:
$$
\phi(x,y,z) = y^2z^3 \sin x - x^4z + C
$$

### Solved Problem 15

#### Question

Let $\mathbf{F}$ be a conservative force field such that $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla\phi$. Suppose a particle of constant mass $m$ moves in this field. If $A$ and $B$ are any two points in space, prove that:
$$
\phi(A) + \frac{1}{2}mv_A^2 = \phi(B) + \frac{1}{2}mv_B^2
$$
where $v_A$ and $v_B$ are the magnitudes of the velocities of the particle at $A$ and $B$ respectively.

#### Solution

By Newton's second law of motion:
$$
\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a} = m\frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = m\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt}
$$
Take the dot product of $\mathbf{F}$ with the velocity vector $\mathbf{v} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}$:
$$
\mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{v} dt = m\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} \cdot \mathbf{v} dt = mv \cdot dv = d\left( \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \right)
$$
Integrate this expression along the path from point $A$ to point $B$:
$$
\int_A^B \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_A^B d\left( \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \right) = \frac{1}{2}mv_B^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_A^2
$$
Since the force is conservative, $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla\phi$. Thus:
$$
\int_A^B \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = -\int_A^B \nabla\phi \cdot d\mathbf{r} = -\int_A^B d\phi = \phi(A) - \phi(B)
$$
Equating the two expressions for the line integral:
$$
\phi(A) - \phi(B) = \frac{1}{2}mv_B^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_A^2
$$
Rearranging terms:
$$
\phi(A) + \frac{1}{2}mv_A^2 = \phi(B) + \frac{1}{2}mv_B^2
$$
This represents the law of conservation of mechanical energy, where $\phi$ is potential energy and $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$ is kinetic energy.

### Supplementary Problem 48

#### Question

Prove that $\mathbf{F} = r^2 \mathbf{r}$ is conservative, and find the scalar potential.

#### Solution

Let $\mathbf{r} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}$ and $r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}$. The force field is:
$$
\mathbf{F} = r^2(x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}) = (x^2+y^2+z^2)(x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k})
$$
Verify that the curl of $\mathbf{F}$ is zero:
$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
x(x^2+y^2+z^2) & y(x^2+y^2+z^2) & z(x^2+y^2+z^2)
\end{vmatrix}

$$
(\nabla \times \mathbf{F})_x = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}[z(x^2+y^2+z^2)] - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}[y(x^2+y^2+z^2)] = 2yz - 2yz = 0
$$
By symmetry, the $y$ and $z$ components are also zero. Since $\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$, $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative.

$$
\nabla \times (r^2 \mathbf{r}) = \nabla(r^2) \times \mathbf{r} + r^2(\nabla \times \mathbf{r})
$$
Since $\nabla(r^2) = 2r \nabla r = 2r \frac{\mathbf{r}}{r} = 2\mathbf{r}$, and $\nabla \times \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{0}$:
$$
\nabla \times (r^2 \mathbf{r}) = 2\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{r} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}
$$

$$
\nabla\phi(r) = \frac{d\phi}{dr}\frac{\mathbf{r}}{r}
$$
Equating to $\mathbf{F}$:
$$
\frac{d\phi}{dr}\frac{\mathbf{r}}{r} = r^2\mathbf{r} \implies \frac{d\phi}{dr} = r^3
$$
Integrate with respect to $r$:
$$
\phi(r) = \frac{r^4}{4} + C
$$

## Type-04: Surface Integrals

### Solved Problem 17

#### Question

Give a definition of $\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS$ over a surface $S$ in terms of the limit of a sum.

#### Solution

Subdivide the surface $S$ into $M$ elements of area $\Delta S_p$ where $p = 1, 2, \dots, M$. Let $P_p(x_p, y_p, z_p)$ be any point in $\Delta S_p$. Define the vector field value at this point as $\mathbf{A}_p = \mathbf{A}(x_p, y_p, z_p)$. Let $\mathbf{n}_p$ be the positive unit normal vector to the element $\Delta S_p$ at $P_p$, directed outward if the surface is closed. Form the sum of the normal components:
$$
\sum_{p=1}^M \mathbf{A}_p \cdot \mathbf{n}_p \Delta S_p
$$
Take the limit of this sum as the number of elements $M \to \infty$ such that the largest linear dimension of each element $\Delta S_p$ approaches zero. If this limit exists, it is called the surface integral of the normal component of $\mathbf{A}$ over $S$ and is written:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \lim_{M \to \infty} \sum_{p=1}^M \mathbf{A}_p \cdot \mathbf{n}_p \Delta S_p
$$

### Solved Problem 18

#### Question

Suppose that a surface $S$ has a projection $R$ on the $xy$ plane. Show that:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} \frac{dx dy}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k}|}
$$

#### Solution

The surface integral is defined as the limit of the sum:
$$
\sum_{p=1}^M \mathbf{A}_p \cdot \mathbf{n}_p \Delta S_p
$$
The projection of each surface element $\Delta S_p$ onto the $xy$ plane is a rectangular region of area $\Delta x_p \Delta y_p$. The area of the projection is related to the surface area element by the angle between the normal vector $\mathbf{n}_p$ and the unit vector $\mathbf{k}$ (perpendicular to the $xy$ plane):
$$
\Delta x_p \Delta y_p = |\mathbf{n}_p \cdot \mathbf{k}| \Delta S_p \implies \Delta S_p = \frac{\Delta x_p \Delta y_p}{|\mathbf{n}_p \cdot \mathbf{k}|}
$$
Substituting this into the sum gives:
$$
\sum_{p=1}^M \mathbf{A}_p \cdot \mathbf{n}_p \frac{\Delta x_p \Delta y_p}{|\mathbf{n}_p \cdot \mathbf{k}|}
$$
Taking the limit as $M \to \infty$ where the largest dimensions of $\Delta x_p$ and $\Delta y_p$ approach zero, the sum converges to the double integral over the projection region $R$:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} \frac{dx dy}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k}|}
$$

### Solved Problem 19

Evaluate $\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS$ where $\mathbf{A} = 18z\mathbf{i} - 12\mathbf{j} + 3y\mathbf{k}$ and $S$ is the portion of the plane $2x + 3y + 6z = 12$ located in the first octant.

$$
\nabla(2x+3y+6z) = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} + 6\mathbf{k}
$$
The unit normal vector is:
$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} + 6\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{2^2 + 3^2 + 6^2}} = \frac{2}{7}\mathbf{i} + \frac{3}{7}\mathbf{j} + \frac{6}{7}\mathbf{k}
$$
The projection of $S$ onto the $xy$ plane is the region $R$ bounded by $x=0, y=0$, and $2x+3y=12$ (with $z=0$). The projection factor is:
$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k} = \frac{6}{7} \implies \frac{dx dy}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k}|} = \frac{7}{6} dx dy
$$

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = (18z\mathbf{i} - 12\mathbf{j} + 3y\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{2}{7}\mathbf{i} + \frac{3}{7}\mathbf{j} + \frac{6}{7}\mathbf{k}\right) = \frac{36z - 36 + 18y}{7}
$$

$$
36z = 6(6z) = 6(12 - 2x - 3y) = 72 - 12x - 18y
$$
Substitute this expression back into the integrand:
$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = \frac{(72 - 12x - 18y) - 36 + 18y}{7} = \frac{36 - 12x}{7}
$$
Now set up and evaluate the double integral:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \left(\frac{36 - 12x}{7}\right) \frac{7}{6} dx dy = \iint_R (6 - 2x) dx dy
$$
The region $R$ is bounded by $x \in [0, 6]$ and $y \in [0, \frac{12-2x}{3}]$:
$$
= \int_{x=0}^6 \int_{y=0}^{(12-2x)/3} (6-2x) dy dx = \int_0^6 (6-2x) \frac{12-2x}{3} dx
$$
$$
= \frac{2}{3} \int_0^6 (3-x)(12-2x) dx = \frac{4}{3} \int_0^6 (3-x)(6-x) dx
$$
$$
= \frac{4}{3} \int_0^6 (18 - 9x + x^2) dx = \frac{4}{3} \left[ 18x - \frac{9}{2}x^2 + \frac{1}{3}x^3 \right]\Big|_0^6
$$
$$
= \frac{4}{3} \left( 18(6) - \frac{9}{2}(36) + \frac{1}{3}(216) \right) = \frac{4}{3} (108 - 162 + 72) = \frac{4}{3} (18) = 24
$$

Evaluate $\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS$ where $\mathbf{A} = z\mathbf{i} + x\mathbf{j} - 3y^2 z\mathbf{k}$ and $S$ is the surface of the cylinder $x^2 + y^2 = 16$ in the first octant bounded by $z=0$ and $z=5$.

\iint_S \phi \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \phi \mathbf{n} \frac{dx dz}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j}|}

Substitute the potential $\phi = \frac{3}{8}xyz$, unit normal $\mathbf{n} = \frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}}{4}$, and projection factor $|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j}| = \frac{y}{4}$:
$$
\iint_S \phi \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \left(\frac{3}{8}xyz\right) \left(\frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}}{4}\right) \frac{4}{y} dx dz
$$
$$
= \frac{3}{8} \iint_R xz (x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}) dx dz
$$
Substitute $y = \sqrt{16-x^2}$:
$$
= \frac{3}{8} \int_{z=0}^5 \int_{x=0}^4 \left( x^2z\mathbf{i} + xz\sqrt{16-x^2}\mathbf{j} \right) dx dz
$$
Evaluate the inner integrals with respect to $x$:
1. For the $\mathbf{i}$ component:
$$
\int_0^4 x^2z dx = z \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} \right]\Big|_0^4 = \frac{64}{3}z
$$
2. For the $\mathbf{j}$ component:
$$
\int_0^4 xz\sqrt{16-x^2} dx = z \left[ -\frac{1}{3}(16-x^2)^{3/2} \right]\Big|_0^4 = z \left( 0 - \left(-\frac{64}{3}\right) \right) = \frac{64}{3}z
$$
Substitute these results back and integrate with respect to $z$:
$$
\iint_S \phi \mathbf{n} dS = \frac{3}{8} \int_0^5 \left( \frac{64}{3}z\mathbf{i} + \frac{64}{3}z\mathbf{j} \right) dz = 8 (\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}) \int_0^5 z dz
$$
$$
= 8 (\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}) \left[ \frac{z^2}{2} \right]\Big|_0^5 = 100\mathbf{i} + 100\mathbf{j}
$$

### Solved Problem 22

#### Question

If $\mathbf{F} = y\mathbf{i} + (x - 2xz)\mathbf{j} - xy\mathbf{k}$, evaluate $\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS$ where $S$ is the surface of the hemisphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = a^2$ above the $xy$ plane.

#### Solution

First, compute the curl of $\mathbf{F}$:
$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
y & x - 2xz & -xy
\end{vmatrix}
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-xy) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(x-2xz) \right] - \mathbf{j}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-xy) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(y) \right] + \mathbf{k}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x-2xz) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y) \right]
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i}(-x - (-2x)) - \mathbf{j}(-y - 0) + \mathbf{k}(1 - 2z - 1) = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} - 2z\mathbf{k}
$$
Find the unit normal vector $\mathbf{n}$ pointing outward from the hemisphere:
$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{\nabla(x^2+y^2+z^2)}{|\nabla(x^2+y^2+z^2)|} = \frac{2x\mathbf{i} + 2y\mathbf{j} + 2z\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{4(x^2+y^2+z^2)}} = \frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}}{a}
$$
Project the hemisphere onto the $xy$ plane. The projection region $R$ is the circle $x^2+y^2 \le a^2$:
$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k} = \frac{z}{a} \implies dS = \frac{a}{z} dx dy
$$
Compute the integrand:
$$
(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n} = (x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} - 2z\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}}{a}\right) = \frac{x^2 + y^2 - 2z^2}{a}
$$
Substitute $z^2 = a^2 - x^2 - y^2$:
$$
(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n} = \frac{x^2 + y^2 - 2(a^2 - x^2 - y^2)}{a} = \frac{3(x^2+y^2) - 2a^2}{a}
$$
Set up the surface integral:
$$
\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \left(\frac{3(x^2+y^2) - 2a^2}{a}\right) \frac{a}{z} dx dy = \iint_R \frac{3(x^2+y^2) - 2a^2}{\sqrt{a^2-x^2-y^2}} dx dy
$$
Transform the double integral to polar coordinates ($x = \rho\cos\theta, y = \rho\sin\theta, dx dy = \rho d\rho d\theta$):
$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^a \frac{3\rho^2 - 2a^2}{\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2}} \rho d\rho d\theta
$$
Evaluate the inner integral:
$$
\int_0^a \frac{3\rho^2 - 2a^2}{\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2}} \rho d\rho = \int_0^a \frac{3\rho^2 - 3a^2 + a^2}{\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2}} \rho d\rho
$$
$$
= \int_0^a \left( -3\rho\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2} + \frac{a^2\rho}{\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2}} \right) d\rho
$$
$$
= \left[ (a^2-\rho^2)^{3/2} - a^2\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2} \right]\Big|_0^a
$$
$$
= \left( 0 - 0 \right) - \left( a^3 - a^3 \right) = 0
$$
Since the inner integral is zero, the surface integral is:
$$
\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = 0
$$

### Solved Problem 23

#### Question

Evaluate $\iint_S \mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS$ over:  
(a) the surface $S$ of the unit cube bounded by the coordinate planes and the planes $x=1, y=1, z=1$.  
(b) the surface of a sphere of radius $a$ with center at the origin.

#### Solution

(a) The cube surface $S$ has six faces:
1. Face $x=1$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{i}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = x = 1$. The integral over this face is:
$$
\int_0^1 \int_0^1 (1) dy dz = 1
$$
2. Face $x=0$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{i}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = -x = 0$. The integral is $0$.
3. Face $y=1$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{j}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = y = 1$. The integral over this face is:
$$
\int_0^1 \int_0^1 (1) dx dz = 1
$$
4. Face $y=0$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{j}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = -y = 0$. The integral is $0$.
5. Face $z=1$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{k}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = z = 1$. The integral over this face is:
$$
\int_0^1 \int_0^1 (1) dx dy = 1
$$
6. Face $z=0$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{k}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = -z = 0$. The integral is $0$.
Adding the contributions from all six faces:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = 1 + 0 + 1 + 0 + 1 + 0 = 3
$$

(b) For the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=a^2$, the outward unit normal at any point is:
$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}}{a} = \frac{\mathbf{r}}{a}
$$
Compute the integrand:
$$
\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{r} \cdot \frac{\mathbf{r}}{a} = \frac{r^2}{a} = \frac{a^2}{a} = a
$$
Since $a$ is constant on the surface:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_S a dS = a \iint_S dS = a (4\pi a^2) = 4\pi a^3
$$

### Solved Problem 24

#### Question

Show that $\iint_S \mathbf{n} dS = \mathbf{0}$ for any closed surface $S$.

#### Solution

Let the unit normal vector be written as:
$$
\mathbf{n} = \cos \alpha \mathbf{i} + \cos \beta \mathbf{j} + \cos \gamma \mathbf{k}
$$
where $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ are the angles $\mathbf{n}$ makes with the positive $x, y, z$ axes. The surface integral can be written:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{n} dS = \mathbf{i} \iint_S \cos \alpha dS + \mathbf{j} \iint_S \cos \beta dS + \mathbf{k} \iint_S \cos \gamma dS
$$
Consider the projection of $S$ onto the $yz$ plane. Let $R$ be this projection. Since $S$ is closed, any line parallel to the $x$ axis intersects the surface at an even number of points (typically two). The surface element $dS$ projects onto $dy dz$ such that:
$$
dy dz = |\cos \alpha| dS
$$
For the part of the surface where the normal points away from the origin ($\cos \alpha > 0$), we have $\cos \alpha dS = dy dz$. For the part where the normal points toward the origin ($\cos \alpha < 0$), we have $\cos \alpha dS = -dy dz$.
Integrating over the entire closed surface:
$$
\iint_S \cos \alpha dS = \iint_R dy dz - \iint_R dy dz = 0
$$
By projecting $S$ onto the $xz$ and $xy$ planes, we similarly find:
$$
\iint_S \cos \beta dS = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \iint_S \cos \gamma dS = 0
$$
Therefore:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{n} dS = \mathbf{0}
$$

### Solved Problem 25

#### Question

Let $S$ be a given surface which projects onto a region $R$ of the $xy$ plane. Let the equation of $S$ be $z = f(x,y)$. Show that the area of $S$ is given by:
$$
\iint_R \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^2} dx dy
$$

#### Solution

The surface area of $S$ is given by $\iint_S dS$.
Let $F(x,y,z) = z - f(x,y) = 0$. The gradient vector normal to the surface is:
$$
\nabla F = -\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} - \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}
$$
The unit normal vector is:
$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{\nabla F}{|\nabla F|} = \frac{-\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} - \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^2}}
$$
Take the dot product of $\mathbf{n}$ with the unit vector $\mathbf{k}$:
$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^2}}
$$
The projection factor relates $dS$ and $dx dy$:
$$
dS = \frac{dx dy}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k}|} = \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^2} dx dy
$$
Integrating over the projection region $R$:
$$
\text{Area} = \iint_S dS = \iint_R \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^2} dx dy
$$

### Supplementary Problem 58

#### Question

Evaluate $\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS$ for each of the following cases:  
(a) $\mathbf{A} = y\mathbf{i} + 2x\mathbf{j} - z\mathbf{k}$ and $S$ is the surface of the plane $2x+y=6$ in the first octant cut off by the plane $z=4$.  
(b) $\mathbf{A} = (x+y^2)\mathbf{i} - 2x\mathbf{j} + 2yz\mathbf{k}$ and $S$ is the surface of the plane $2x+y+2z=6$ in the first octant.

#### Solution

(a) Since the plane $2x+y=6$ is vertical (parallel to the $z$ axis), we project the surface $S$ onto the $xz$ plane. The projection region $R$ is bounded by $x \in [0, 3]$, $z \in [0, 4]$.
Find the unit normal vector $\mathbf{n}$:
$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{\nabla(2x+y)}{|\nabla(2x+y)|} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}}{\sqrt{2^2+1^2}} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}}{\sqrt{5}}
$$
The projection factor is:
$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} \implies dS = \frac{dx dz}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j}|} = \sqrt{5} dx dz
$$
Compute the integrand:
$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = (y\mathbf{i} + 2x\mathbf{j} - z\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}}{\sqrt{5}}\right) = \frac{2y+2x}{\sqrt{5}}
$$
Multiply the integrand by the projection factor and substitute $y = 6-2x$:
$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \frac{2y+2x}{\sqrt{5}} \sqrt{5} dx dz = (2y+2x) dx dz = (2(6-2x)+2x) dx dz = (12-2x) dx dz
$$
Set up and evaluate the integral:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \int_0^4 \int_0^3 (12-2x) dx dz = \left( \int_0^4 dz \right) \left( \int_0^3 (12-2x) dx \right)
$$
$$
= 4 \left[ 12x - x^2 \right]\Big|_0^3 = 4 (36 - 9) = 4(27) = 108
$$

(b) The plane is $2x+y+2z=6$. The unit normal vector $\mathbf{n}$ is:
$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{2^2+1^2+2^2}} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{3}
$$
Project the plane onto the $xy$ plane. The projection region $R$ is bounded by $x \in [0, 3]$, $y \in [0, 6-2x]$. The projection factor is:
$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k} = \frac{2}{3} \implies dS = \frac{dx dy}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k}|} = \frac{3}{2} dx dy
$$
Compute the integrand:
$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = ((x+y^2)\mathbf{i} - 2x\mathbf{j} + 2yz\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{3}\right) = \frac{2(x+y^2) - 2x + 4yz}{3} = \frac{2y^2 + 4yz}{3}
$$
Multiply the integrand by the projection factor:
$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \frac{2y^2 + 4yz}{3} \frac{3}{2} dx dy = (y^2 + 2yz) dx dy
$$
Express $z$ in terms of $x$ and $y$ using the plane equation $2x + y + 2z = 6 \implies 2z = 6 - 2x - y$:
$$
y^2 + 2yz = y^2 + y(6-2x-y) = y^2 + 6y - 2xy - y^2 = 6y - 2xy
$$
Set up and evaluate the double integral:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \int_0^3 \int_0^{6-2x} (6y-2xy) dy dx = \int_0^3 (6-2x) \left[ \frac{y^2}{2} \right]\Big|_0^{6-2x} dx
$$
$$
= \frac{1}{2} \int_0^3 (6-2x)^3 dx
$$
Using the substitution $u = 6-2x \implies du = -2dx$:
$$
= \frac{1}{2} \int_6^0 u^3 \left(-\frac{1}{2} du\right) = \frac{1}{4} \int_0^6 u^3 du = \frac{1}{4} \left[ \frac{u^4}{4} \right]\Big|_0^6 = \frac{1296}{16} = 81
$$

### Supplementary Problem 60

#### Question

Evaluate $\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS$ over the entire surface $S$ of the region bounded by the cylinder $x^2+z^2=9$, $x=0, y=0, z=0$ and $y=8$, if $\mathbf{A} = 6z\mathbf{i} + (2x+y)\mathbf{j} - x\mathbf{k}$.

#### Solution

The closed surface $S$ bounds a wedge-like volume in the first octant. Subdivide $S$ into five distinct faces:
1. Face $S_1$ (on $x=0$ plane): Here $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{i}$, $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = -6z$. The region is a rectangle in the $yz$ plane with $y \in [0, 8]$ and $z \in [0, 3]$:
$$
\iint_{S_1} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_1 = \int_0^8 \int_0^3 -6z dz dy = 8 \left[ -3z^2 \right]\Big|_0^3 = -216
$$
2. Face $S_2$ (on $y=0$ plane): Here $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{j}$, $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = -(2x+y) = -2x$ (since $y=0$). The region is a quarter circle of radius 3 in the $xz$ plane:
$$
\iint_{S_2} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_2 = \iint_{S_2} -2x dx dz
$$
Transforming to polar coordinates ($x = \rho\cos\theta, z = \rho\sin\theta, dx dz = \rho d\rho d\theta$):
$$
= \int_0^{\pi/2} \int_0^3 -2\rho^2 \cos\theta d\rho d\theta = \left( \int_0^{\pi/2} \cos\theta d\theta \right) \left( \int_0^3 -2\rho^2 d\rho \right) = (1)\left( -18 \right) = -18
$$
3. Face $S_3$ (on $z=0$ plane): Here $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{k}$, $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = x$. The region is a rectangle in the $xy$ plane with $x \in [0, 3]$ and $y \in [0, 8]$:
$$
\iint_{S_3} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_3 = \int_0^8 \int_0^3 x dx dy = 8 \left[ \frac{x^2}{2} \right]\Big|_0^3 = 36
$$
4. Face $S_4$ (on $y=8$ plane): Here $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{j}$, $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = 2x+y = 2x+8$ (since $y=8$). The region is a quarter circle of radius 3:
$$
\iint_{S_4} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_4 = \iint_{S_4} (2x+8) dx dz = \iint_{S_4} 2x dx dz + 8 \iint_{S_4} dx dz
$$
From Face $S_2$, $\iint_{S_2} -2x dx dz = -18 \implies \iint_{S_4} 2x dx dz = 18$. The area of the quarter circle is $\frac{9\pi}{4}$:
$$
\iint_{S_4} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_4 = 18 + 8\left(\frac{9\pi}{4}\right) = 18 + 18\pi
$$
5. Face $S_5$ (on the cylinder $x^2+z^2=9$): Here the outward normal vector is:
$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{x\mathbf{i} + z\mathbf{k}}{3}
$$
The projection of this face onto the $xy$ plane is a rectangle with $x \in [0, 3]$ and $y \in [0, 8]$. The projection factor is:
$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k} = \frac{z}{3} \implies dS_5 = \frac{3}{z} dx dy
$$
Compute the integrand:
$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = (6z\mathbf{i} + (2x+y)\mathbf{j} - x\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{x\mathbf{i} + z\mathbf{k}}{3}\right) = \frac{6xz - xz}{3} = \frac{5xz}{3}
$$
Multiply the integrand by the projection factor:
$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_5 = \frac{5xz}{3} \frac{3}{z} dx dy = 5x dx dy
$$
Set up and evaluate the integral:
$$
\iint_{S_5} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_5 = \int_0^8 \int_0^3 5x dx dy = 8 \left[ \frac{5}{2}x^2 \right]\Big|_0^3 = 8\left(\frac{45}{2}\right) = 180
$$

Sum the contributions of all five faces to find the total surface integral:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_{S_1} + \iint_{S_2} + \iint_{S_3} + \iint_{S_4} + \iint_{S_5}
$$
$$
= -216 - 18 + 36 + (18 + 18\pi) + 180 = 18\pi
$$

### Solved Problem 26

#### Question

Find the volume of the region bounded by the coordinate planes and the plane $x+y+z=1$.

#### Solution

The volume is:
$$
V = \iiint_V dV = \iiint_V dz dy dx
$$
The region is bounded by $x \ge 0, y \ge 0, z \ge 0$ and $x+y+z \le 1$.
The limits of integration are:
- $z$ goes from $0$ to $1-x-y$
- $y$ goes from $0$ to $1-x$
- $x$ goes from $0$ to $1$
Set up and evaluate the integral:
$$
V = \int_{x=0}^1 \int_{y=0}^{1-x} \int_{z=0}^{1-x-y} dz dy dx = \int_{x=0}^1 \int_{y=0}^{1-x} (1-x-y) dy dx
$$
$$
= \int_0^1 \left[ (1-x)y - \frac{y^2}{2} \right]\Big|_0^{1-x} dx = \int_0^1 \left( (1-x)^2 - \frac{(1-x)^2}{2} \right) dx
$$
$$
= \frac{1}{2} \int_0^1 (1-x)^2 dx = \frac{1}{2} \left[ -\frac{(1-x)^3}{3} \right]\Big|_0^1 = \frac{1}{2}\left( 0 - \left(-\frac{1}{3}\right) \right) = \frac{1}{6}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 68

#### Question

Evaluate $\iiint_V (2x+y) dV$ where $V$ is the closed region bounded by the cylinder $z = 4-x^2$ and the planes $x=0, y=0, y=2, z=0$ in the first octant.

#### Solution

Set up the limits of integration for the region $V$ in the first octant:
- $z$ goes from $0$ to $4-x^2$
- $y$ goes from $0$ to $2$
- $x$ goes from $0$ to $2$ (since $z = 4-x^2 \ge 0 \implies x^2 \le 4 \implies x \le 2$)
Evaluate the volume integral:
$$
\iiint_V (2x+y) dV = \int_{x=0}^2 \int_{y=0}^2 \int_{z=0}^{4-x^2} (2x+y) dz dy dx
$$
$$
= \int_0^2 \int_0^2 (2x+y)(4-x^2) dy dx
$$
$$
= \int_0^2 (4-x^2) \left[ 2xy + \frac{y^2}{2} \right]\Big|_0^2 dx = \int_0^2 (4-x^2) (4x + 2) dx
$$
$$
= 2 \int_0^2 (4-x^2)(2x+1) dx = 2 \int_0^2 (8x + 4 - 2x^3 - x^2) dx
$$
$$
= 2 \left[ 4x^2 + 4x - \frac{1}{2}x^4 - \frac{1}{3}x^3 \right]\Big|_0^2
$$
$$
= 2 \left( 4(4) + 4(2) - \frac{1}{2}(16) - \frac{1}{3}(8) \right)
$$
$$
= 2 \left( 16 + 8 - 8 - \frac{8}{3} \right) = 2 \left( 16 - \frac{8}{3} \right) = 2 \left( \frac{40}{3} \right) = \frac{80}{3}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 69

#### Question

If $\mathbf{F} = (2x^2-3z)\mathbf{i} - 2xy\mathbf{j} - 4x\mathbf{k}$, evaluate:  
(a) $\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} dV$  
(b) $\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{F} dV$  
where $V$ is the closed region bounded by the planes $x=0, y=0, z=0$ and $2x+2y+z=4$.

#### Solution

(a) Find the divergence of $\mathbf{F}$:
$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(2x^2-3z) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-2xy) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(-4x)
$$
$$
= 4x - 2x + 0 = 2x
$$
Set up the limits of integration for the tetrahedron bounded by the coordinate planes and the plane $2x+2y+z=4$:
- $z$ goes from $0$ to $4-2x-2y$
- $y$ goes from $0$ to $2-x$
- $x$ goes from $0$ to $2$
Evaluate the integral:
$$
\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} dV = \int_{x=0}^2 \int_{y=0}^{2-x} \int_{z=0}^{4-2x-2y} 2x dz dy dx
$$
$$
= \int_0^2 \int_0^{2-x} 2x(4-2x-2y) dy dx = 2 \int_0^2 x \left[ (4-2x)y - y^2 \right]\Big|_0^{2-x} dx
$$
$$
= 2 \int_0^2 x \left[ 2(2-x)(2-x) - (2-x)^2 \right] dx
$$
$$
= 2 \int_0^2 x (2-x)^2 dx = 2 \int_0^2 (4x - 4x^2 + x^3) dx
$$
$$
= 2 \left[ 2x^2 - \frac{4}{3}x^3 + \frac{1}{4}x^4 \right]\Big|_0^2
$$
$$
= 2 \left( 2(4) - \frac{4}{3}(8) + \frac{1}{4}(16) \right) = 2 \left( 8 - \frac{32}{3} + 4 \right)
$$
$$
= 2 \left( 12 - \frac{32}{3} \right) = 2\left(\frac{4}{3}\right) = \frac{8}{3}
$$

(b) Find the curl of $\mathbf{F}$:
$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
2x^2-3z & -2xy & -4x
\end{vmatrix}
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-4x) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(-2xy) \right] - \mathbf{j}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-4x) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(2x^2-3z) \right]
$$
$$
+ \mathbf{k}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-2xy) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(2x^2-3z) \right]
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i}(0 - 0) - \mathbf{j}(-4 - (-3)) + \mathbf{k}(-2y - 0) = \mathbf{j} - 2y\mathbf{k}
$$
The volume integral of the curl is:
$$
\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{F} dV = \mathbf{j} \iiint_V dV - 2\mathbf{k} \iiint_V y dV
$$
The volume $V$ of the tetrahedron is:
$$
V = \frac{1}{6} (2)(2)(4) = \frac{8}{3}
$$
Evaluate the integral for the $\mathbf{k}$ component:
$$
\iiint_V y dV = \int_{x=0}^2 \int_{y=0}^{2-x} \int_{z=0}^{4-2x-2y} y dz dy dx
$$
$$
= \int_0^2 \int_0^{2-x} y(4-2x-2y) dy dx = \int_0^2 \left[ (4-2x)\frac{y^2}{2} - \frac{2}{3}y^3 \right]\Big|_0^{2-x} dx
$$
$$
= \int_0^2 (2-x)^3 \left( 1 - \frac{2}{3} \right) dx = \frac{1}{3} \int_0^2 (2-x)^3 dx
$$
Let $u = 2-x \implies du = -dx$:
$$
= \frac{1}{3} \int_0^2 u^3 du = \frac{1}{3} \left[ \frac{u^4}{4} \right]\Big|_0^2 = \frac{4}{3}
$$
Substitute these values back:
$$
\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{F} dV = \frac{8}{3}\mathbf{j} - 2\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)\mathbf{k} = \frac{8}{3}(\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k})
$$

### Supplementary Problem 70

#### Question

If $\mathbf{F} = x^2\mathbf{i} + y^2\mathbf{j} + z^2\mathbf{k}$, evaluate:  
(a) $\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} dV$  
(b) $\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{F} dV$  
where $V$ is the closed region bounded by the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=a^2$.

#### Solution

(a) Find the divergence of $\mathbf{F}$:
$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(z^2) = 2x + 2y + 2z = 2(x+y+z)
$$
By the symmetry of the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2 \le a^2$ centered at the origin:
$$
\iiint_V x dV = 0, \quad \iiint_V y dV = 0, \quad \iiint_V z dV = 0
$$
Thus:
$$
\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} dV = 2 \left( \iiint_V x dV + \iiint_V y dV + \iiint_V z dV \right) = 0
$$

(b) Find the curl of $\mathbf{F}$:
$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
x^2 & y^2 & z^2
\end{vmatrix}
$$
$$
= \mathbf{i}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(z^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(y^2) \right] - \mathbf{j}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(z^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(x^2) \right] + \mathbf{k}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(y^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2) \right] = \mathbf{0}
$$
Therefore:
$$
\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{F} dV = \mathbf{0}
$$
