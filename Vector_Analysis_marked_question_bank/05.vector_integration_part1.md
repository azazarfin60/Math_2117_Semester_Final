## Type-01: Ordinary Integrals of Vectors (Evaluations and Differential Equations)

### Solved Problem 1

#### Question

If

$$
\mathbf{R}(u) = (u-u^2)\mathbf{i} + 2u^3\mathbf{j} - 3\mathbf{k}
$$

, find:
(a) $\int \mathbf{R}(u) du$
(b) $\int_1^2 \mathbf{R}(u) du$

#### Solution

(a) Compute the indefinite integral of each component of $\mathbf{R}(u)$ with respect to $u$:

$$
\int \mathbf{R}(u) du = \mathbf{i} \int (u-u^2) du + \mathbf{j} \int 2u^3 du + \mathbf{k} \int -3 du
$$

$$
= \left( \frac{u^2}{2} - \frac{u^3}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + \frac{u^4}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3u\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}
$$

where

$$
\mathbf{c} = c_1\mathbf{i} + c_2\mathbf{j} + c_3\mathbf{k}
$$

is a constant vector.

(b) Compute the definite integral by evaluating the antiderivative from $u = 1$ to $u = 2$:

$$
\int_1^2 \mathbf{R}(u) du = \left[ \left( \frac{u^2}{2} - \frac{u^3}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + \frac{u^4}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3u\mathbf{k} \right]\Big|_1^2
$$

$$
= \left[ \left( \frac{2^2}{2} - \frac{2^3}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + \frac{2^4}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3(2)\mathbf{k} \right] - \left[ \left( \frac{1^2}{2} - \frac{1^3}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + \frac{1^4}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3(1)\mathbf{k} \right]
$$

$$
= \left( 2 - \frac{8}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + 8\mathbf{j} - 6\mathbf{k} - \left[ \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} \right)\mathbf{i} + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3\mathbf{k} \right]
$$

$$
= -\frac{2}{3}\mathbf{i} + 8\mathbf{j} - 6\mathbf{k} - \left( \frac{1}{6}\mathbf{i} + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3\mathbf{k} \right)
$$

$$
= \left(-\frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{6}\right)\mathbf{i} + \left(8 - \frac{1}{2}\right)\mathbf{j} + (-6 - (-3))\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
= -\frac{5}{6}\mathbf{i} + \frac{15}{2}\mathbf{j} - 3\mathbf{k}
$$

### Solved Problem 2

#### Question

The acceleration of a particle at any time $t \ge 0$ is given by:

$$
\mathbf{a} = \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = 12 \cos 2t \mathbf{i} - 8 \sin 2t \mathbf{j} + 16t \mathbf{k}
$$

If the velocity $\mathbf{v}$ and displacement $\mathbf{r}$ are zero at $t=0$, find $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{r}$ at any time.

#### Solution

Integrate the acceleration vector $\mathbf{a}$ with respect to $t$ to find the velocity $\mathbf{v}$:

$$
\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{i} \int 12 \cos 2t dt + \mathbf{j} \int -8 \sin 2t dt + \mathbf{k} \int 16t dt
$$

$$
= 6 \sin 2t \mathbf{i} + 4 \cos 2t \mathbf{j} + 8t^2 \mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}_1
$$

Apply the initial condition $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ at $t = 0$:

$$
\mathbf{0} = 6 \sin(0)\mathbf{i} + 4 \cos(0)\mathbf{j} + 8(0)^2\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}_1
$$

$$
\mathbf{0} = 4\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{c}_1 \implies \mathbf{c}_1 = -4\mathbf{j}
$$

Substitute $\mathbf{c}_1$ back into the velocity expression:

$$
\mathbf{v} = 6 \sin 2t \mathbf{i} + (4 \cos 2t - 4)\mathbf{j} + 8t^2 \mathbf{k}
$$

Integrate the velocity vector

$$
\mathbf{v} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}
$$

with respect to $t$ to find the position vector $\mathbf{r}$:

$$
\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{i} \int 6 \sin 2t dt + \mathbf{j} \int (4 \cos 2t - 4) dt + \mathbf{k} \int 8t^2 dt
$$

$$
= -3 \cos 2t \mathbf{i} + (2 \sin 2t - 4t)\mathbf{j} + \frac{8}{3}t^3 \mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}_2
$$

Apply the initial condition $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{0}$ at $t = 0$:

$$
\mathbf{0} = -3 \cos(0)\mathbf{i} + (2\sin(0) - 4(0))\mathbf{j} + \frac{8}{3}(0)^3\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}_2
$$

$$
\mathbf{0} = -3\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{c}_2 \implies \mathbf{c}_2 = 3\mathbf{i}
$$

Substitute $\mathbf{c}_2$ back into the position vector expression:

$$
\mathbf{r} = (3 - 3 \cos 2t)\mathbf{i} + (2 \sin 2t - 4t)\mathbf{j} + \frac{8}{3}t^3 \mathbf{k}
$$

### Solved Problem 3

#### Question

Evaluate:

$$
\int \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{A}}{dt^2} dt
$$

#### Solution

Consider the derivative of the cross product of $\mathbf{A}$ and its first derivative:

$$
\frac{d}{dt} \left( \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} \right) = \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{A}}{dt^2} + \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} \times \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}
$$

Since the cross product of any vector with itself is zero,

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} \times \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = \mathbf{0}
$$

. Therefore:

$$
\frac{d}{dt} \left( \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} \right) = \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{A}}{dt^2}
$$

Integrating both sides with respect to $t$:

$$
\int \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{A}}{dt^2} dt = \mathbf{A} \times \frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} + \mathbf{c}
$$

where $\mathbf{c}$ is a constant vector.

### Solved Problem 4

#### Question

The equation of motion of a particle $P$ of mass $m$ is given by:

$$
m \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = f(r)\mathbf{r}_1
$$

where $\mathbf{r}$ is the position vector of $P$ measured from an origin $O$, $\mathbf{r}_1$ is a unit vector in the direction of $\mathbf{r}$, and $f(r)$ is a scalar function of the distance $r$ of $P$ from $O$.
(a) Show that

$$
\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \mathbf{c}
$$

where $\mathbf{c}$ is a constant vector.
(b) Interpret physically the cases $f(r) < 0$ and $f(r) > 0$.
(c) Interpret the result in (a) geometrically.
(d) Describe how the results obtained relate to the motion of the planets in our solar system.

#### Solution

(a) Take the cross product of $\mathbf{r}$ with both sides of the equation of motion:

$$
m \mathbf{r} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = f(r) \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{r}_1
$$

Since the unit vector $\mathbf{r}_1$ is collinear with the position vector $\mathbf{r}$, their cross product is zero:

$$
\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{r}_1 = \mathbf{0}
$$

Thus, dividing by the non-zero mass $m$:

$$
\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = \mathbf{0}
$$

Now consider the time derivative of

$$
\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}:
$$

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\right) = \mathbf{r} \times \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} + \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \mathbf{0} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}
$$

Integrating both sides with respect to $t$:

$$
\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \mathbf{c}
$$

where $\mathbf{c}$ is a constant vector.

(b) If $f(r) < 0$, the force points in the direction of $-\mathbf{r}_1$ (toward the origin $O$), representing an attractive force.
If $f(r) > 0$, the force points in the direction of $\mathbf{r}_1$ (away from the origin $O$), representing a repulsive force.
Such a central force depends only on the distance $r$ from $O$.

(c) The quantity

$$
\mathbf{H} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\mathbf{r} \times \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\right)
$$

represents the areal velocity of the particle. The result in (a) shows that the areal velocity is constant, meaning the position vector sweeps out equal areas in equal times. Since $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{c} = 0$, the position vector always lies in a plane perpendicular to the constant vector $\mathbf{c}$, meaning the motion is planar.

(d) A planet of mass $m$ revolving around the Sun of mass $M$ experiences an attractive gravitational force:

$$
m \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = -\frac{GMm}{r^2}\mathbf{r}_1
$$

By the result in (c), the planet's orbit is planar, and its position vector sweeps out equal areas in equal times (Kepler's second law of planetary motion).

### Supplementary Problem 28

#### Question

If

$$
\mathbf{R}(t) = (3t^2-t)\mathbf{i} + (2-6t)\mathbf{j} - 4t\mathbf{k}
$$

, find:
(a) $\int \mathbf{R}(t) dt$
(b) $\int_2^4 \mathbf{R}(t) dt$

#### Solution

(a) Compute the indefinite integral of each component:

$$
\int \mathbf{R}(t) dt = \mathbf{i} \int (3t^2-t) dt + \mathbf{j} \int (2-6t) dt + \mathbf{k} \int -4t dt
$$

$$
= (t^3 - t^2/2)\mathbf{i} + (2t - 3t^2)\mathbf{j} - 2t^2\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{c}
$$

where $\mathbf{c}$ is a constant vector.

(b) Evaluate the antiderivative at the upper and lower limits:

$$
\int_2^4 \mathbf{R}(t) dt = \left[ (t^3 - t^2/2)\mathbf{i} + (2t - 3t^2)\mathbf{j} - 2t^2\mathbf{k} \right]\Big|_2^4
$$

$$
= \left[ (4^3 - 4^2/2)\mathbf{i} + (2(4) - 3(4^2))\mathbf{j} - 2(4^2)\mathbf{k} \right] - \left[ (2^3 - 2^2/2)\mathbf{i} + (2(2) - 3(2^2))\mathbf{j} - 2(2^2)\mathbf{k} \right]
$$

$$
= (56\mathbf{i} - 40\mathbf{j} - 32\mathbf{k}) - (6\mathbf{i} - 8\mathbf{j} - 8\mathbf{k})
$$

$$
= 50\mathbf{i} - 32\mathbf{j} - 24\mathbf{k}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 30

#### Question

If

$$
\mathbf{A}(t) = t\mathbf{i} - t^2\mathbf{j} + (t-1)\mathbf{k}
$$

and

$$
\mathbf{B}(t) = 2t^2\mathbf{i} + 6t\mathbf{k}
$$

, evaluate:
(a)

$$
\int_0^2 \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} dt
$$

(b)

$$
\int_0^2 \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} dt
$$

#### Solution

(a) Compute the dot product $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B}$:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} = (t)(2t^2) + (-t^2)(0) + (t-1)(6t) = 2t^3 + 6t^2 - 6t
$$

Integrate this scalar expression from $t = 0$ to $t = 2$:

$$
\int_0^2 \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B} dt = \int_0^2 (2t^3 + 6t^2 - 6t) dt = \left[ \frac{1}{2}t^4 + 2t^3 - 3t^2 \right]\Big|_0^2
$$

$$
= \left( \frac{1}{2}(16) + 2(8) - 3(4) \right) - 0 = 8 + 16 - 12 = 12
$$

(b) Compute the cross product $\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}$:

$$
\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
t & -t^2 & t-1 \\
2t^2 & 0 & 6t
\end{vmatrix}
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i} \left[ (-t^2)(6t) - 0 \right] - \mathbf{j} \left[ (t)(6t) - (t-1)(2t^2) \right] + \mathbf{k} \left[ 0 - (-t^2)(2t^2) \right]
$$

$$
= -6t^3\mathbf{i} - (6t^2 - 2t^3 + 2t^2)\mathbf{j} + 2t^4\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
= -6t^3\mathbf{i} - (8t^2 - 2t^3)\mathbf{j} + 2t^4\mathbf{k}
$$

Integrate this vector expression component-wise from $t = 0$ to $t = 2$:

$$
\int_0^2 \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} dt = \mathbf{i} \int_0^2 -6t^3 dt - \mathbf{j} \int_0^2 (8t^2 - 2t^3) dt + \mathbf{k} \int_0^2 2t^4 dt
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i} \left[ -\frac{3}{2}t^4 \right]\Big|_0^2 - \mathbf{j} \left[ \frac{8}{3}t^3 - \frac{1}{2}t^4 \right]\Big|_0^2 + \mathbf{k} \left[ \frac{2}{5}t^5 \right]\Big|_0^2
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i} \left(-\frac{3}{2}(16)\right) - \mathbf{j} \left( \frac{8}{3}(8) - \frac{1}{2}(16) \right) + \mathbf{k} \left( \frac{2}{5}(32) \right)
$$

$$
= -24\mathbf{i} - \frac{40}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{64}{5}\mathbf{k}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 39

#### Question

Solve the differential equation:

$$
\frac{d^2\mathbf{A}}{dt^2} - 4\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} + 3\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{0}
$$

subject to the conditions that at $t=0$, $\mathbf{A} = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}$ and

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = \mathbf{0}.
$$

#### Solution

This is a second-order linear homogeneous ordinary differential equation. The auxiliary equation for the components is:

$$
m^2 - 4m + 3 = 0 \implies (m-1)(m-3) = 0 \implies m = 1, \quad m = 3
$$

Thus, the general solution is:

$$
\mathbf{A}(t) = \mathbf{C}_1 e^t + \mathbf{C}_2 e^{3t}
$$

where $\mathbf{C}_1$ and $\mathbf{C}_2$ are constant vectors.
The derivative with respect to $t$ is:

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt} = \mathbf{C}_1 e^t + 3\mathbf{C}_2 e^{3t}
$$

Apply the initial conditions at $t=0$:
1.

$$
\mathbf{A}(0) = \mathbf{C}_1 + \mathbf{C}_2 = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}
$$

2.

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}(0) = \mathbf{C}_1 + 3\mathbf{C}_2 = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{C}_1 = -3\mathbf{C}_2
$$

Substitute

$$
\mathbf{C}_1 = -3\mathbf{C}_2
$$

into the first equation:

$$
-3\mathbf{C}_2 + \mathbf{C}_2 = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}
$$

$$
-2\mathbf{C}_2 = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k} \implies \mathbf{C}_2 = -\frac{1}{2}(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k})
$$

Then find $\mathbf{C}_1$:

$$
\mathbf{C}_1 = -3\left[-\frac{1}{2}(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k})\right] = \frac{3}{2}(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k})
$$

Substitute $\mathbf{C}_1$ and $\mathbf{C}_2$ back into the general solution:

$$
\mathbf{A}(t) = \frac{3}{2}(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}) e^t - \frac{1}{2}(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}) e^{3t}
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{2}(3e^t - e^{3t})(2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k})
$$

## Type-02: Line Integrals and Work Done (Path-Dependent Fields)

### Solved Problem 6

#### Question

If

$$
\mathbf{A} = (3x^2+6y)\mathbf{i} - 14yz\mathbf{j} + 20xz^2\mathbf{k}
$$

, evaluate

$$
\int_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$

from $(0,0,0)$ to $(1,1,1)$ along the following paths $C$:
(a) $x=t$,

$$
y=t^2,
$$

$$
z=t^3.
$$

(b) the straight lines from $(0,0,0)$ to $(1,0,0)$, then to $(1,1,0)$, and then to $(1,1,1)$.
(c) the straight line joining $(0,0,0)$ and $(1,1,1)$.

#### Solution

The differential line integral is:

$$
\int_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_C (3x^2+6y) dx - 14yz dy + 20xz^2 dz
$$

(a) For the path

$$
x=t, y=t^2, z=t^3
$$

, the limits of integration are $t=0$ to $t=1$. The differentials are

$$
dx = dt, dy = 2t dt, dz = 3t^2 dt
$$

. Substitute these variables:

$$
\int_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^1 \left[ 3t^2+6t^2 \right] dt - 14(t^2)(t^3) (2t dt) + 20(t)(t^3)^2 (3t^2 dt)
$$

$$
= \int_{0}^1 (9t^2 - 28t^6 + 60t^9) dt
$$

$$
= \left[ 3t^3 - 4t^7 + 6t^{10} \right]\Big|_0^1 = 3 - 4 + 6 = 5
$$

(b) Break the path into three straight segments:
1. Segment $C_1$: $(0,0,0)$ to $(1,0,0)$. Here $y=0, z=0$, so $dy=0, dz=0$, while $x$ goes from $0$ to $1$:

$$
\int_{C_1} \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{x=0}^1 3x^2 dx = \left[ x^3 \right]\Big|_0^1 = 1
$$

2. Segment $C_2$: $(1,0,0)$ to $(1,1,0)$. Here $x=1, z=0$, so $dx=0, dz=0$, while $y$ goes from $0$ to $1$:

$$
\int_{C_2} \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{y=0}^1 -14y(0) dy = 0
$$

3. Segment $C_3$: $(1,1,0)$ to $(1,1,1)$. Here $x=1, y=1$, so $dx=0, dy=0$, while $z$ goes from $0$ to $1$:

$$
\int_{C_3} \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{z=0}^1 20(1)z^2 dz = \left[ \frac{20}{3}z^3 \right]\Big|_0^1 = \frac{20}{3}
$$

Sum the contributions of each segment:

$$
\int_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 1 + 0 + \frac{20}{3} = \frac{23}{3}
$$

(c) The straight line joining $(0,0,0)$ and $(1,1,1)$ is parameterized by $x=t, y=t, z=t$ from $t=0$ to $t=1$. The differentials are $dx = dy = dz = dt$. Substitute these variables:

$$
\int_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^1 (3t^2+6t) dt - 14(t)(t) dt + 20(t)(t)^2 dt
$$

$$
= \int_{0}^1 (20t^3 - 11t^2 + 6t) dt
$$

$$
= \left[ 5t^4 - \frac{11}{3}t^3 + 3t^2 \right]\Big|_0^1 = 5 - \frac{11}{3} + 3 = \frac{13}{3}
$$

### Solved Problem 7

#### Question

Find the total work done in moving a particle in a force field given by $\mathbf{F} = 3xy\mathbf{i} - 5z\mathbf{j} + 10x\mathbf{k}$ along the curve

$$
x = t^2+1,
$$

$$
y = 2t^2,
$$

$$
z = t^3
$$

from $t=1$ to $t=2$.

#### Solution

The work done is:

$$
W = \int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_C 3xy dx - 5z dy + 10x dz
$$

Given the parameterizations:

$$
x = t^2+1 \implies dx = 2t dt
$$

$$
y = 2t^2 \implies dy = 4t dt
$$

$$
z = t^3 \implies dz = 3t^2 dt
$$

Substitute these expressions into the integrand:

$$
W = \int_{1}^2 3(t^2+1)(2t^2)(2t dt) - 5(t^3)(4t dt) + 10(t^2+1)(3t^2 dt)
$$

$$
= \int_1^2 \left[ 12t^3(t^2+1) - 20t^4 + 30t^2(t^2+1) \right] dt
$$

$$
= \int_1^2 \left( 12t^5 + 12t^3 - 20t^4 + 30t^4 + 30t^2 \right) dt
$$

$$
= \int_1^2 \left( 12t^5 + 10t^4 + 12t^3 + 30t^2 \right) dt
$$

$$
= \left[ 2t^6 + 2t^5 + 3t^4 + 10t^3 \right]\Big|_1^2
$$

$$
= \left[ 2(64) + 2(32) + 3(16) + 10(8) \right] - \left[ 2(1) + 2(1) + 3(1) + 10(1) \right]
$$

$$
= (128 + 64 + 48 + 80) - (2 + 2 + 3 + 10) = 320 - 17 = 303
$$

### Solved Problem 8

#### Question

If

$$
\mathbf{F} = 3xy\mathbf{i} - y^2\mathbf{j}
$$

, evaluate

$$
\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$

where $C$ is the curve in the $xy$ plane,

$$
y = 2x^2
$$

, from $(0,0)$ to $(1,2)$.

#### Solution

Since the motion is in the $xy$ plane, $z = 0$ and $dz = 0$. The line integral is:

$$
\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_C 3xy dx - y^2 dy
$$

Substitute

$$
y = 2x^2
$$

and $dy = 4x dx$ directly, with $x$ varying from $0$ to $1$:

$$
\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^1 3x(2x^2) dx - (2x^2)^2 (4x dx)
$$

$$
= \int_0^1 (6x^3 - 16x^5) dx
$$

$$
= \left[ \frac{3}{2}x^4 - \frac{8}{3}x^6 \right]\Big|_0^1
$$

$$
= \frac{3}{2} - \frac{8}{3} = \frac{9 - 16}{6} = -\frac{7}{6}
$$

### Solved Problem 9

#### Question

Find the work done in moving a particle once around a circle $C$ in the $xy$ plane, if the circle has center at the origin and radius 3 and if the force field is given by:

$$
\mathbf{F} = (2x - y + z)\mathbf{i} + (x + y - z^2)\mathbf{j} + (3x - 2y + 4z)\mathbf{k}
$$

#### Solution

Since the curve $C$ lies in the $xy$ plane, $z = 0$ and $dz = 0$. The force field simplifies to:

$$
\mathbf{F} = (2x - y)\mathbf{i} + (x + y)\mathbf{j} + (3x - 2y)\mathbf{k}
$$

The work done is:

$$
W = \oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \oint_C (2x-y) dx + (x+y) dy
$$

Parameterize the circle of radius 3 as $x = 3 \cos t, y = 3 \sin t$ where $t$ varies from $0$ to $2\pi$. The differentials are $dx = -3\sin t dt, dy = 3\cos t dt$:

$$
W = \int_{0}^{2\pi} [2(3\cos t) - 3\sin t](-3\sin t dt) + [3\cos t + 3\sin t](3\cos t dt)
$$

$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} \left( -18\cos t \sin t + 9\sin^2 t + 9\cos^2 t + 9\sin t \cos t \right) dt
$$

$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} \left( 9(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t) - 9\sin t \cos t \right) dt
$$

$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} \left( 9 - 9\sin t \cos t \right) dt
$$

$$
= \left[ 9t - \frac{9}{2}\sin^2 t \right]\Big|_0^{2\pi} = 18\pi
$$

### Solved Problem 16

#### Question

If

$$
\phi = 2xyz^2,
$$

$$
\mathbf{F} = xy\mathbf{i} - z\mathbf{j} + x^2\mathbf{k}
$$

and $C$ is the curve

$$
x=t^2
$$

, $y=2t$,

$$
z=t^3
$$

from $t=0$ to $t=1$, evaluate the line integrals:
(a) $\int_C \phi d\mathbf{r}$
(b)

$$
\int_C \mathbf{F} \times d\mathbf{r}
$$

#### Solution

(a) Express $\phi$ and $\mathbf{r}$ along the path $C$ in terms of the parameter $t$:

$$
\phi = 2(t^2)(2t)(t^3)^2 = 4t^9
$$

$$
\mathbf{r} = t^2\mathbf{i} + 2t\mathbf{j} + t^3\mathbf{k} \implies d\mathbf{r} = (2t\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} + 3t^2\mathbf{k}) dt
$$

Now set up and evaluate the integral:

$$
\int_C \phi d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^1 4t^9 (2t\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} + 3t^2\mathbf{k}) dt
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i} \int_0^1 8t^{10} dt + \mathbf{j} \int_0^1 8t^9 dt + \mathbf{k} \int_0^1 12t^{11} dt
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i} \left[ \frac{8}{11}t^{11} \right]\Big|_0^1 + \mathbf{j} \left[ \frac{4}{5}t^{10} \right]\Big|_0^1 + \mathbf{k} \left[ t^{12} \right]\Big|_0^1
$$

$$
= \frac{8}{11}\mathbf{i} + \frac{4}{5}\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}
$$

(b) Express $\mathbf{F}$ along the path $C$:

$$
\mathbf{F} = (t^2)(2t)\mathbf{i} - (t^3)\mathbf{j} + (t^2)^2\mathbf{k} = 2t^3\mathbf{i} - t^3\mathbf{j} + t^4\mathbf{k}
$$

Compute the cross product $\mathbf{F} \times d\mathbf{r}$:

$$
\mathbf{F} \times d\mathbf{r} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
2t^3 & -t^3 & t^4 \\
2t & 2 & 3t^2
\end{vmatrix} dt
$$

$$
= \left[ \mathbf{i}( -3t^5 - 2t^4 ) - \mathbf{j}( 6t^5 - 2t^5 ) + \mathbf{k}( 4t^3 - (-2t^4) ) \right] dt
$$

$$
= \left[ (-3t^5 - 2t^4)\mathbf{i} - 4t^5\mathbf{j} + (4t^3 + 2t^4)\mathbf{k} \right] dt
$$

Evaluate the integral component-wise from $t=0$ to $t=1$:

$$
\int_C \mathbf{F} \times d\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{i} \int_0^1 (-3t^5 - 2t^4) dt - \mathbf{j} \int_0^1 4t^5 dt + \mathbf{k} \int_0^1 (4t^3 + 2t^4) dt
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i} \left[ -\frac{1}{2}t^6 - \frac{2}{5}t^5 \right]\Big|_0^1 - \mathbf{j} \left[ \frac{2}{3}t^6 \right]\Big|_0^1 + \mathbf{k} \left[ t^4 + \frac{2}{5}t^5 \right]\Big|_0^1
$$

$$
= \left( -\frac{1}{2} - \frac{2}{5} \right)\mathbf{i} - \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} + \left( 1 + \frac{2}{5} \right)\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
= -\frac{9}{10}\mathbf{i} - \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{j} + \frac{7}{5}\mathbf{k}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 41

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$

where $\mathbf{F} = (x-3y)\mathbf{i} + (y-2x)\mathbf{j}$ and $C$ is the closed curve in the $xy$ plane, $x=2\cos t, y=3\sin t$ from $t=0$ to $t=2\pi$.

#### Solution

Since $C$ lies in the $xy$ plane, $z=0$ and $dz=0$. The line integral is:

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \oint_C (x-3y) dx + (y-2x) dy
$$

Given the parameterization of the ellipse:

$$
x = 2\cos t \implies dx = -2\sin t dt
$$

$$
y = 3\sin t \implies dy = 3\cos t dt
$$

Substitute these into the integrand:

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^{2\pi} (2\cos t - 9\sin t)(-2\sin t dt) + (3\sin t - 4\cos t)(3\cos t dt)
$$

$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} \left( -4\sin t \cos t + 18\sin^2 t + 9\sin t \cos t - 12\cos^2 t \right) dt
$$

$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} \left( 18\sin^2 t - 12\cos^2 t + 5\sin t \cos t \right) dt
$$

Write

$$
\sin^2 t = \frac{1-\cos 2t}{2}
$$

 and

$$
\cos^2 t = \frac{1+\cos 2t}{2}:
$$

$$
18\sin^2 t - 12\cos^2 t = 9(1-\cos 2t) - 6(1+\cos 2t) = 3 - 15\cos 2t
$$

Also,

$$
5\sin t \cos t = \frac{5}{2}\sin 2t
$$

. Substitute these back:

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi} \left( 3 - 15\cos 2t + \frac{5}{2}\sin 2t \right) dt
$$

$$
= \left[ 3t - \frac{15}{2}\sin 2t - \frac{5}{4}\cos 2t \right]\Big|_0^{2\pi}
$$

$$
= \left( 6\pi - 0 - \frac{5}{4} \right) - \left( 0 - 0 - \frac{5}{4} \right) = 6\pi
$$

### Supplementary Problem 43

#### Question

If

$$
\mathbf{F} = (2x+y^2)\mathbf{i} + (3y-4x)\mathbf{j}
$$

, evaluate

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$

around the triangle $C$ with vertices at $(0,0)$, $(2,0)$, and $(2,1)$ in the counterclockwise direction.

#### Solution

The closed loop triangle $C$ has three segments:
1. Segment $C_1$: Along the $x$ axis from $(0,0)$ to $(2,0)$. Here $y=0$ and $dy=0$, while $x$ goes from $0$ to $2$:

$$
\int_{C_1} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^2 (2x+0^2) dx = \left[ x^2 \right]\Big|_0^2 = 4
$$

2. Segment $C_2$: Along the vertical line $x=2$ from $(2,0)$ to $(2,1)$. Here $dx=0$, while $y$ goes from $0$ to $1$:

$$
\int_{C_2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^1 (3y - 4(2)) dy = \int_0^1 (3y-8) dy = \left[ \frac{3}{2}y^2 - 8y \right]\Big|_0^1 = \frac{3}{2} - 8 = -\frac{13}{2}
$$

3. Segment $C_3$: Along the hypotenuse from $(2,1)$ back to $(0,0)$. The line equation is $y = x/2 \implies x = 2y$, so $dx = 2dy$, where $y$ goes from $1$ to $0$:

$$
\int_{C_3} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_1^0 [2(2y) + y^2](2dy) + (3y - 4(2y)) dy
$$

$$
= \int_1^0 (8y + 2y^2 - 5y) dy = \int_1^0 (2y^2 + 3y) dy
$$

$$
= \left[ \frac{2}{3}y^3 + \frac{3}{2}y^2 \right]\Big|_1^0 = 0 - \left( \frac{2}{3} + \frac{3}{2} \right) = -\frac{13}{6}
$$

Sum the contributions from all three segments:

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 4 - \frac{13}{2} - \frac{13}{6} = \frac{24 - 39 - 13}{6} = -\frac{28}{6} = -\frac{14}{3}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 44

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$

around the closed curve $C$ bounded by

$$
y^2 = x
$$

and

$$
y = x^2
$$

in the positive direction, where $\mathbf{A} = (x-y)\mathbf{i} + (x+y)\mathbf{j}$.

#### Solution

The curves intersect at $(0,0)$ and $(1,1)$. The boundary $C$ consists of two segments:
1. Segment $C_1$: Along the curve

$$
y = x^2
$$

from $(0,0)$ to $(1,1)$. Here $dy = 2x dx$, where $x$ goes from $0$ to $1$:

$$
\int_{C_1} \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^1 (x-x^2) dx + (x+x^2)(2x dx)
$$

$$
= \int_0^1 (x - x^2 + 2x^2 + 2x^3) dx = \int_0^1 (2x^3 + x^2 + x) dx
$$

$$
= \left[ \frac{1}{2}x^4 + \frac{1}{3}x^3 + \frac{1}{2}x^2 \right]\Big|_0^1 = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{4}{3}
$$

2. Segment $C_2$: Along the curve

$$
y^2 = x
$$

(or

$$
x = y^2
$$

) from $(1,1)$ to $(0,0)$. Here $dx = 2y dy$, where $y$ goes from $1$ to $0$:

$$
\int_{C_2} \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_1^0 (y^2-y)(2y dy) + (y^2+y) dy
$$

$$
= \int_1^0 (2y^3 - 2y^2 + y^2 + y) dy = \int_1^0 (2y^3 - y^2 + y) dy
$$

$$
= \left[ \frac{1}{2}y^4 - \frac{1}{3}y^3 + \frac{1}{2}y^2 \right]\Big|_1^0 = 0 - \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2} \right) = -\frac{2}{3}
$$

Sum the two contributions:

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \frac{4}{3} - \frac{2}{3} = \frac{2}{3}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 55(a)

#### Question

If

$$
\phi = 2x y^2 z + x^2 y
$$

, evaluate $\int_C \phi d\mathbf{r}$ where $C$ is the curve

$$
x=t, y=t^2, z=t^3
$$

from $t=0$ to $t=1$.

#### Solution

Express $\phi$ and $\mathbf{r}$ along the path $C$ in terms of the parameter $t$:

$$
\phi = 2(t)(t^2)^2(t^3) + (t)^2(t^2) = 2t^8 + t^4
$$

$$
\mathbf{r} = t\mathbf{i} + t^2\mathbf{j} + t^3\mathbf{k} \implies d\mathbf{r} = (\mathbf{i} + 2t\mathbf{j} + 3t^2\mathbf{k}) dt
$$

Set up and evaluate the integral component-wise:

$$
\int_C \phi d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^1 (2t^8+t^4) (\mathbf{i} + 2t\mathbf{j} + 3t^2\mathbf{k}) dt
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i} \int_0^1 (2t^8+t^4) dt + \mathbf{j} \int_0^1 (4t^9+2t^5) dt + \mathbf{k} \int_0^1 (6t^{10}+3t^6) dt
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i} \left[ \frac{2}{9}t^9 + \frac{1}{5}t^5 \right]\Big|_0^1 + \mathbf{j} \left[ \frac{2}{5}t^{10} + \frac{1}{3}t^6 \right]\Big|_0^1 + \mathbf{k} \left[ \frac{6}{11}t^{11} + \frac{3}{7}t^7 \right]\Big|_0^1
$$

$$
= \left( \frac{2}{9} + \frac{1}{5} \right)\mathbf{i} + \left( \frac{2}{5} + \frac{1}{3} \right)\mathbf{j} + \left( \frac{6}{11} + \frac{3}{7} \right)\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
= \frac{19}{45}\mathbf{i} + \frac{11}{15}\mathbf{j} + \frac{75}{77}\mathbf{k}
$$
