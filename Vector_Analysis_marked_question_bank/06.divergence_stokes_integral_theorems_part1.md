## Type-01: Green's Theorem in the Plane: Proofs and Area Applications

### Solved Problem 1

#### Question

Prove Green's theorem in the plane if $C$ is a closed curve which has the property that any straight line parallel to the coordinate axes cuts $C$ in at most two points.

#### Solution

Let the region bounded by $C$ be $R$. Let the lower boundary of $C$ from $x=a$ to $x=b$ be given by $y = Y_1(x)$ and the upper boundary by $y = Y_2(x)$.
Compute the double integral of

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y}
$$

over $R$

$$
\iint_R \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} dx dy = \int_{x=a}^b \left[ \int_{y=Y_1(x)}^{Y_2(x)} \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} dy \right] dx
$$

$$
= \int_a^b \left[ M(x, y) \Big|_{y=Y_1(x)}^{Y_2(x)} \right] dx = \int_a^b [ M(x, Y_2(x)) - M(x, Y_1(x)) ] dx
$$

Reversing the limits of the second integral to represent the counterclockwise boundary traversal

$$
= - \int_a^b M(x, Y_1(x)) dx - \int_b^a M(x, Y_2(x)) dx = - \oint_C M dx
$$

Thus

$$
\oint_C M dx = - \iint_R \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} dx dy
$$

Similarly, let the left boundary of $C$ from $y=e$ to $y=f$ be $x = X_1(y)$ and the right boundary be $x = X_2(y)$. Compute the double integral of

$$
\frac{\partial N}{\partial x}
$$

over $R$

$$
\iint_R \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} dx dy = \int_{y=e}^f \left[ \int_{x=X_1(y)}^{X_2(y)} \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} dx \right] dy
$$

$$
= \int_e^f [ N(X_2(y), y) - N(X_1(y), y) ] dy
$$

$$
= \int_e^f N(X_2(y), y) dy + \int_f^e N(X_1(y), y) dy = \oint_C N dy
$$

Thus

$$
\oint_C N dy = \iint_R \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} dx dy
$$

Adding the two equations yields Green's theorem in the plane

$$
\oint_C M dx + N dy = \iint_R \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy
$$

### Solved Problem 3

#### Question

Extend the proof of Green's theorem in the plane given in Solved Problem 1 to curves $C$ for which lines parallel to the coordinate axes may cut $C$ in more than two points.

#### Solution

Consider a region $R$ bounded by a curve $C$ where a parallel line can intersect $C$ at more than two points. Construct a cross-cut line segment $ST$ dividing $R$ into two sub-regions $R_1$ and $R_2$ that each satisfy the conditions of Solved Problem 1.
Applying Green's theorem to $R_1$ and $R_2$

$$
\int_{STUS} M dx + N dy = \iint_{R_1} \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy
$$

$$
\int_{SVTS} M dx + N dy = \iint_{R_2} \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy
$$

Adding these two equations:
The line integrals along the boundary of $R_1$ and $R_2$ consist of

$$
\oint_{STUS} + \oint_{SVTS} = \left( \int_{ST} + \int_{TUS} \right) + \left( \int_{SVT} + \int_{TS} \right)
$$

Since the line segment $ST$ is traversed in opposite directions in the two integrals

$$
\int_{ST} = - \int_{TS}
$$

These cancel, leaving

$$
\int_{TUS} + \int_{SVT} = \oint_C M dx + N dy
$$

The sum of the double integrals over $R_1$ and $R_2$ is the double integral over the entire region $R$

$$
\iint_{R_1} + \iint_{R_2} = \iint_R \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy
$$

Therefore

$$
\oint_C M dx + N dy = \iint_R \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) dx dy
$$

For more complex regions, the theorem is established by constructing additional cross-cut lines.

### Solved Problem 4

#### Question

Express Green's theorem in the plane in vector notation.

#### Solution

Let

$$
\mathbf{A} = M\mathbf{i} + N\mathbf{j}
$$

and

$$
\mathbf{r} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}
$$

so that

$$
d\mathbf{r} = dx\mathbf{i} + dy\mathbf{j}.
$$

The line integral can be written

$$
\oint_C M dx + N dy = \oint_C (M\mathbf{i} + N\mathbf{j}) \cdot (dx\mathbf{i} + dy\mathbf{j}) = \oint_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r}
$$

The curl of $\mathbf{A}$ is

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
M & N & 0
\end{vmatrix} = \left( \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \right) \mathbf{k}
$$

Thus

$$
(\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) \cdot \mathbf{k} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y}
$$

Using $dR = dx dy$ as the area element, Green's theorem in vector notation is

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_R (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) \cdot \mathbf{k} dR
$$

Alternatively, let $\mathbf{T}$ be the unit tangent vector to $C$. If $\mathbf{n}$ is the outward-drawn unit normal to $C$, then

$$
\mathbf{T} = \mathbf{k} \times \mathbf{n}.
$$

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{T} ds = \mathbf{A} \cdot (\mathbf{k} \times \mathbf{n}) ds = (\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{k}) \cdot \mathbf{n} ds
$$

Letting

$$
\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{k} = N\mathbf{i} - M\mathbf{j},
$$

we find

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y}.
$$

The theorem becomes

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{n} ds = \iint_R \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} dR
$$

### Solved Problem 7

#### Question

Show that the area bounded by a simple closed curve $C$ is given by

$$
\frac{1}{2} \oint_C x dy - y dx.
$$

#### Solution

In Green's theorem, set $M = -y$ and $N = x$.
Find the partial derivatives

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = -1, \quad \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = 1
$$

Substitute these into Green's theorem

$$
\oint_C -y dx + x dy = \iint_R (1 - (-1)) dx dy = 2 \iint_R dx dy = 2A
$$

where $A$ is the area of the region $R$. Thus

$$
A = \frac{1}{2} \oint_C x dy - y dx
$$

### Solved Problem 8

#### Question

Find the area of the ellipse $x = a \cos \theta$, $y = b \sin \theta$.

#### Solution

Using the area formula

$$
A = \frac{1}{2} \oint_C x dy - y dx
$$

The parametric differentials are $dx = -a \sin \theta d\theta$ and $dy = b \cos \theta d\theta$.
Substitute these into the formula

$$
A = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^{2\pi} [ (a \cos \theta)(b \cos \theta) - (b \sin \theta)(-a \sin \theta) ] d\theta
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{2} \int_0^{2\pi} ab (\cos^2 \theta + \sin^2 \theta) d\theta = \frac{1}{2} ab \int_0^{2\pi} d\theta = \pi ab
$$

### Supplementary Problem 43

#### Question

Find the area bounded by one arch of the cycloid $x = a(\theta - \sin \theta)$, $y = a(1 - \cos \theta)$, $a > 0$, and the $x$-axis.

#### Solution

For one arch of the cycloid, the parameter $\theta$ ranges from $0$ to $2\pi$. The area is

$$
A = \int_{x=0}^{2\pi a} y dx
$$

Since $dx = a(1 - \cos \theta) d\theta$

$$
A = \int_0^{2\pi} a(1 - \cos \theta) \cdot a(1 - \cos \theta) d\theta = a^2 \int_0^{2\pi} (1 - 2\cos \theta + \cos^2 \theta) d\theta
$$

Substitute

$$
\cos^2 \theta = \frac{1 + \cos 2\theta}{2}
$$

$$
A = a^2 \int_0^{2\pi} \left( 1 - 2\cos \theta + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\cos 2\theta \right) d\theta
$$

$$
= a^2 \left[ \frac{3}{2}\theta - 2\sin \theta + \frac{1}{4}\sin 2\theta \right]\Big|_0^{2\pi} = a^2 \left( 3\pi \right) = 3\pi a^2
$$

### Supplementary Problem 44

#### Question

Find the area bounded by the hypocycloid $x^{2/3} + y^{2/3} = a^{2/3}$, $a > 0$.

#### Solution

The parametric equations of the hypocycloid are $x = a \cos^3 \theta$, $y = a \sin^3 \theta$ for $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$.
Compute the differentials

$$
dx = -3a \cos^2 \theta \sin \theta d\theta, \quad dy = 3a \sin^2 \theta \cos \theta d\theta
$$

Use the area formula

$$
A = \frac{1}{2} \oint_C x dy - y dx
$$

Substitute the parametric expressions

$$
x dy - y dx = (a \cos^3 \theta)(3a \sin^2 \theta \cos \theta) d\theta - (a \sin^3 \theta)(-3a \cos^2 \theta \sin \theta) d\theta
$$

$$
= 3a^2 \cos^2 \theta \sin^2 \theta (\cos^2 \theta + \sin^2 \theta) d\theta = 3a^2 \cos^2 \theta \sin^2 \theta d\theta
$$

Using the identity

$$
\cos \theta \sin \theta = \frac{1}{2} \sin 2\theta
$$

$$
= \frac{3}{4} a^2 \sin^2 2\theta d\theta = \frac{3}{8} a^2 (1 - \cos 4\theta) d\theta
$$

Integrate from $0$ to $2\pi$

$$
A = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{3}{8} a^2 (1 - \cos 4\theta) d\theta = \frac{3}{16} a^2 \left[ \theta - \frac{\sin 4\theta}{4} \right]\Big|_0^{2\pi} = \frac{3\pi}{8} a^2
$$

### Supplementary Problem 45

#### Question

Show that in polar coordinates $(\rho, \phi)$ the expression $x dy - y dx = \rho^2 d\phi$. Interpret

$$
\frac{1}{2} \int x dy - y dx.
$$

#### Solution

In polar coordinates

$$
x = \rho \cos \phi, \quad y = \rho \sin \phi
$$

Find the differentials

$$
dx = \cos \phi d\rho - \rho \sin \phi d\phi
$$

$$
dy = \sin \phi d\rho + \rho \cos \phi d\phi
$$

Substitute into the expression

$$
x dy - y dx = (\rho \cos \phi)(\sin \phi d\rho + \rho \cos \phi d\phi) - (\rho \sin \phi)(\cos \phi d\rho - \rho \sin \phi d\phi)
$$

$$
= \rho \sin \phi \cos \phi d\rho + \rho^2 \cos^2 \phi d\phi - \rho \sin \phi \cos \phi d\rho + \rho^2 \sin^2 \phi d\phi
$$

$$
= \rho^2 (\cos^2 \phi + \sin^2 \phi) d\phi = \rho^2 d\phi
$$

Interpretation: The quantity

$$
\frac{1}{2} \rho^2 d\phi
$$

represents the area of an infinitesimal circular sector of radius $\rho$ and angle $d\phi$. The integral

$$
\frac{1}{2} \oint_C x dy - y dx = \frac{1}{2} \oint_C \rho^2 d\phi
$$

summates these sectors to find the total area enclosed by the path $C$.

### Supplementary Problem 46

#### Question

Find the area of a loop of the four-leafed rose $\rho = 3 \sin 2\phi$.

#### Solution

One loop is defined for $\phi \in [0, \pi/2]$ where $\rho \ge 0$.
The area of a region in polar coordinates is

$$
A = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^{\pi/2} \rho^2 d\phi = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^{\pi/2} (3 \sin 2\phi)^2 d\phi = \frac{9}{2} \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^2 2\phi d\phi
$$

Using the half-angle formula

$$
\sin^2 2\phi = \frac{1 - \cos 4\phi}{2}
$$

$$
A = \frac{9}{4} \int_0^{\pi/2} (1 - \cos 4\phi) d\phi = \frac{9}{4} \left[ \phi - \frac{\sin 4\phi}{4} \right]\Big|_0^{\pi/2} = \frac{9}{4} \left( \frac{\pi}{2} \right) = \frac{9\pi}{8}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 47

#### Question

Find the area of both loops of the lemniscate $\rho^2 = a^2 \cos 2\phi$.

#### Solution

The lemniscate has two symmetric loops. One loop is defined for $\phi \in [-\pi/4, \pi/4]$ where $\cos 2\phi \ge 0$.
The area of one loop is

$$
A_1 = \frac{1}{2} \int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4} \rho^2 d\phi = \frac{1}{2} \int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4} a^2 \cos 2\phi d\phi = \frac{a^2}{2} \left[ \frac{\sin 2\phi}{2} \right]\Big|_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}
$$

$$
= \frac{a^2}{4} (1 - (-1)) = \frac{a^2}{2}
$$

Since the lemniscate consists of two identical loops, the total area is

$$
A = 2 A_1 = 2 \left( \frac{a^2}{2} \right) = a^2
$$

### Supplementary Problem 48

#### Question

Find the area of the loop of the folium of Descartes $x^3 + y^3 = 3axy$, $a > 0$.

#### Solution

Let $y = tx$. Substitute this into the equation

$$
x^3 + t^3 x^3 = 3ax(tx) = 3atx^2 \implies x = \frac{3at}{1+t^3}
$$

Then

$$
y = tx = \frac{3at^2}{1+t^3}
$$

For the loop in the first quadrant, the parameter $t$ ranges from $0$ to $\infty$.
Use the formula

$$
A = \frac{1}{2} \oint_C x^2 d\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\infty x^2 dt = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\infty \frac{9a^2 t^2}{(1+t^3)^2} dt = \frac{9a^2}{2} \int_0^\infty \frac{t^2}{(1+t^3)^2} dt
$$

Let $u = 1+t^3 \implies du = 3t^2 dt$. When $t=0, u=1$ and as $t \to \infty, u \to \infty$

$$
A = \frac{9a^2}{2} \int_1^\infty \frac{\frac{1}{3} du}{u^2} = \frac{3a^2}{2} \left[ -\frac{1}{u} \right]\Big|_1^\infty = \frac{3a^2}{2} (0 - (-1)) = \frac{3a^2}{2}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 51

#### Question

By changing variables from $(x,y)$ to $(u,v)$ according to the transformation $x = x(u,v)$, $y = y(u,v)$, show that the area $A$ of a region $R$ bounded by a simple closed curve $C$ is given by

$$
A = \iint_R \left| J\left(\frac{x,y}{u,v}\right) \right| du dv
$$

where $J$ is the Jacobian determinant of the transformation.

#### Solution

The area of the region is

$$
A = \frac{1}{2} \oint_C x dy - y dx
$$

Substitute the differentials

$$
dx = \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} du + \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} dv, \quad dy = \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} du + \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} dv
$$

Then

$$
x dy - y dx = x \left( \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} du + \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} dv \right) - y \left( \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} du + \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} dv \right)
$$

$$
= \left( x \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} - y \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} \right) du + \left( x \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} - y \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \right) dv
$$

Set

$$
M' = x \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} - y \frac{\partial x}{\partial u}
$$

 and

$$
N' = x \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} - y \frac{\partial x}{\partial v}
$$

. Apply Green's theorem in the $uv$ plane

$$
A = \frac{1}{2} \iint_{R'} \left( \frac{\partial N'}{\partial u} - \frac{\partial M'}{\partial v} \right) du dv
$$

Compute the partial derivatives

$$
\frac{\partial N'}{\partial u} = \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} + x \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial u \partial v} - \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} - y \frac{\partial^2 x}{\partial u \partial v}
$$

$$
\frac{\partial M'}{\partial v} = \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} + x \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial v \partial u} - \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} - y \frac{\partial^2 x}{\partial v \partial u}
$$

Subtracting these

$$
\frac{\partial N'}{\partial u} - \frac{\partial M'}{\partial v} = 2 \left( \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} - \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} \right) = 2 J\left(\frac{x,y}{u,v}\right)
$$

Thus

$$
A = \frac{1}{2} \iint_{R'} 2 J\left(\frac{x,y}{u,v}\right) du dv = \iint_{R'} J\left(\frac{x,y}{u,v}\right) du dv
$$

To ensure the area is positive, we use the absolute value

$$
A = \iint_{R'} \left| J\left(\frac{x,y}{u,v}\right) \right| du dv
$$
