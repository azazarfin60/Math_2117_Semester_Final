## Type-04: Surface Integrals

### Solved Problem 17

#### Question

Give a definition of

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

over a surface $S$ in terms of the limit of a sum.

#### Solution

Subdivide the surface $S$ into $M$ elements of area $\Delta S_p$ where $p = 1, 2, \dots, M$. Let $P_p(x_p, y_p, z_p)$ be any point in $\Delta S_p$. Define the vector field value at this point as $\mathbf{A}_p = \mathbf{A}(x_p, y_p, z_p)$. Let $\mathbf{n}_p$ be the positive unit normal vector to the element $\Delta S_p$ at $P_p$, directed outward if the surface is closed. Form the sum of the normal components:

$$
\sum_{p=1}^M \mathbf{A}_p \cdot \mathbf{n}_p \Delta S_p
$$

Take the limit of this sum as the number of elements $M \to \infty$ such that the largest linear dimension of each element $\Delta S_p$ approaches zero. If this limit exists, it is called the surface integral of the normal component of $\mathbf{A}$ over $S$ and is written:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \lim_{M \to \infty} \sum_{p=1}^M \mathbf{A}_p \cdot \mathbf{n}_p \Delta S_p
$$

### Solved Problem 18

#### Question

Suppose that a surface $S$ has a projection $R$ on the $xy$ plane. Show that:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} \frac{dx dy}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k}|}
$$

#### Solution

The surface integral is defined as the limit of the sum:

$$
\sum_{p=1}^M \mathbf{A}_p \cdot \mathbf{n}_p \Delta S_p
$$

The projection of each surface element $\Delta S_p$ onto the $xy$ plane is a rectangular region of area $\Delta x_p \Delta y_p$. The area of the projection is related to the surface area element by the angle between the normal vector $\mathbf{n}_p$ and the unit vector $\mathbf{k}$ (perpendicular to the $xy$ plane):

$$
\Delta x_p \Delta y_p = |\mathbf{n}_p \cdot \mathbf{k}| \Delta S_p \implies \Delta S_p = \frac{\Delta x_p \Delta y_p}{|\mathbf{n}_p \cdot \mathbf{k}|}
$$

Substituting this into the sum gives:

$$
\sum_{p=1}^M \mathbf{A}_p \cdot \mathbf{n}_p \frac{\Delta x_p \Delta y_p}{|\mathbf{n}_p \cdot \mathbf{k}|}
$$

Taking the limit as $M \to \infty$ where the largest dimensions of $\Delta x_p$ and $\Delta y_p$ approach zero, the sum converges to the double integral over the projection region $R$:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} \frac{dx dy}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k}|}
$$

### Solved Problem 19

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

where $\mathbf{A} = 18z\mathbf{i} - 12\mathbf{j} + 3y\mathbf{k}$ and $S$ is the portion of the plane $2x + 3y + 6z = 12$ located in the first octant.

#### Solution

First, determine the unit normal vector $\mathbf{n}$ to the plane. The gradient of the plane equation is:

$$
\nabla(2x+3y+6z) = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} + 6\mathbf{k}
$$

The unit normal vector is:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} + 6\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{2^2 + 3^2 + 6^2}} = \frac{2}{7}\mathbf{i} + \frac{3}{7}\mathbf{j} + \frac{6}{7}\mathbf{k}
$$

The projection of $S$ onto the $xy$ plane is the region $R$ bounded by $x=0, y=0$, and $2x+3y=12$ (with $z=0$). The projection factor is:

$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k} = \frac{6}{7} \implies \frac{dx dy}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k}|} = \frac{7}{6} dx dy
$$

Compute the integrand:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = (18z\mathbf{i} - 12\mathbf{j} + 3y\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{2}{7}\mathbf{i} + \frac{3}{7}\mathbf{j} + \frac{6}{7}\mathbf{k}\right) = \frac{36z - 36 + 18y}{7}
$$

Express $z$ in terms of $x$ and $y$ using the plane equation $2x + 3y + 6z = 12 \implies 6z = 12 - 2x - 3y$:

$$
36z = 6(6z) = 6(12 - 2x - 3y) = 72 - 12x - 18y
$$

Substitute this expression back into the integrand:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = \frac{(72 - 12x - 18y) - 36 + 18y}{7} = \frac{36 - 12x}{7}
$$

Now set up and evaluate the double integral:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \left(\frac{36 - 12x}{7}\right) \frac{7}{6} dx dy = \iint_R (6 - 2x) dx dy
$$

The region $R$ is bounded by $x \in [0, 6]$ and $y \in [0, \frac{12-2x}{3}]$:

$$
= \int_{x=0}^6 \int_{y=0}^{(12-2x)/3} (6-2x) dy dx = \int_0^6 (6-2x) \frac{12-2x}{3} dx
$$

$$
= \frac{2}{3} \int_0^6 (3-x)(12-2x) dx = \frac{4}{3} \int_0^6 (3-x)(6-x) dx
$$

$$
= \frac{4}{3} \int_0^6 (18 - 9x + x^2) dx = \frac{4}{3} \left[ 18x - \frac{9}{2}x^2 + \frac{1}{3}x^3 \right]\Big|_0^6
$$

$$
= \frac{4}{3} \left( 18(6) - \frac{9}{2}(36) + \frac{1}{3}(216) \right) = \frac{4}{3} (108 - 162 + 72) = \frac{4}{3} (18) = 24
$$

### Solved Problem 20

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

where $\mathbf{A} = z\mathbf{i} + x\mathbf{j} - 3y^2 z\mathbf{k}$ and $S$ is the surface of the cylinder $x^2 + y^2 = 16$ in the first octant bounded by $z=0$ and $z=5$.

#### Solution

Since the cylinder's axis is parallel to the $z$ axis, we project the surface $S$ onto the $xz$ plane. The projection region $R$ is a rectangle defined by $x \in [0, 4]$ and $z \in [0, 5]$.
Find the unit normal vector $\mathbf{n}$:

$$
\nabla(x^2+y^2) = 2x\mathbf{i} + 2y\mathbf{j}
$$

Since $x^2+y^2=16$ on the cylinder:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{2x\mathbf{i} + 2y\mathbf{j}}{\sqrt{(2x)^2 + (2y)^2}} = \frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}}{4}
$$

The projection factor is:

$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j} = \frac{y}{4} \implies dS = \frac{dx dz}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j}|} = \frac{4}{y} dx dz
$$

Compute the integrand:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = (z\mathbf{i} + x\mathbf{j} - 3y^2 z\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}}{4}\right) = \frac{xz + xy}{4}
$$

Multiply the integrand by the projection factor:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \frac{xz+xy}{4} \frac{4}{y} dx dz = \left( \frac{xz}{y} + x \right) dx dz
$$

Using the cylinder equation $y = \sqrt{16-x^2}$ in the first octant:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \int_{z=0}^5 \int_{x=0}^4 \left( \frac{xz}{\sqrt{16-x^2}} + x \right) dx dz
$$

Evaluate the inner integral with respect to $x$:

$$
\int_0^4 \left( xz(16-x^2)^{-1/2} + x \right) dx = \left[ -z\sqrt{16-x^2} + \frac{x^2}{2} \right]\Big|_0^4
$$

$$
= \left( 0 + 8 \right) - \left( -4z + 0 \right) = 4z + 8
$$

Now integrate with respect to $z$ from $0$ to $5$:

$$
\int_0^5 (4z+8) dz = \left[ 2z^2 + 8z \right]\Big|_0^5 = 2(25) + 8(5) = 50 + 40 = 90
$$

### Solved Problem 21

#### Question

Evaluate $\iint_S \phi \mathbf{n} dS$ where $\phi = \frac{3}{8}xyz$ and $S$ is the cylinder surface from Solved Problem 20.

#### Solution

Using the projection of $S$ onto the $xz$ plane, the integral is:

$$
\iint_S \phi \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \phi \mathbf{n} \frac{dx dz}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j}|}
$$

Substitute the potential $\phi = \frac{3}{8}xyz$, unit normal $\mathbf{n} = \frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}}{4}$, and projection factor $|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j}| = \frac{y}{4}$:

$$
\iint_S \phi \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \left(\frac{3}{8}xyz\right) \left(\frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}}{4}\right) \frac{4}{y} dx dz
$$

$$
= \frac{3}{8} \iint_R xz (x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}) dx dz
$$

Substitute $y = \sqrt{16-x^2}$:

$$
= \frac{3}{8} \int_{z=0}^5 \int_{x=0}^4 \left( x^2z\mathbf{i} + xz\sqrt{16-x^2}\mathbf{j} \right) dx dz
$$

Evaluate the inner integrals with respect to $x$:
1. For the $\mathbf{i}$ component:

$$
\int_0^4 x^2z dx = z \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} \right]\Big|_0^4 = \frac{64}{3}z
$$

2. For the $\mathbf{j}$ component:

$$
\int_0^4 xz\sqrt{16-x^2} dx = z \left[ -\frac{1}{3}(16-x^2)^{3/2} \right]\Big|_0^4 = z \left( 0 - \left(-\frac{64}{3}\right) \right) = \frac{64}{3}z
$$

Substitute these results back and integrate with respect to $z$:

$$
\iint_S \phi \mathbf{n} dS = \frac{3}{8} \int_0^5 \left( \frac{64}{3}z\mathbf{i} + \frac{64}{3}z\mathbf{j} \right) dz = 8 (\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}) \int_0^5 z dz
$$

$$
= 8 (\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}) \left[ \frac{z^2}{2} \right]\Big|_0^5 = 100\mathbf{i} + 100\mathbf{j}
$$

### Solved Problem 22

#### Question

If $\mathbf{F} = y\mathbf{i} + (x - 2xz)\mathbf{j} - xy\mathbf{k}$, evaluate

$$
\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

where $S$ is the surface of the hemisphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = a^2$ above the $xy$ plane.

#### Solution

First, compute the curl of $\mathbf{F}$:

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
y & x - 2xz & -xy
\end{vmatrix}
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-xy) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(x-2xz) \right] - \mathbf{j}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-xy) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(y) \right] + \mathbf{k}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x-2xz) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y) \right]
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i}(-x - (-2x)) - \mathbf{j}(-y - 0) + \mathbf{k}(1 - 2z - 1) = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} - 2z\mathbf{k}
$$

Find the unit normal vector $\mathbf{n}$ pointing outward from the hemisphere:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{\nabla(x^2+y^2+z^2)}{|\nabla(x^2+y^2+z^2)|} = \frac{2x\mathbf{i} + 2y\mathbf{j} + 2z\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{4(x^2+y^2+z^2)}} = \frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}}{a}
$$

Project the hemisphere onto the $xy$ plane. The projection region $R$ is the circle $x^2+y^2 \le a^2$:

$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k} = \frac{z}{a} \implies dS = \frac{a}{z} dx dy
$$

Compute the integrand:

$$
(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n} = (x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} - 2z\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}}{a}\right) = \frac{x^2 + y^2 - 2z^2}{a}
$$

Substitute $z^2 = a^2 - x^2 - y^2$:

$$
(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n} = \frac{x^2 + y^2 - 2(a^2 - x^2 - y^2)}{a} = \frac{3(x^2+y^2) - 2a^2}{a}
$$

Set up the surface integral:

$$
\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_R \left(\frac{3(x^2+y^2) - 2a^2}{a}\right) \frac{a}{z} dx dy = \iint_R \frac{3(x^2+y^2) - 2a^2}{\sqrt{a^2-x^2-y^2}} dx dy
$$

Transform the double integral to polar coordinates ($x = \rho\cos\theta, y = \rho\sin\theta, dx dy = \rho d\rho d\theta$):

$$
= \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^a \frac{3\rho^2 - 2a^2}{\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2}} \rho d\rho d\theta
$$

Evaluate the inner integral:

$$
\int_0^a \frac{3\rho^2 - 2a^2}{\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2}} \rho d\rho = \int_0^a \frac{3\rho^2 - 3a^2 + a^2}{\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2}} \rho d\rho
$$

$$
= \int_0^a \left( -3\rho\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2} + \frac{a^2\rho}{\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2}} \right) d\rho
$$

$$
= \left[ (a^2-\rho^2)^{3/2} - a^2\sqrt{a^2-\rho^2} \right]\Big|_0^a
$$

$$
= \left( 0 - 0 \right) - \left( a^3 - a^3 \right) = 0
$$

Since the inner integral is zero, the surface integral is:

$$
\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = 0
$$

### Solved Problem 23

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

over:
(a) the surface $S$ of the unit cube bounded by the coordinate planes and the planes $x=1, y=1, z=1$.
(b) the surface of a sphere of radius $a$ with center at the origin.

#### Solution

(a) The cube surface $S$ has six faces:
1. Face $x=1$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{i}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = x = 1$. The integral over this face is:

$$
\int_0^1 \int_0^1 (1) dy dz = 1
$$

2. Face $x=0$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{i}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = -x = 0$. The integral is $0$.
3. Face $y=1$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{j}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = y = 1$. The integral over this face is:

$$
\int_0^1 \int_0^1 (1) dx dz = 1
$$

4. Face $y=0$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{j}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = -y = 0$. The integral is $0$.
5. Face $z=1$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{k}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = z = 1$. The integral over this face is:

$$
\int_0^1 \int_0^1 (1) dx dy = 1
$$

6. Face $z=0$: Outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{k}$. Here $\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = -z = 0$. The integral is $0$.
Adding the contributions from all six faces:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = 1 + 0 + 1 + 0 + 1 + 0 = 3
$$

(b) For the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=a^2$, the outward unit normal at any point is:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}}{a} = \frac{\mathbf{r}}{a}
$$

Compute the integrand:

$$
\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{r} \cdot \frac{\mathbf{r}}{a} = \frac{r^2}{a} = \frac{a^2}{a} = a
$$

Since $a$ is constant on the surface:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_S a dS = a \iint_S dS = a (4\pi a^2) = 4\pi a^3
$$

### Solved Problem 24

#### Question

Show that

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{n} dS = \mathbf{0}
$$

for any closed surface $S$.

#### Solution

Let the unit normal vector be written as:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \cos \alpha \mathbf{i} + \cos \beta \mathbf{j} + \cos \gamma \mathbf{k}
$$

where $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ are the angles $\mathbf{n}$ makes with the positive $x, y, z$ axes. The surface integral can be written:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{n} dS = \mathbf{i} \iint_S \cos \alpha dS + \mathbf{j} \iint_S \cos \beta dS + \mathbf{k} \iint_S \cos \gamma dS
$$

Consider the projection of $S$ onto the $yz$ plane. Let $R$ be this projection. Since $S$ is closed, any line parallel to the $x$ axis intersects the surface at an even number of points (typically two). The surface element $dS$ projects onto $dy dz$ such that:

$$
dy dz = |\cos \alpha| dS
$$

For the part of the surface where the normal points away from the origin ($\cos \alpha > 0$), we have $\cos \alpha dS = dy dz$. For the part where the normal points toward the origin ($\cos \alpha < 0$), we have $\cos \alpha dS = -dy dz$.
Integrating over the entire closed surface:

$$
\iint_S \cos \alpha dS = \iint_R dy dz - \iint_R dy dz = 0
$$

By projecting $S$ onto the $xz$ and $xy$ planes, we similarly find:

$$
\iint_S \cos \beta dS = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \iint_S \cos \gamma dS = 0
$$

Therefore:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{n} dS = \mathbf{0}
$$

### Solved Problem 25

#### Question

Let $S$ be a given surface which projects onto a region $R$ of the $xy$ plane. Let the equation of $S$ be $z = f(x,y)$. Show that the area of $S$ is given by:

$$
\iint_R \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^2} dx dy
$$

#### Solution

The surface area of $S$ is given by $\iint_S dS$.
Let $F(x,y,z) = z - f(x,y) = 0$. The gradient vector normal to the surface is:

$$
\nabla F = -\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} - \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}
$$

The unit normal vector is:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{\nabla F}{|\nabla F|} = \frac{-\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} - \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^2}}
$$

Take the dot product of $\mathbf{n}$ with the unit vector $\mathbf{k}$:

$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^2}}
$$

The projection factor relates $dS$ and $dx dy$:

$$
dS = \frac{dx dy}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k}|} = \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^2} dx dy
$$

Integrating over the projection region $R$:

$$
\text{Area} = \iint_S dS = \iint_R \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^2} dx dy
$$

### Supplementary Problem 58

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

for each of the following cases:
(a) $\mathbf{A} = y\mathbf{i} + 2x\mathbf{j} - z\mathbf{k}$ and $S$ is the surface of the plane $2x+y=6$ in the first octant cut off by the plane $z=4$.
(b) $\mathbf{A} = (x+y^2)\mathbf{i} - 2x\mathbf{j} + 2yz\mathbf{k}$ and $S$ is the surface of the plane $2x+y+2z=6$ in the first octant.

#### Solution

(a) Since the plane $2x+y=6$ is vertical (parallel to the $z$ axis), we project the surface $S$ onto the $xz$ plane. The projection region $R$ is bounded by $x \in [0, 3]$, $z \in [0, 4]$.
Find the unit normal vector $\mathbf{n}$:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{\nabla(2x+y)}{|\nabla(2x+y)|} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}}{\sqrt{2^2+1^2}} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}}{\sqrt{5}}
$$

The projection factor is:

$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} \implies dS = \frac{dx dz}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{j}|} = \sqrt{5} dx dz
$$

Compute the integrand:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = (y\mathbf{i} + 2x\mathbf{j} - z\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j}}{\sqrt{5}}\right) = \frac{2y+2x}{\sqrt{5}}
$$

Multiply the integrand by the projection factor and substitute $y = 6-2x$:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \frac{2y+2x}{\sqrt{5}} \sqrt{5} dx dz = (2y+2x) dx dz = (2(6-2x)+2x) dx dz = (12-2x) dx dz
$$

Set up and evaluate the integral:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \int_0^4 \int_0^3 (12-2x) dx dz = \left( \int_0^4 dz \right) \left( \int_0^3 (12-2x) dx \right)
$$

$$
= 4 \left[ 12x - x^2 \right]\Big|_0^3 = 4 (36 - 9) = 4(27) = 108
$$

(b) The plane is $2x+y+2z=6$. The unit normal vector $\mathbf{n}$ is:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{2^2+1^2+2^2}} = \frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{3}
$$

Project the plane onto the $xy$ plane. The projection region $R$ is bounded by $x \in [0, 3]$, $y \in [0, 6-2x]$. The projection factor is:

$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k} = \frac{2}{3} \implies dS = \frac{dx dy}{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k}|} = \frac{3}{2} dx dy
$$

Compute the integrand:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = ((x+y^2)\mathbf{i} - 2x\mathbf{j} + 2yz\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{2\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}}{3}\right) = \frac{2(x+y^2) - 2x + 4yz}{3} = \frac{2y^2 + 4yz}{3}
$$

Multiply the integrand by the projection factor:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \frac{2y^2 + 4yz}{3} \frac{3}{2} dx dy = (y^2 + 2yz) dx dy
$$

Express $z$ in terms of $x$ and $y$ using the plane equation $2x + y + 2z = 6 \implies 2z = 6 - 2x - y$:

$$
y^2 + 2yz = y^2 + y(6-2x-y) = y^2 + 6y - 2xy - y^2 = 6y - 2xy
$$

Set up and evaluate the double integral:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \int_0^3 \int_0^{6-2x} (6y-2xy) dy dx = \int_0^3 (6-2x) \left[ \frac{y^2}{2} \right]\Big|_0^{6-2x} dx
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{2} \int_0^3 (6-2x)^3 dx
$$

Using the substitution $u = 6-2x \implies du = -2dx$:

$$
= \frac{1}{2} \int_6^0 u^3 \left(-\frac{1}{2} du\right) = \frac{1}{4} \int_0^6 u^3 du = \frac{1}{4} \left[ \frac{u^4}{4} \right]\Big|_0^6 = \frac{1296}{16} = 81
$$

### Supplementary Problem 60

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

over the entire surface $S$ of the region bounded by the cylinder $x^2+z^2=9$, $x=0, y=0, z=0$ and $y=8$, if $\mathbf{A} = 6z\mathbf{i} + (2x+y)\mathbf{j} - x\mathbf{k}$.

#### Solution

The closed surface $S$ bounds a wedge-like volume in the first octant. Subdivide $S$ into five distinct faces:
1. Face $S_1$ (on $x=0$ plane): Here $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{i}$, $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = -6z$. The region is a rectangle in the $yz$ plane with $y \in [0, 8]$ and $z \in [0, 3]$:

$$
\iint_{S_1} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_1 = \int_0^8 \int_0^3 -6z dz dy = 8 \left[ -3z^2 \right]\Big|_0^3 = -216
$$

2. Face $S_2$ (on $y=0$ plane): Here $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{j}$, $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = -(2x+y) = -2x$ (since $y=0$). The region is a quarter circle of radius 3 in the $xz$ plane:

$$
\iint_{S_2} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_2 = \iint_{S_2} -2x dx dz
$$

Transforming to polar coordinates ($x = \rho\cos\theta, z = \rho\sin\theta, dx dz = \rho d\rho d\theta$):

$$
= \int_0^{\pi/2} \int_0^3 -2\rho^2 \cos\theta d\rho d\theta = \left( \int_0^{\pi/2} \cos\theta d\theta \right) \left( \int_0^3 -2\rho^2 d\rho \right) = (1)\left( -18 \right) = -18
$$

3. Face $S_3$ (on $z=0$ plane): Here $\mathbf{n} = -\mathbf{k}$, $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = x$. The region is a rectangle in the $xy$ plane with $x \in [0, 3]$ and $y \in [0, 8]$:

$$
\iint_{S_3} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_3 = \int_0^8 \int_0^3 x dx dy = 8 \left[ \frac{x^2}{2} \right]\Big|_0^3 = 36
$$

4. Face $S_4$ (on $y=8$ plane): Here $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{j}$, $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = 2x+y = 2x+8$ (since $y=8$). The region is a quarter circle of radius 3:

$$
\iint_{S_4} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_4 = \iint_{S_4} (2x+8) dx dz = \iint_{S_4} 2x dx dz + 8 \iint_{S_4} dx dz
$$

From Face $S_2$,

$$
\iint_{S_2} -2x dx dz = -18 \implies \iint_{S_4} 2x dx dz = 18.
$$

. The area of the quarter circle is $\frac{9\pi}{4}$:

$$
\iint_{S_4} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_4 = 18 + 8\left(\frac{9\pi}{4}\right) = 18 + 18\pi
$$

5. Face $S_5$ (on the cylinder $x^2+z^2=9$): Here the outward normal vector is:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{x\mathbf{i} + z\mathbf{k}}{3}
$$

The projection of this face onto the $xy$ plane is a rectangle with $x \in [0, 3]$ and $y \in [0, 8]$. The projection factor is:

$$
\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{k} = \frac{z}{3} \implies dS_5 = \frac{3}{z} dx dy
$$

Compute the integrand:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = (6z\mathbf{i} + (2x+y)\mathbf{j} - x\mathbf{k}) \cdot \left(\frac{x\mathbf{i} + z\mathbf{k}}{3}\right) = \frac{6xz - xz}{3} = \frac{5xz}{3}
$$

Multiply the integrand by the projection factor:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_5 = \frac{5xz}{3} \frac{3}{z} dx dy = 5x dx dy
$$

Set up and evaluate the integral:

$$
\iint_{S_5} \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS_5 = \int_0^8 \int_0^3 5x dx dy = 8 \left[ \frac{5}{2}x^2 \right]\Big|_0^3 = 8\left(\frac{45}{2}\right) = 180
$$

Sum the contributions of all five faces to find the total surface integral:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_{S_1} + \iint_{S_2} + \iint_{S_3} + \iint_{S_4} + \iint_{S_5}
$$

$$
= -216 - 18 + 36 + (18 + 18\pi) + 180 = 18\pi
$$

### Supplementary Problem 66

#### Question

Evaluate

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS
$$

over the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=a^2$ if $\mathbf{A} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}$.

#### Solution

Let $\mathbf{r} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}$ be the position vector. Thus, $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{r}$. The outward unit normal to the sphere of radius $a$ is:

$$
\mathbf{n} = \frac{\mathbf{r}}{a}
$$

Compute the integrand:

$$
\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{r} \cdot \frac{\mathbf{r}}{a} = \frac{r^2}{a} = \frac{a^2}{a} = a
$$

Since $a$ is constant on the spherical surface:

$$
\iint_S \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iint_S a dS = a \iint_S dS = a (4\pi a^2) = 4\pi a^3
$$

## Type-05: Volume Integrals

### Solved Problem 26

#### Question

Find the volume of the region bounded by the coordinate planes and the plane $x+y+z=1$.

#### Solution

The volume is:

$$
V = \iiint_V dV = \iiint_V dz dy dx
$$

The region is bounded by $x \ge 0, y \ge 0, z \ge 0$ and $x+y+z \le 1$.
The limits of integration are:
- $z$ goes from $0$ to $1-x-y$
- $y$ goes from $0$ to $1-x$
- $x$ goes from $0$ to $1$
Set up and evaluate the integral:

$$
V = \int_{x=0}^1 \int_{y=0}^{1-x} \int_{z=0}^{1-x-y} dz dy dx = \int_{x=0}^1 \int_{y=0}^{1-x} (1-x-y) dy dx
$$

$$
= \int_0^1 \left[ (1-x)y - \frac{y^2}{2} \right]\Big|_0^{1-x} dx = \int_0^1 \left( (1-x)^2 - \frac{(1-x)^2}{2} \right) dx
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{2} \int_0^1 (1-x)^2 dx = \frac{1}{2} \left[ -\frac{(1-x)^3}{3} \right]\Big|_0^1 = \frac{1}{2}\left( 0 - \left(-\frac{1}{3}\right) \right) = \frac{1}{6}
$$

### Solved Problem 27

#### Question

Find the volume of the region common to the intersecting cylinders $x^2 + y^2 = a^2$ and $x^2 + z^2 = a^2$.

#### Solution

The volume is symmetric across all eight octants. Thus, we compute the volume in the first octant ($x \ge 0, y \ge 0, z \ge 0$) and multiply by 8.
In the first octant:
- $z$ goes from $0$ to $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$
- $y$ goes from $0$ to $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$
- $x$ goes from $0$ to $a$
Set up the volume integral:

$$
V = 8 \int_{x=0}^a \int_{y=0}^{\sqrt{a^2-x^2}} \int_{z=0}^{\sqrt{a^2-x^2}} dz dy dx
$$

$$
= 8 \int_0^a \left( \int_0^{\sqrt{a^2-x^2}} dy \right) \left( \int_0^{\sqrt{a^2-x^2}} dz \right) dx
$$

$$
= 8 \int_0^a \sqrt{a^2-x^2} \sqrt{a^2-x^2} dx = 8 \int_0^a (a^2 - x^2) dx
$$

$$
= 8 \left[ a^2x - \frac{x^3}{3} \right]\Big|_0^a = 8 \left( a^3 - \frac{a^3}{3} \right) = \frac{16}{3}a^3
$$

### Supplementary Problem 68

#### Question

Evaluate $\iiint_V (2x+y) dV$ where $V$ is the closed region bounded by the cylinder $z = 4-x^2$ and the planes $x=0, y=0, y=2, z=0$ in the first octant.

#### Solution

Set up the limits of integration for the region $V$ in the first octant:
- $z$ goes from $0$ to $4-x^2$
- $y$ goes from $0$ to $2$
- $x$ goes from $0$ to $2$ (since $z = 4-x^2 \ge 0 \implies x^2 \le 4 \implies x \le 2$)
Evaluate the volume integral:

$$
\iiint_V (2x+y) dV = \int_{x=0}^2 \int_{y=0}^2 \int_{z=0}^{4-x^2} (2x+y) dz dy dx
$$

$$
= \int_0^2 \int_0^2 (2x+y)(4-x^2) dy dx
$$

$$
= \int_0^2 (4-x^2) \left[ 2xy + \frac{y^2}{2} \right]\Big|_0^2 dx = \int_0^2 (4-x^2) (4x + 2) dx
$$

$$
= 2 \int_0^2 (4-x^2)(2x+1) dx = 2 \int_0^2 (8x + 4 - 2x^3 - x^2) dx
$$

$$
= 2 \left[ 4x^2 + 4x - \frac{1}{2}x^4 - \frac{1}{3}x^3 \right]\Big|_0^2
$$

$$
= 2 \left( 4(4) + 4(2) - \frac{1}{2}(16) - \frac{1}{3}(8) \right)
$$

$$
= 2 \left( 16 + 8 - 8 - \frac{8}{3} \right) = 2 \left( 16 - \frac{8}{3} \right) = 2 \left( \frac{40}{3} \right) = \frac{80}{3}
$$

### Supplementary Problem 69

#### Question

If $\mathbf{F} = (2x^2-3z)\mathbf{i} - 2xy\mathbf{j} - 4x\mathbf{k}$, evaluate:
(a)

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} dV
$$

(b)

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{F} dV
$$

where $V$ is the closed region bounded by the planes $x=0, y=0, z=0$ and $2x+2y+z=4$.

#### Solution

(a) Find the divergence of $\mathbf{F}$:

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(2x^2-3z) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-2xy) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(-4x)
$$

$$
= 4x - 2x + 0 = 2x
$$

Set up the limits of integration for the tetrahedron bounded by the coordinate planes and the plane $2x+2y+z=4$:
- $z$ goes from $0$ to $4-2x-2y$
- $y$ goes from $0$ to $2-x$
- $x$ goes from $0$ to $2$
Evaluate the integral:

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} dV = \int_{x=0}^2 \int_{y=0}^{2-x} \int_{z=0}^{4-2x-2y} 2x dz dy dx
$$

$$
= \int_0^2 \int_0^{2-x} 2x(4-2x-2y) dy dx = 2 \int_0^2 x \left[ (4-2x)y - y^2 \right]\Big|_0^{2-x} dx
$$

$$
= 2 \int_0^2 x \left[ 2(2-x)(2-x) - (2-x)^2 \right] dx
$$

$$
= 2 \int_0^2 x (2-x)^2 dx = 2 \int_0^2 (4x - 4x^2 + x^3) dx
$$

$$
= 2 \left[ 2x^2 - \frac{4}{3}x^3 + \frac{1}{4}x^4 \right]\Big|_0^2
$$

$$
= 2 \left( 2(4) - \frac{4}{3}(8) + \frac{1}{4}(16) \right) = 2 \left( 8 - \frac{32}{3} + 4 \right)
$$

$$
= 2 \left( 12 - \frac{32}{3} \right) = 2\left(\frac{4}{3}\right) = \frac{8}{3}
$$

(b) Find the curl of $\mathbf{F}$:

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
2x^2-3z & -2xy & -4x
\end{vmatrix}
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-4x) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(-2xy) \right] - \mathbf{j}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-4x) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(2x^2-3z) \right]
$$

$$
+\mathbf{k}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-2xy) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(2x^2-3z) \right]
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i}(0 - 0) - \mathbf{j}(-4 - (-3)) + \mathbf{k}(-2y - 0) = \mathbf{j} - 2y\mathbf{k}
$$

The volume integral of the curl is:

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{F} dV = \mathbf{j} \iiint_V dV - 2\mathbf{k} \iiint_V y dV
$$

The volume $V$ of the tetrahedron is:

$$
V = \frac{1}{6} (2)(2)(4) = \frac{8}{3}
$$

Evaluate the integral for the $\mathbf{k}$ component:

$$
\iiint_V y dV = \int_{x=0}^2 \int_{y=0}^{2-x} \int_{z=0}^{4-2x-2y} y dz dy dx
$$

$$
= \int_0^2 \int_0^{2-x} y(4-2x-2y) dy dx = \int_0^2 \left[ (4-2x)\frac{y^2}{2} - \frac{2}{3}y^3 \right]\Big|_0^{2-x} dx
$$

$$
= \int_0^2 (2-x)^3 \left( 1 - \frac{2}{3} \right) dx = \frac{1}{3} \int_0^2 (2-x)^3 dx
$$

Let $u = 2-x \implies du = -dx$:

$$
= \frac{1}{3} \int_0^2 u^3 du = \frac{1}{3} \left[ \frac{u^4}{4} \right]\Big|_0^2 = \frac{4}{3}
$$

Substitute these values back:

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{F} dV = \frac{8}{3}\mathbf{j} - 2\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)\mathbf{k} = \frac{8}{3}(\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k})
$$

### Supplementary Problem 70

#### Question

If $\mathbf{F} = x^2\mathbf{i} + y^2\mathbf{j} + z^2\mathbf{k}$, evaluate:
(a)

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} dV
$$

(b)

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{F} dV
$$

where $V$ is the closed region bounded by the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=a^2$.

#### Solution

(a) Find the divergence of $\mathbf{F}$:

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(z^2) = 2x + 2y + 2z = 2(x+y+z)
$$

By the symmetry of the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2 \le a^2$ centered at the origin:

$$
\iiint_V x dV = 0, \quad \iiint_V y dV = 0, \quad \iiint_V z dV = 0
$$

Thus:

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} dV = 2 \left( \iiint_V x dV + \iiint_V y dV + \iiint_V z dV \right) = 0
$$

(b) Find the curl of $\mathbf{F}$:

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\
x^2 & y^2 & z^2
\end{vmatrix}
$$

$$
= \mathbf{i}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(z^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(y^2) \right] - \mathbf{j}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(z^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(x^2) \right] + \mathbf{k}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(y^2) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2) \right] = \mathbf{0}
$$

Therefore:

$$
\iiint_V \nabla \times \mathbf{F} dV = \mathbf{0}
$$
