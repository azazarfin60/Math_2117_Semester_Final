## 9. Year-over-Year Trend Observations

**Shift in 2023–2024 (60-mark format):**
- Each sub-question now carries 2–5 marks instead of the earlier 4–8 range.
- More short questions per paper. The older papers had fewer questions with higher individual marks.
- Theory definitions (2–3 marks) are now more frequent as standalone questions.

**Recurring patterns:**
- Green's theorem proof appears roughly every other year (2017, 2021, 2024).
- Stokes' theorem is gaining presence (2023 proof, 2024 verification). This was absent in 2017–2021.
- Cayley-Hamilton theorem follows a cycle. It appeared in 2018, 2019, skipped 2020–2023, then returned in 2024.
- Matrix differential equations appeared 2017, 2020, 2021, then disappeared. Might return.
- Polynomial vector space basis problems appeared 2020, 2023, 2024. This is a rising trend.

**CT signals for your batch:**
- Your CTs tested conservative fields, line integrals, rank, and parametric systems. These are strong signals for the final.
- Frenet-Serret formulas appeared in CT-1 but rarely in finals. Still worth a quick review.

## 11. Strategic Preparation Advice

**Start with the guaranteed three.** Rank, eigenvalues, and systems of equations appear every year. Practice at least 3 variants of each.

**Master Green's theorem both ways.** You need to know the proof for theory marks and the verification technique for computation marks. This covers 6–10 marks reliably.

**Practice the exact repeat questions from Section 5.** Many of these will appear again with minor changes. Work through each one until you can solve it from memory.

**CT questions are a preview.** Your class test topics (conservative fields, line integrals, rank, parametric systems) align directly with high-frequency final exam topics.

**Watch the Stokes' theorem trend.** It was rare before 2023 but appeared in both 2023 and 2024. Prepare both proof and verification.

**For definitions, keep them short.** The 2–3 mark definition questions (vector space, rank, eigenvalue, direct sum) are easy marks. Write the definition, give one example, and move on.

**Solve at least one matrix differential equation problem.** It appeared 3 times in the older format. Even if it does not appear, the matrix exponential technique builds understanding of eigenvalues.
