# Chapter 1: Vectors and Scalars

## Theory

### Vectors and Scalars

<mark>A VECTOR</mark> is a quantity having <u>both magnitude and direction, such as displacement, velocity, force, and acceleration</u>.

Graphically a vector is represented by an arrow $OP$ (Fig. 1) defining the direction, the magnitude of the vector being indicated by the length of the arrow. The tail end $O$ of the arrow is called the origin or initial point of the vector, and the head $P$ is called the terminal point or terminus.

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 1, Fig. 1):** A directed line segment representing a vector. The arrow starts at the origin point $O$ (tail) and ends at the terminal point $P$ (head). The vector is labeled as $\vec{A}$ or $\mathbf{A}$.

Analytically a vector is represented by a letter with an arrow over it, as $\vec{A}$ in Fig. 1, and its magnitude is denoted by $|\vec{A}|$ or $A$. In printed works, bold faced type, such as $\mathbf{A}$, is used to indicate the vector $\mathbf{A}$ while $|\mathbf{A}|$ or $A$ indicates its magnitude. We shall use this bold faced notation in this book. The vector $\mathbf{OP}$ is also indicated as $\overline{OP}$ or $\mathbf{OP}$; in such case we shall denote its magnitude by $\overline{OP}$, $|\overline{OP}|$, or $|\mathbf{OP}|$.

<mark>A SCALAR</mark> is a quantity having magnitude but <u>(no) direction, e.g. mass, length, time, temperature, and any real number</u>. *[Note: The word "no" is circled in the book]* Scalars are indicated by letters in ordinary type as in elementary algebra. Operations with scalars follow the same rules as in elementary algebra.

### Vector Algebra

<mark>VECTOR ALGEBRA.</mark> The operations of addition, subtraction and multiplication familiar in the algebra of numbers or scalars are, with suitable definition, capable of extension to an algebra of vectors. The following definitions are fundamental.

1. Two vectors $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are *equal* if they have the same magnitude and direction regardless of the position of their initial points. Thus $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{B}$ in Fig. 2.

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 1, Fig. 2):** Two parallel vectors $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ pointing in the same direction with equal lengths. This shows that they are equal vectors ($\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{B}$).

2. A vector having direction opposite to that of vector $\mathbf{A}$ but having the same magnitude is denoted by $-\mathbf{A}$ (Fig. 3).

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 1, Fig. 3):** Two parallel vectors $\mathbf{A}$ and $-\mathbf{A}$ with equal lengths but opposite directions. This shows the negative of a vector.

3. The sum or resultant of vectors $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ is a vector $\mathbf{C}$ formed by placing the initial point of $\mathbf{B}$ on the terminal point of $\mathbf{A}$ and then joining the initial point of $\mathbf{A}$ to the terminal point of $\mathbf{B}$ (Fig. 4). This sum is written $\mathbf{A}+\mathbf{B}$, i.e. $\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{A}+\mathbf{B}$.

The definition here is equivalent to the *parallelogram law* for vector addition (see Prob. 3).

Extensions to sums of more than two vectors are immediate (see Problem 4).

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 2, Fig. 4):** A triangle and a shaded parallelogram illustrating the triangle rule and the parallelogram law of vector addition. The resultant vector is $\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}$.

4. The difference of vectors $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$, represented by $\mathbf{A}-\mathbf{B}$, is that vector $\mathbf{C}$ which added to $\mathbf{B}$ yields vector $\mathbf{A}$. Equivalently, $\mathbf{A}-\mathbf{B}$ can be defined as the sum $\mathbf{A}+(-\mathbf{B})$.

If $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{B}$, then $\mathbf{A}-\mathbf{B}$ is defined as the *null* or *zero vector* and is represented by the symbol $\mathbf{0}$ or simply $0$. It has zero magnitude and no specific direction. A vector which is not null is a *proper vector*. All vectors will be assumed proper unless otherwise stated.

5. The product of a vector $\mathbf{A}$ by a scalar $m$ is a vector $m\mathbf{A}$ with magnitude $|m|$ times the magnitude of $\mathbf{A}$ and with direction the same as or opposite to that of $\mathbf{A}$, according as $m$ is positive or negative. If $m = 0$, $m\mathbf{A}$ is the null vector.

### Laws of Vector Algebra

<mark>LAWS OF VECTOR ALGEBRA.</mark> If $\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$ and $\mathbf{C}$ are vectors and $m$ and $n$ are scalars, then:

$$
\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B} = \mathbf{B} + \mathbf{A} \quad \text{Commutative Law for Addition}
$$

$$
\mathbf{A} + (\mathbf{B} + \mathbf{C}) = (\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}) + \mathbf{C} \quad \text{Associative Law for Addition}
$$

$$
m\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}m \quad \text{Commutative Law for Multiplication}
$$

$$
m(n\mathbf{A}) = (mn)\mathbf{A} \quad \text{Associative Law for Multiplication}
$$

$$
(m + n)\mathbf{A} = m\mathbf{A} + n\mathbf{A} \quad \text{Distributive Law}
$$

$$
m(\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}) = m\mathbf{A} + m\mathbf{B} \quad \text{Distributive Law}
$$

Note that in these laws only multiplication of a vector by one or more scalars is used. In Chapter 2, products of vectors are defined.

These laws enable us to treat vector equations in the same way as ordinary algebraic equations. For example, if $\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B} = \mathbf{C}$ then by transposing $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{C} - \mathbf{B}$.

### Unit Vectors

<mark>A UNIT VECTOR</mark> is a vector having unit magnitude. If $\mathbf{A}$ is a vector <u>with magnitude $A \neq 0$</u>, then $\mathbf{A}/A$ is a unit vector having the <u>same direction as $\mathbf{A}$</u>.

Any vector $\mathbf{A}$ can be represented by a unit vector $\mathbf{a}$ in the direction of $\mathbf{A}$ multiplied by the magnitude of $\mathbf{A}$. In symbols, $\mathbf{A} = A\mathbf{a}$.

#### The Rectangular Unit Vectors $\mathbf{i}$, $\mathbf{j}$, $\mathbf{k}$

An important set of unit vectors are those having the directions of the positive $x$, $y$, and $z$ axes of a three dimensional rectangular coordinate system, and are denoted respectively by <u>$\mathbf{i}$, $\mathbf{j}$, and $\mathbf{k}$ (Fig. 5)</u>.

We shall use right-handed rectangular coordinate systems unless otherwise stated. Such a system derives its name from the fact that a right threaded screw rotated through $90^\circ$ from $Ox$ to $Oy$ will advance in the positive $z$ direction, as in Fig. 5 above.

In general, three vectors $\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$ and $\mathbf{C}$ which have coincident initial points and are not *coplanar*, i.e. do not lie in or are not parallel to the same plane, are said to form a *right-handed system* or *dextral system* if a right threaded screw rotated through an angle less than $180^\circ$ from $\mathbf{A}$ to $\mathbf{B}$ will advance in the direction $\mathbf{C}$ as shown in Fig. 6.

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 2, Fig. 5):** A three-dimensional rectangular coordinate system (Cartesian axes $x$, $y$, $z$) showing unit vectors $\mathbf{i}$, $\mathbf{j}$, and $\mathbf{k}$ along the positive direction of the axes, forming a unit cube.

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 3, Fig. 6):** An illustration of three vectors $\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$, and $\mathbf{C}$ forming a right-handed system. A right-threaded screw rotated from $\mathbf{A}$ to $\mathbf{B}$ advances in the direction of $\mathbf{C}$.
>
> **Handwritten Annotation:** There is a hand-drawn arrow pointing from the rotation spiral to the handwriting: "from A to B".

### Components of a Vector

<mark>COMPONENTS OF A VECTOR.</mark> <u>Any vector $\mathbf{A}$ in 3 dimensions can be represented with initial point at the origin $O$ of a rectangular coordinate system (Fig. 7)</u>. Let $(A_1, A_2, A_3)$ be the rectangular coordinates of the terminal point of vector $\mathbf{A}$ with initial point at $O$. The vectors $A_1\mathbf{i}$, $A_2\mathbf{j}$, and $A_3\mathbf{k}$ are called the *rectangular component vectors* or simply *component vectors* of $\mathbf{A}$ in the $x$, $y$ and $z$ directions respectively. $A_1$, $A_2$ and $A_3$ are called the *rectangular components* or simply *components* of $\mathbf{A}$ in the $x$, $y$ and $z$ directions respectively.

The sum or resultant of $A_1\mathbf{i}$, $A_2\mathbf{j}$ and $A_3\mathbf{k}$ is the vector $\mathbf{A}$ so that we can write:

$$
\mathbf{A} = A_1\mathbf{i} + A_2\mathbf{j} + A_3\mathbf{k}
$$

The magnitude of $\mathbf{A}$ is:

$$
A = |\mathbf{A}| = \sqrt{A_1^2 + A_2^2 + A_3^2}
$$

In particular, the position vector or radius vector $\mathbf{r}$ from $O$ to the point $(x,y,z)$ is written:

$$
\mathbf{r} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}
$$

and has magnitude:

$$
r = |\mathbf{r}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}
$$

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 3, Fig. 7):** A vector $\mathbf{A}$ in a 3D coordinate system, represented as the diagonal of a rectangular box. The projection components along the $x$, $y$, and $z$ axes are shown as $A_1\mathbf{i}$, $A_2\mathbf{j}$, and $A_3\mathbf{k}$.

### Vector and Scalar Fields

<mark>SCALAR FIELD.</mark> If to each point $(x,y,z)$ of a region $R$ in space there corresponds a number or scalar $\phi(x,y,z)$, then $\phi$ is called a <u>scalar function of position</u> or *scalar point function* and we say that a <u>scalar field</u> $\phi$ has been defined in $R$. *[Note: The function $\phi(x,y,z)$ is circled in the book]*

<mark>Examples.</mark> (1) The temperature at any point within or on the earth's surface at a certain time defines a scalar field.
(2) $\phi(x,y,z) = x^3y - z^2$ defines <u>a scalar field</u>.

A scalar field which is independent of time is called a <u>stationary or steady-state scalar field</u>.

<mark>VECTOR FIELD.</mark> If to each point $(x,y,z)$ of a region $R$ in space there corresponds a vector $\mathbf{V}(x,y,z)$, then $\mathbf{V}$ is called a *vector function of position* or *vector point function* and we say that a <u>vector field</u> $\mathbf{V}$ has been defined in $R$.

<mark>Examples.</mark> (1) If the velocity at any point $(x,y,z)$ within a moving fluid is known at a certain time, then a vector field is defined.
(2) $\mathbf{V}(x,y,z) = xy^2\mathbf{i} - 2yz^3\mathbf{j} + x^2z\mathbf{k}$ defines <u>a vector field</u>.

A vector field which is independent of time is called a <u>stationary or steady-state vector field</u>.

### Solved Problem 3

An automobile travels 3 miles due north, then 5 miles northeast. Represent these displacements graphically and determine the resultant displacement:
(a) graphically,
(b) analytically.

**Answer:**

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 4, Fig. 9):**
> A vector diagram showing vector $\mathbf{A}$ starting at origin $O$ and going 3 units straight up (North) to point $P$. Vector $\mathbf{B}$ starts at $P$ and goes 5 units northeast (at $45^\circ$ to the horizontal) to point $Q$. The resultant vector $\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}$ is the vector from $O$ to $Q$. The angle between the direction of North and East is $90^\circ$, so angle $\angle OPQ = 180^\circ - 45^\circ = 135^\circ$. A scale bar shows "Unit = 1 mile".

**(a) Graphical Determination of Resultant:**
Lay off the 1 mile unit on vector $\mathbf{OQ}$ to find the magnitude. Measure angle $EOQ$ using a protractor. The vector $\mathbf{OQ}$ has magnitude $7.4 \text{ mi}$ and direction $61.5^\circ$ north of east.

**(b) Analytical Determination of Resultant:**
From triangle $OPQ$, let the magnitudes of $\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$ be $A$, $B$, $C$. By the law of cosines:

$$
C^2 = A^2 + B^2 - 2AB \cos \angle OPQ
$$

$$
C^2 = 3^2 + 5^2 - 2(3)(5) \cos 135^\circ = 34 - 30(-0.7071) = 34 + 15\sqrt{2} \approx 55.21
$$

$$
C \approx 7.43 \text{ miles}
$$

By the law of sines:

$$
\frac{A}{\sin \angle OQP} = \frac{C}{\sin \angle OPQ}
$$

$$
\sin \angle OQP = \frac{A \sin \angle OPQ}{C} = \frac{3(0.7071)}{7.43} \approx 0.2855
$$

$$
\angle OQP \approx 16^\circ 35'
$$

So the vector $\mathbf{OQ}$ has magnitude $7.43 \text{ mi}$ and direction $45^\circ + 16^\circ 35' = 61^\circ 35'$ north of east.

### Solved Problem 6

Show that the addition of vectors is associative, i.e. $\mathbf{A} + (\mathbf{B} + \mathbf{C}) = (\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}) + \mathbf{C}$.

**Answer:**

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 5, Fig. 11):**
> A three-dimensional quadrilateral in space with vertices $O$, $P$, $Q$, $R$.
> - Vector $\mathbf{A}$ goes from $O$ to $P$.
> - Vector $\mathbf{B}$ goes from $P$ to $Q$.
> - Vector $\mathbf{C}$ goes from $Q$ to $R$.
> - Diagonal vector from $O$ to $Q$ represents $\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}$.
> - Diagonal vector from $P$ to $R$ represents $\mathbf{B} + \mathbf{C}$.
> - The final resultant from $O$ to $R$ is $\mathbf{D}$.

From the figure:

$$
\mathbf{OP} + \mathbf{PQ} = \mathbf{OQ} = \mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}
$$

$$
\mathbf{PQ} + \mathbf{QR} = \mathbf{PR} = \mathbf{B} + \mathbf{C}
$$

Now:

$$
\mathbf{OP} + \mathbf{PR} = \mathbf{OR} = \mathbf{D} \implies \mathbf{A} + (\mathbf{B} + \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{D}
$$

$$
\mathbf{OQ} + \mathbf{QR} = \mathbf{OR} = \mathbf{D} \implies (\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}) + \mathbf{C} = \mathbf{D}
$$

So $\mathbf{A} + (\mathbf{B} + \mathbf{C}) = (\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}) + \mathbf{C}$.

Forces $\mathbf{F}_1, \mathbf{F}_2, \dots, \mathbf{F}_6$ act as shown on object $P$. What force is needed to prevent $P$ from moving?

### Solved Problem 9

An airplane moves in a northwesterly direction at $125 \text{ mi/hr}$ relative to the ground, due to the fact there is a westerly wind of $50 \text{ mi/hr}$ relative to the ground. How fast and in what direction would the plane have traveled if there were no wind?

**Answer:**

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 7, Fig. 14):**
> A vector triangle representing velocities:
> - $\mathbf{V}_a$ is the velocity of the wind (50 mi/hr East).
> - $\mathbf{V}_b$ is the velocity of the plane relative to the ground (125 mi/hr Northwest).
> - The vector subtraction $\mathbf{V}_b - \mathbf{V}_a = \mathbf{V}_b + (-\mathbf{V}_a)$ gives the velocity of the plane without wind.

Let:
- $\mathbf{V}_a = \text{wind velocity}$
- $\mathbf{V}_b = \text{velocity of plane with wind}$
- $\mathbf{V}_c = \text{velocity of plane without wind}$

Then:

$$
\mathbf{V}_a = \mathbf{V}_c + \mathbf{V}_a \implies \mathbf{V}_c = \mathbf{V}_b - \mathbf{V}_a = \mathbf{V}_b + (-\mathbf{V}_a)
$$

From graphical measurement, $\mathbf{V}_c$ has a magnitude of $163 \text{ mi/hr}$ and direction $33^\circ$ north of west.

### Solved Problem 11

Given three non-coplanar vectors $\mathbf{a}$, $\mathbf{b}$, and $\mathbf{c}$, find an expression for any vector $\mathbf{r}$ in three dimensional space.

**Answer:**

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 7, Fig. 16):**
> Vectors $\mathbf{a}$, $\mathbf{b}$, and $\mathbf{c}$ have a common initial point $O$. A vector $\mathbf{r}$ starts at $O$ and projects into space. A rectangular box $PQRSTUV$ is constructed with edges parallel to $\mathbf{a}$, $\mathbf{b}$, and $\mathbf{c}$. The diagonal of this box represents the vector $\mathbf{r} = x\mathbf{a} + y\mathbf{b} + z\mathbf{c}$.

From the construction:

$$
\mathbf{OV} = x\mathbf{a}, \quad \mathbf{OP} = y\mathbf{b}, \quad \mathbf{OT} = z\mathbf{c}
$$

By vector addition:

$$
\mathbf{OR} = \mathbf{OV} + \mathbf{VQ} + \mathbf{QR} = \mathbf{OV} + \mathbf{OP} + \mathbf{OT}
$$

So:

$$
\mathbf{r} = x\mathbf{a} + y\mathbf{b} + z\mathbf{c}
$$

### Solved Problem 12

Prove that if $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are non-collinear then $x\mathbf{a} + y\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}$ implies $x = y = 0$.

**Answer:**
Suppose $x \neq 0$. Then $x\mathbf{a} + y\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}$ implies:

$$
\mathbf{a} = -\left( \frac{y}{x} \right)\mathbf{b}
$$

This means $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ must be parallel to the same line (collinear). This contradicts the hypothesis that they are non-collinear. So $x = 0$.
If $x = 0$, then $y\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}$, which implies $y = 0$ since $\mathbf{b} \neq \mathbf{0}$.

### Solved Problem 13

If $x_1\mathbf{a} + y_1\mathbf{b} = x_2\mathbf{a} + y_2\mathbf{b}$, where $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are non-collinear, then $x_1 = x_2$ and $y_1 = y_2$.

**Answer:**
The equation can be rewritten as:

$$
(x_1 - x_2)\mathbf{a} + (y_1 - y_2)\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}
$$

Since $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are non-collinear, by Solved Problem 12:

$$
x_1 - x_2 = 0 \implies x_1 = x_2
$$

$$
y_1 - y_2 = 0 \implies y_1 = y_2
$$

### Solved Problem 14

Prove that if $\mathbf{a}$, $\mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{c}$ are non-coplanar then $x\mathbf{a} + y\mathbf{b} + z\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{0}$ implies $x = y = z = 0$.

**Answer:**
Suppose $x \neq 0$. Then $x\mathbf{a} + y\mathbf{b} + z\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{0}$ implies:

$$
\mathbf{a} = -\left( \frac{y}{x} \right)\mathbf{b} - \left( \frac{z}{x} \right)\mathbf{c}
$$

This means $\mathbf{a}$ is a vector lying in the plane of $\mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{c}$, which contradicts the hypothesis that $\mathbf{a}$, $\mathbf{b}$, and $\mathbf{c}$ are non-coplanar. So $x = 0$.
By similar reasoning, $y = 0$ and $z = 0$.

### Solved Problem 15

If $x_1\mathbf{a} + y_1\mathbf{b} + z_1\mathbf{c} = x_2\mathbf{a} + y_2\mathbf{b} + z_2\mathbf{c}$, where $\mathbf{a}$, $\mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{c}$ are non-coplanar, then $x_1 = x_2, y_1 = y_2, z_1 = z_2$.

**Answer:**
The equation can be rewritten as:

$$
(x_1 - x_2)\mathbf{a} + (y_1 - y_2)\mathbf{b} + (z_1 - z_2)\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{0}
$$

Since $\mathbf{a}$, $\mathbf{b}$, and $\mathbf{c}$ are non-coplanar, by Solved Problem 14:

$$
x_1 - x_2 = 0, \quad y_1 - y_2 = 0, \quad z_1 - z_2 = 0
$$

which gives $x_1 = x_2, y_1 = y_2, z_1 = z_2$.

### Solved Problem 16

Prove that the diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other.

**Answer:**

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 8, Fig. 17):**
> A parallelogram $ABCD$ with diagonals $AC$ and $BD$ intersecting at point $P$.
> - Vector $\mathbf{a}$ represents side $AB$.
> - Vector $\mathbf{b}$ represents side $AD$.

Let $P$ be the intersection point of the diagonals.
Since $\mathbf{BD} + \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{b}$, we have $\mathbf{BD} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}$. Then $\mathbf{BP} = x(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a})$.
Since $\mathbf{AC} = \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}$, we have $\mathbf{AP} = y(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b})$.
But $\mathbf{AB} = \mathbf{AP} + \mathbf{PB} = \mathbf{AP} - \mathbf{BP}$, so:

$$
\mathbf{a} = y(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}) - x(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}) = (x + y)\mathbf{a} + (y - x)\mathbf{b}
$$

Since $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are non-collinear, we must have:

$$
x + y = 1 \quad \text{and} \quad y - x = 0
$$

### Solved Problem 17

If the midpoints of the consecutive sides of any quadrilateral are connected by straight lines, prove that the resulting quadrilateral is a parallelogram.

**Answer:**

> [!NOTE]
> **Figure Description (Page 8, Fig. 18):**
> A general quadrilateral $ABCD$ with midpoints $P$, $Q$, $R$, $S$ of sides $AB$, $BC$, $CD$, $DA$ respectively. The midpoints are connected to form the inner quadrilateral $PQRS$.

Let the vertices be $ABCD$. The midpoints are $P, Q, R, S$.
Then:

$$
\mathbf{PQ} = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}), \quad \mathbf{QR} = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{c}), \quad \mathbf{RS} = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{c} + \mathbf{d}), \quad \mathbf{SP} = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{d} + \mathbf{a})

Thus opposite sides are equal and parallel. So $PQRS$ is a parallelogram.

### Supplementary Problem 54

Let the position vectors of points $P$ and $Q$ relative to an origin $O$ be given by $\mathbf{p}$ and $\mathbf{q}$ respectively. If $R$ is a point which divides line segment $PQ$ into segments which are in the ratio $m:n$, show that the position vector of $R$ is given by:

$$
\mathbf{r} = \frac{m\mathbf{p} + n\mathbf{q}}{m+n}
$$

and that this is independent of the origin.

### Supplementary Problem 55

If $\mathbf{r}_1, \mathbf{r}_2, \dots, \mathbf{r}_n$ are the position vectors of masses $m_1, m_2, \dots, m_n$ respectively relative to an origin $O$, show that the position vector of the centroid is given by:

$$
\mathbf{r} = \frac{m_1\mathbf{r}_1 + m_2\mathbf{r}_2 + \dots + m_n\mathbf{r}_n}{m_1 + m_2 + \dots + m_n}
$$

and that this is independent of the origin.

### Supplementary Problem 56

A quadrilateral $ABCD$ has masses of 1, 2, 3 and 4 units located respectively at its vertices $A(-1,-2,2)$, $B(3,2,-1)$, $C(1,-2,4)$, and $D(3,1,2)$. Find the coordinates of the centroid.

**Answer:**
$(2, 0, 2)$

### Supplementary Problem 57

Show that the equation of a plane which passes through three given points $A, B, C$ not in the same straight line, and having position vectors $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}$ relative to an origin $O$, can be written:

$$
\mathbf{r} = \frac{m\mathbf{a} + n\mathbf{b} + p\mathbf{c}}{m+n+p}
$$

where $m, n, p$ are scalars. Verify that the equation is independent of the origin.

### Supplementary Problem 58

The position vectors of points $P$ and $Q$ are given by $\mathbf{r}_1 = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}$, $\mathbf{r}_2 = 4\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j} + 2\mathbf{k}$. Determine $\mathbf{PQ}$ in terms of $\mathbf{i}$, $\mathbf{j}$, $\mathbf{k}$ and find its magnitude.

**Answer:**
$2\mathbf{i} - 6\mathbf{j} + 3\mathbf{k}$, magnitude 7.

### Supplementary Problem 59

If $\mathbf{A} = 3\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j} - 4\mathbf{k}$, $\mathbf{B} = -2\mathbf{i} + 4\mathbf{j} - 3\mathbf{k}$, $\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}$, find:
(a) $2\mathbf{A} - \mathbf{B} + 3\mathbf{C}$,
(b) $|\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B} + \mathbf{C}|$,
(c) $|3\mathbf{A} - 2\mathbf{B} + 4\mathbf{C}|$,
(d) a unit vector parallel to $3\mathbf{A} - 2\mathbf{B} + 4\mathbf{C}$.

**Answer:**
(a) $11\mathbf{i} - 8\mathbf{k}$
(b) $\sqrt{93}$
(c) $\sqrt{398}$
(d) $\frac{3\mathbf{A} - 2\mathbf{B} + 4\mathbf{C}}{\sqrt{398}}$

### Supplementary Problem 60

The following forces act on a particle $P$: $\mathbf{F}_1 = 2\mathbf{i} + 3\mathbf{j} - 5\mathbf{k}$, $\mathbf{F}_2 = -5\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + 3\mathbf{k}$, $\mathbf{F}_3 = \mathbf{i} - 2\mathbf{j} + 4\mathbf{k}$, $\mathbf{F}_4 = 4\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j} - 2\mathbf{k}$, measured in pounds. Find:
(a) the resultant of the forces,
(b) the magnitude of the resultant.

**Answer:**
(a) $2\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j}$

### Supplementary Problem 63

Show that a necessary and sufficient condition that the vectors:

$$
\mathbf{A} = A_1\mathbf{i} + A_2\mathbf{j} + A_3\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\mathbf{B} = B_1\mathbf{i} + B_2\mathbf{j} + B_3\mathbf{k}
$$

$$
\mathbf{C} = C_1\mathbf{i} + C_2\mathbf{j} + C_3\mathbf{k}
$$

be linearly independent is that the determinant:

$$
\begin{vmatrix}
A_1 & A_2 & A_3 \\
B_1 & B_2 & B_3 \\
C_1 & C_2 & C_3
\end{vmatrix}
$$

be different from zero.
